# Appendix E — A First Entry

*[The last item is mine. I have set it in her form, because it is
the only form I have left, and because the reader was promised
evidence. It is not, the reader will see at once, an entry of the
indexed kind. It is the fourth of the excluded — an account I am
declining to close, in the only direction no one in this book has
tried. — E.M.]*

## The Fourth Excluded Entry: Marsh

**Cambridge; Bergen; the world's reading rooms. Established 1975.
Entered into the ledgers of thirty-one institutions between 2021
and this year. Account: open — and, as of the eleventh of October,
contested.**

On the eleventh of October I went back to Bergen, and I record
what I did there in the flat prose the log-keepers of this book
have taught me, because it is the only prose that can carry it
without asking for sympathy, and sympathy is a toll and I am done
paying tolls.

The manager met me at the warehouse door. I think he knew — they
always know; the knowing is the countersignature — because he
brought the coffee without being asked and then found urgent
business at the far end of the building. I went to the shelf. The
slot of folder VII stood as I left it in March: eleven
centimetres of clean interval in thirty years of dust, the card
upright at the back. *VII. Out.* My name on the reverse, in the
ink that is older than my reading. The first offer, patient as a
timetable, waiting to be answered or to become an errand.

I took the black stone out of my left coat pocket, where it has
ridden for seven months, twenty-two grams, continuously
declared. I read the label on its bag a last time — *For the
successor. Return if declining* — and I stood there for a length
of time I did not measure, on purpose, because measurement is
how they enter you, and then I set the bag in the slot, against
the card, squarely, the way you square a debt, and I said — out
loud, to the shelving, in an empty herring warehouse, a woman of
fifty with a doctorate and a dead teacher and no God — I said:
"No."

Nothing happened. I want that on the record, in this book where
something always happens: no fog, no bell, no smell of iron. The
slot held its two objects, card and stone, offer and answer, and
the dust went on being dust. Declining, it turns out, is not an
event. It is the withdrawal of oneself from the class of things
that events happen to, and it feels — the reader has waited two
hundred pages behind a man in a map room to hear it said by
someone who chose it sane — it feels like the tide going out of
the body. He was exact. It has not come back in. I am told, by
his example, that it never quite does, and that a person can
spend seventy-three years at the tideline, buying doorknobs.
That is the invoice for this ending and I print it in full. I
do not expect to escape the missing; I expect to husband it.

Why, then — since the whole book has bent toward the water like
a lead on its line? The reader has earned the true answer, and
the true answer is in the papers, where everything true in my
life now is. It is in the file of Thompson Island.

There may have been, once, in three hundred years of ledgers,
one clean exit: a thing that was real, and was seen by an honest
man, and then left the world without an audit — unweighed,
unbanked, unkept — its account not closed but annulled. Voss,
who opened every door she was shown, could not hold the thought
of it for a whole night. I have held it every night since
February, and I have understood something about my teacher, at
the end, that her genius never let her see: she looked for the
outside of the system for fifty years, in archives and on
freighters and finally from the deck of the Alma K. — and every
place she looked was a place the system kept. Of course it was.
She was reading the *house's* books; the house's books contain
no exits; that is what a house is. But there is one ledger in
this story that no clerk maintains, one set of entries kept in
no iron and stored at no depth — and the reader is holding it.
A published book cannot be recalled. It cannot be dusted, or
lent forward, or kept; it is copied, it scatters, it survives
in wrong editions and coffee-stained and misremembered — it is
the one deposit that cannot be withdrawn, because it is made
into people, and people, Thompson Island whispers, might —
might — be the black young country that nothing weighs.

So I have made my deposit, and it is not in the fog on any
meridian. It is here — everything she found, everything they
keep, the shelf, the band, the weighings, the doors — printed,
public, unrecallable, in your hands and ten thousand hands like
yours. That is my bet: that a truth sufficiently scattered can
no more be called in than an island blown to sea-smoke can be
sounded. It is not a good bet. I want that on the record too. I
have laid the only thing I still own against the oldest house
in the world, on the strength of one sealer's log and a folder
that would not lie flat — and there are nights, this is one,
when I can hear the other reading of my own evidence, plain as
print. *Bring it; you know the meridian.* I have taken that
sentence, all year, for a voyage I refused. But a meridian is a
line drawn from clean air by agreement, real because enough
people consent to steer by it, and there was never a better
description of a readership. On the other reading, nothing was
refused in that warehouse and nothing has scattered here: a
book is brought to its meridian by publication, and this
printing is not my escape but my errand — the placement, the
delivery they asked of me from the beginning, performed the
more perfectly for being performed in defiance. I cannot tell
the two readings apart. I have tried for a year. Perhaps that
is what it is to be a good instrument: to read true on either
account. I print both, and I will not tip the scale. It was
never mine to tip.

The practicalities, for the estate I will someday leave. The
papers stay public. The letters unseal in 2031; I will be
fifty-six; I have marked the date in my diary in ordinary ink.
The light in Bergen I paid up for a year and then, on the
morning I left, I asked the manager to turn it off. He looked
at me for a long time — the fathom, the kindness — and he said,
"It's your shelf now, one way or the other," and then he ruined
it, thank God, by complaining at length about the new invoicing
software and asking me to mind the loose step on the way down,
because he is not an oracle; none of them were; he is a man who
manages a warehouse, and the fathom in his kindness may only
ever have been the ordinary deep patience of people who work
alone among other people's kept things. I have made them all
strange, I think, by the company I bring. Put that in the
record too. The light is off. The room can see the shelf. That
is the other arrangement, and nobody ever said it was
forbidden. Only that it was dark.

My scale ticket this morning printed one line. I expect one
line tomorrow. I have stopped checking the day after; a woman
must keep something for herself.

The showing at Aran is next autumn, they say, if the fog
serves. I have rooms above the harbour, booked in my own name,
signed clearly. I am going to stand on the cliff with my eyes
open, visible, unclaimed, holding no stone and owing no
difference, and I am going to watch the curtain move — as a
reader, the last free trade there is — and then I am going to
turn around, and take the boat home, and keep my own light on,
at my own desk, at the address where all my differences are
banked. You know it. You are inside it. It has visiting hours.

They are called reading.

Sign clearly on your way out.

— E.M.
