# Appendix E — A First Entry

*[The last item is mine. I have set it in her form, because it is
the only form I have left, and because the reader was promised
evidence and a scholar delivers in the format of the field.
— E.M.]*

---

## 1. Marsh

**Cambridge, and elsewhere. Established 1975. Charted by the
present edition. A kept place, lately entered into service.
Visiting conditions: see below.**

Every entry in this Atlas concerns a place that was on the charts
and not in the world, or in the world and not on the charts. This
first entry of mine concerns the third and largest class, which
Voss taught without ever quite listing: the places that are
entered into the ledger while still believing themselves to be
persons.

The evidence, in the order a stranger would want it.

Item. In 1979, in an ink that had gone out of use, a hand that has
signed fifty years of letters wrote a name in full on the reverse
of a lending card, and the name was mine, and I was four years
old, and the card was filed against a folder that was already out.
A berth, in other words, was reserved — not for a person; persons
cannot be reserved; for an *address* that a person would grow
into, the way a firm reserves premises on a street where the
houses are not yet built.

Item. In 2019 a solicitor telephoned the students of Iris Voss in
an order I have never asked about, and I asked about the papers
before I asked about the money, and the telephoning stopped. The
reader knows by now what an examination is. I passed, or failed;
the distinction has retired.

Item. Between 2021 and this year I verified the archive of a
woman who could not be verified, and every archive I entered
entered me: reader's tickets, registers, visitors' books — my
name, my dates, my own upright and increasingly old-fashioned
hand, deposited in thirty-one institutions across eleven
countries. I set out to trace Voss through the world's ledgers.
I have concluded the errand with my own route lying in them like
a survey line. The biographer's secret, which the trade does not
print: you cannot follow a track without laying one.

Item. A shop in Tórshavn has placed my letter. A scale in my
bathroom prints one line, and I check. A stone that is not mine
is in my pocket, on loan from a shelf I have stood on. A man in
Basel is waiting for it with a velvet case and a card already
ruled. And a sentence in brown ink is waiting too, patient as a
timetable: *when the book is finished, bring it.*

The book is finished.

I am aware — the reader will be ahead of me; the reader has been
ahead of me since the preface, which is what readers are *for* —
that the expected ending stands ready, like the boatman on the
shingle: Elena Marsh follows her professor down to the water and
the papers pass to a third keeper and the book closes on the
fog. I decline that ending, and I want to be exact about why,
because the why is my first finding as a cataloguer of these
places, and I believe it is new; I believe it is the one thing
in this book that is mine.

Voss was a cartographer. When her summons came, it came as a
place — a bearing, a depth, a boat. The instrument is fetched in
the shape of its work; the letters said so all along; a lead is
recalled to the water, a chart to the chart-house. But I am not
a cartographer. I am an editor. My work, my whole life's work,
is the making of one thing: *readers*. And so I have looked at
the sentence in brown ink for a year — *when the book is
finished, bring it; you know the meridian* — and I have
understood, finally, on my own authority, in my own trade's
grammar, what it instructs, and it does not instruct a voyage.

A book is brought to its meridian by publication.

You are the meridian. The line agreed by convention, drawn out
of clean air, real because we all consent to steer by it: there
was never a better description of a readership. I was not asked
to sail anywhere. I was asked to *deliver* — and delivery, my
own entry in this Atlas's long ledger of doctrines, is what a
book does when it is opened. The deposit is made. You made it,
at the preface, when you agreed to hold the difference column
of two women's lives in your hands; it has been accruing in you,
page by page, entry by entry — every impossible folio you
credited for the length of a chapter, every depth you kept in
the margin of your attention, every time you looked up from
this book and the room seemed, for a half-second, *filed*. That
half-second is the entry. You have been weighed while reading.
The toll, the reader remembers, was always the weighing.

So this is the first entry of the next atlas, and its subject is
its reader, and its finding is as follows. The post is offered
twice. The first offer is made early, and gently, and so that it
can be declined, because there is no other way to learn what
declining feels like — and it does not come as a boatman for
everyone, or a card, or a stone. For most of us it comes as a
book: some volume met too young or too late at night, which
showed you the gap under the door and let you choose, in the
privacy of your own reading, whether to be the kind of person
who pretends they did not see it. You hold such a book. You are
at its last page. The showing, the folklore says, is brief, and
the folklore, as usual, has it inverted: you are briefly visible.

I do not know what I will do when the second offer comes, in its
own year, dressed as an errand. I know what she did. I know what
the man in Basel did, and what it cost each of them, and I have
printed both invoices in full and will not tip the scale. The
stone stays in my pocket for now — lent, not kept, like
everything else in this economy: the papers, the post, the
light in Bergen, which is on, and which I pay for monthly, and
which is not for finding things.

It is so the shelf can see the room. Good night, then, from my
own kept place. The account is current. The light is on.

Sign clearly on your way out.

— E.M.
