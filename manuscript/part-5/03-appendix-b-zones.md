# Appendix B — From Notebook IV: On the Zones

*[Promised at the Mountains of Kong. Written, by the ink and the
hand, in the late 1970s. I have cut nothing. — E.M.]*

IV: The reader of my Kong file will have noticed that I pass quickly
over what the century's atlases did with their wall once they had
it, and I pass quickly because the subject will not stay in a
footnote, and here it is, at length, where it can be watched.

The nineteenth century drew zones. Torrid, temperate, frigid — bands
ruled across the sheet with the confidence of lines of latitude,
which they pretended to be, and were not. A line of latitude is an
observation. A zone is a *verdict*. Into the bands the century
sorted climates, and then crops, and then constitutions, and then —
the sequence takes one generation, and can be watched happening in
successive editions of the same school atlas, which I own and will
leave to whoever has the stomach — *persons*: the temperate peoples
industrious, the torrid indolent, the frigid stunted, each race
shelved at its latitude like stock in a warehouse, and the whole
arrangement presented as geography, which is to say as *fact found*,
when every line of it was fact *installed*.

I have spent this Atlas showing that a wall on a map does work in
the world. Kong kept ships from a coast for ninety years. The zones
kept whole peoples filed under verdicts for a century and more, and
here is the difference, and it is why this page is in the appendix
and not the entry: Kong was at least a lie about *rock*. The rock
could be walked to, and walked through, and one honest lieutenant
with a notebook ended it. The zones were a lie about the people
inside them, and the people inside them were the one class of
witness the profession had agreed in advance could not testify —
their knowledge filed as folklore, their charts as curiosities,
their denials as the very indolence the zone predicted. An error
that silences its own counter-evidence is an enclosure. An error
that *classifies the witnesses* is a prison, and the profession of
my lifetime has spent great labour scrubbing the zones from its
sheets and no labour at all asking which of its current instruments
are the same instrument, renamed.

The reader who has followed my doctrine will want the ledger
entry, so: the zones are the largest maintained fiction in this
book, and no dark water was required, and no fog. The maintenance
was performed in daylight, at desks, by men with pensions, in ink
that has not faded — and it was performed, this is the entry I
came to make, *at a profit*, which distinguishes it from every
phantom in my index. Brasil never paid its keepers. The zones
paid. When I say that emptiness at scale is expensive and
something pays for it, the reader has always been free to hear a
ghost story. Let them hear instead an invoice. The blank spaces
of the old maps were kept blank by someone; the full spaces were
kept sorted by someone; and of the two operations, the one done
with fog and singing has, on my complete accounting, the smaller
body count. I know where the third place keeps its differences.
I have never once been shown where we keep ours, and I have
looked, and the looking is this notebook, and the answer, so far
as I can carry it, is: in the margins of other people's maps, at
other people's expense, marked *nondum cognita* — not yet known —
when the honest label, then and now, was *nondum admissa*. Not
yet admitted.

The wall across Africa was demolished in 1889. Its work was
transferred. Errors are never destroyed, reader. You have my
whole life on it.
