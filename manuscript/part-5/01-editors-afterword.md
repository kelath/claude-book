# Editor's Afterword

There is a convention for afterwords of this kind. The editor gathers
the threads, pronounces on what may be safely believed, thanks the
long-suffering, and closes the tomb with a firm classical line. I
have read a hundred of them. I am not able to write one, and the
reader who has come this far knows why: the threads are not mine to
gather. They are lent.

But there are accounts I can settle, and an editor pays her debts in
order.

**The fabrication thesis.** I gave it three days once, and the reader
deserves my final finding. It fails. It fails on the register line
of March 2019, on an offer lodged before a catalogue existed, on
bathymetry that postdates the ink that cites it, on a thermal ticket
carrying intaglio print, and on the plain arithmetic of one
woman's lifetime against the tonnage of what would need to have been
forged. But I want to record the deeper reason I no longer reach for
it, because it is the closest thing this book has taught me to a
method: the thesis explains every document and no behaviour. Forgers
work toward an audience. Voss spent fifty years filing evidence she
never once used — never published, never brandished, never even
footnoted — against the day a reader she had chosen but never
summoned might verify it unprompted. That is not forgery's shape.
That is the shape of a woman keeping a ledger she expected to be
audited on, by an examiner she trusted to be thorough, on a date she
did not set. I have been the examiner. The books balance. I hate it,
some nights, how well the books balance.

**Halter & Cie.** In April I went to Basel, because the fifth offer
came with a train ticket and I have learned from better people than
me what a timetable sentence is. I was received not at the firm but
at a house above the city, by the client, whose name I know and will
not print — not from fear; from the same instinct that stopped
Voss's pen at the uncollected letter, and I understand that instinct
now. He is very old. He is, I think, the oldest person I have ever
sat with, and the room he received me in was a map room, the finest
private one I have seen or heard described: the Zeno chart in its
1558 state; a Salterio northern sheet — the second of the three; I
did not ask after the third — and, in a long case down the centre of
the room, on velvet, like a reliquary's contents, forty-one small
stones.

Beach pebbles. Granite, flint, sandstone, basalt. Each with its card,
in the upright hand: a name, a place, a year. The earliest card I
could read was dated 1809.

"Returned," he said. "All returned. It is the only collection of its
kind, because it is the only thing in the world that can be
collected. Everything else they lend." And then, because he had
watched my face perform its arithmetic — forty-one stones, and the
cards, and the years, and the one uncollected letter, and the black
weight in my left pocket which I swear I had mentioned to no one and
which felt, in that room, like a struck bell — he said, without
appetite, with the terrible mildness of the very old: "I declined in
1953, Dr. Marsh. It was the correct decision. It has been the
correct decision every morning for seventy-three years, and I have
spent the fortune of a dull family proving that the door does not
take money, and buying instead the doorknobs of everyone who was
wiser or weaker than I was. When my firm wrote that the client
wished you kept, they wrote what I dictated. I collect keepers,
Doctor. Kept ones. I had hoped to have your stone in this case
while I was alive to label it."

I asked him — it was the only question worth the train — what
declining had felt like.

He was quiet for a long time. The case between us held its
forty-one names. And he said: "Like the tide going out of the body.
You will know it or you won't," and rang for the car, and at the
door, exactly as if he were remarking on my coat, he said the thing
I have decided to end this Afterword with, because the reader
should hear the case against the book from its best witness. He
said: "She wrote it all down, your professor. That was her theft,
you know. Not from me. From you. A candidate is supposed to come
to the water knowing nothing. You will arrive at yours having read
the pilot-book, and whatever you choose, you will never know
whether the choosing was yours or the syllabus's. She took that
from you and called it an education. I would sue, in your place.
There is no place. There is no suing. Good afternoon."

**The estate.** The heir who dealt with Basel has withdrawn the
petition; the valuation lapsed with the offers, which stopped — all
correspondence from Halter & Cie stopped — the day the typescript
of this book reached my publisher, which the reader may interpret
as coincidence, surrender, or a bid concluded by other means. The
papers are lodged, entire, with a research library whose name the
colophon carries, under a covenant of open access. The light in
Bergen I have arranged with the manager, who did not need it
explained, to be left on.

**The book.** It is done, and I will say the plain thing once. I
undertook a scholarly edition and I have delivered — I possess just
enough of my training to see it — a placement: seventy thousand
words carried by hand, mine, to a shelf, yours, in accordance with
a doctrine I could not falsify and can no longer disbelieve, viz.,
that publication is not announcement but delivery, and that a
reader is a port. If the doctrine is false, you hold a curiosity:
the papers of a brilliant obsessive, edited by a tired one, and no
harm done that was not done first to me. If the doctrine is true,
then the book is doing now, in your hands, at this line, the only
thing the tools of its trade have ever done: taking a sounding.
Carry the bottom up honestly to the thumb. It is all any
instrument was ever asked.

The appendices follow. They are five. The catalogue of the
Bergen folders; the passage from notebook IV that I promised at
the Mountains of Kong and defy the reader to finish comfortably;
a short gazetteer of phantoms for which Voss's index made no room;
the eleven letters of the T. correspondence that passed through
the public post, which I print on the principle that what the
mails have carried the reader may carry; and a last item, which
is not Voss's, and not properly an appendix, and which the reader
of the preface was promised on the first pages of this book, in a sentence I set down when this
edition still felt to me like scholarship, and have not had the
courage to reread until tonight: that one thing
had been added, and it was mine, and judgement should be withheld
until the evidence was seen.

The evidence has been seen. The last of the appendices is mine.

— E.M., Cambridge, September 2026
