# Appendix C — A Short Gazetteer of Phantoms Not in the Index

*[Voss's card file runs to some three hundred places her index
declined. I print a selection, with her card-notes, where they
exist, marked IV. The reader who wishes this appendix longer is
directed to the card file itself, and to a working lifetime.
— E.M.]*

**ANTILLIA, or the Isle of the Seven Cities.** Mid-Atlantic,
1424–c. 1587. The great rectangular island of the Portuguese
charts, refuge of seven bishops fled from the Moors, each with his
city. Expeditions were licensed to find it; sailors reported its
sand in their sounding-tallow was one part gold. Its name survives,
diluted, in "Antilles." *IV: A phantom so administratively complete
it had a diocesan structure. Note that we did not name the real
islands after the bishops. We named them after the error.*

**THULE.** North of Britain, six days' sail; reported by Pytheas of
Massalia, fourth century B.C. The oldest phantom in the file, or
the oldest truth — Iceland, Norway, Shetland, and the sea-ice have
all been convicted of being it. *IV: The first entry in the ledger
and already illegible. This is not a failure of the record. It is
the record's signature.*

**MAYDA.** Atlantic, crescent-shaped, c. 1367–1906. The longest-
lived phantom after Brasil; drifted south-west across five
centuries of charts as the surveys advanced. Last printed by Rand
McNally, 1906. *IV: A crescent is a disc with its difference
struck. The shape was the confession all along.*

**THE ISLE OF DEMONS.** Off Newfoundland, 16th century. Notable
as the only phantom used as a prison: in 1542 Roberval marooned
his own niece, Marguerite de la Rocque, upon it for the crime of
a love affair, with her lover, her maid, and four arquebuses.
She survived; they did not; she was rescued years later and told
her story to the court of Navarre. The island then ceased to be
charted, having served. *IV: They could not have marooned her on
a real island; a real island can be found again, and pardons
follow. Sentence was passed to the third place. She is the one
recorded person to be exiled to a phantom and return, and no one
ever asked her the only question. What did she hear at matins?*

**PEPYS ISLAND.** South Atlantic, 47°S, 1683–1839. Invented by
the buccaneer-editor of Cowley's voyage to flatter the Secretary
of the Admiralty. Searched for by Byron, by the French, by the
Spanish; anchorages for it were specified in sailing directions
for a century. *IV: Flattery, like guano, is a sufficient
foundation. Compare Sarah Ann; compare Crocker; compare, if the
reader has the stomach, the dedications of most published maps.*

**SAXEMBERG.** South Atlantic, 1670–c. 1830. Sighted by the Dutch,
confirmed twice with detail — a low island with a peak like a
nipple at centre — then never again, despite lying on a busy
track. *IV: Two confirmations, both by ships named for saints.
I have run the statistics on saintly names and sightings and
will not publish them, because the correlation is real and I
cannot afford another Vienna.*

**DAVIS LAND.** Pacific, 27°S, 1687. A long sandy coast reported
by the buccaneer Edward Davis, five hundred leagues from Chile;
hunted by three nations; the hunt's consolation prize, in 1722,
was Easter Island. *IV: The promise misdelivered but honoured —
the standard settlement of the eighteenth century. The reader of
my Part III may file Davis Land as the control for Juan de Fuca:
the pilot need not exist for the promise to clear.*

**LOS JARDINES.** Pacific, 1528–1973. "The Gardens": islands
reported by Saavedra north of New Guinea, carried on charts, and
in the case of two of them — this is the point of the card — not
formally deleted by the hydrographic offices until 1973, within
Voss's working lifetime. *IV: I was invited to the deletion. It
was a memorandum. Four and a half centuries of garden, closed by
carbon copy. No one proposed a minute of silence, and I have
kept one privately every year since.*

**RICA DE ORO and RICA DE PLATA.** North Pacific, 16th–20th c.
The isles Rich in Gold and Rich in Silver, east of Japan; the
galleon trade was instructed to find them for a hundred years;
Japan formally claimed Rica de Oro's neighbourhood as late as
the twentieth century for a cable station. *IV: The only phantoms
ever shortlisted as telegraph infrastructure. The cable men, of
all people, should have known: they had been grappling the shelf
for decades. Perhaps they did know. The station was never built.
"Not for stations" is, after all, standing policy.*

**TUANAKI.** South of Rarotonga, until c. 1844. The gentle one.
Cook Islanders within living memory of the missionaries had
visited it — two days' sail, low, peopled, hospitable; a helmsman
left an account of a week ashore among people "who did not know
war." Ships in the 1840s found the sea empty. The neighbouring
islands still remembered the families. *IV: The one phantom
mourned as kin. If it sank, geology took a village. If it was
withdrawn, then withdrawal takes the living, and the third place
has a census. I have never been able to file this card. It is
in the box marked PRACTICE, and I am not sorry.*

**THE CROKER MOUNTAINS.** Lancaster Sound, 1818–1819. A range
sighted by Captain John Ross barring the entrance to the
Northwest Passage; named for the Secretary of the Admiralty;
disbelieved by his own officers on the spot. Parry sailed
through the range's exact position the following summer without
wetting a topsail. Ross's career never recovered. *IV: The only
mountain range with a lifespan shorter than the promotion of the
man it flattered. Refraction builds quickly for those in a
hurry. See also the entire nineteenth century.*

**NEW SOUTH GREENLAND.** Weddell Sea, 1823. Reported by Benjamin
Morrell, the greatest liar in the southern ocean, with his usual
wealth of convincing bearings. Never seen again; probably a
mirage of the real coast, displaced by his invented positions.
*IV: Even Morrell's lies were secondhand — displacement doing
the composing, the liar merely taking dictation. It is the
nearest thing the file offers to mercy for him.*

**MARÍA THERESA REEF.** South Pacific, 1843–still charted within
living memory. A whaler's reef south-east of New Zealand; never
found by any survey; retained on charts into the era of satellite
navigation "for the avoidance of doubt." Jules Verne marooned his
castaways on it twice. *IV: The last of the working phantoms —
kept on the modern chart not because anyone believes but because
deletion is the one act a hydrographer cannot survive being wrong
about. We have built our whole science, at the end, around a
single humility: the sea does not confirm removals. Note the
grammar of the retention: "for the avoidance of doubt." Doubt is
precisely what is being maintained. It is the smallest wall in
the world, eleven letters long, and it is kept.*

**THOMPSON ISLAND.** South Atlantic, near Bouvetøya, 1825–1943.
Sighted by a respected sealing captain; searched for by science
for a century; possibly a real volcanic islet destroyed in an
eruption — the one phantom that may have died not of an audit
but of geology. *IV: If it existed and blew up, it is the only
place in my file to have escaped both ledgers at once. There is
no third column for the free. I have looked. That there is no
third column is, of everything in fifty years, the finding I
most wish I could unmake.*

*[The card file's final divider is labelled, in the brown ink,
"NOT YET." It is empty. I have added nothing to it. — E.M.]*
