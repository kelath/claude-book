# Editor's Preface

When Iris Voss disappeared in the autumn of 2019, she left behind eleven
notebooks, four thousand index cards, a storage unit in Bergen containing
several hundred kilograms of maps, and no will. I was her last doctoral
student, which is to say I was the person the estate's solicitor could find.
This book is the result of that telephone call, and of the six years since,
which I have spent — longer than I intended, longer than was perhaps good for
me — in the company of places that do not exist.

Some introduction to the subject is owed to the general reader.

Cartography has always produced errors, but a *phantom place* is an error of a
particular and durable kind: a place that was mapped, named, described, sworn
to — and that was never there. Not a place that vanished. Not a place misdrawn
or misplaced. A place that was, from the beginning, nothing, and that
nevertheless survived on charts for decades or centuries, accumulating
coastlines, soundings, eyewitnesses, and occasionally colonists who set out
for it and were not seen again.

The island of Hy-Brasil appeared on serious European charts for five hundred
years. The Mountains of Kong ran like a wall across West Africa in every
respectable nineteenth-century atlas; they detained real expeditions; they do
not exist. Sandy Island, in the Coral Sea, survived into the twenty-first
century — it was "undiscovered," as the newspapers put it, in November 2012,
when an Australian research vessel sailed to its coordinates and found 1,400
metres of open water. The island was in Google's databases at the time. It had
been copied, chart to chart, since 1876.

Voss spent fifty years collecting these places. Her question — she would be
angry at how simply I am about to state it, but she is not here — was this:
*where does an error live?* A mistaken island is not on the sea. It is not
quite on the map, either, since the map claims to describe the sea. It exists
in some third place, and it can survive there, breeding soundings and
shorelines, for half a millennium. Voss wanted a natural history of that third
place. She called the project, in her grant applications, *A Catalogue of
Cartographic Errors, 1350–present*. In her notebooks she called it the Atlas.

What you hold is the Atlas — or as much of it as I have been able to assemble.
The entries are Voss's, presented in the order indicated by her final index,
with her spellings and her occasional flights retained. The footnotes are
mine.[^1] I have tried to keep them dry. I have not always succeeded, and in
the later parts of the book I stopped trying, for reasons that will become
apparent and that I no longer think I should smooth over. An editor is
supposed to be a pane of glass. I have instead become a participant, and the
reader deserves to know it from the outset.

Three facts, then, stated plainly, because Voss taught me that the plainest
sentence is the hardest to argue with.

First: Iris Voss was last seen on 14 September 2019, boarding a chartered
fishing vessel at Dutch Harbor, in the Aleutian Islands. She was
eighty-eight years old. She told the skipper she wished to verify a depth
sounding. The vessel returned; she did not. The official finding was death by
misadventure at sea. Her body was not recovered.

Second: her final index lists forty-two entries. I found forty-one. The
missing entry — folder VII of the Bergen unit, according to her own
concordance — concerns a place she calls **Meridian**. I have found the name
in her marginalia one hundred and eighteen times. I have found it on no map,
in no gazetteer, in no archive I have consulted, and I have consulted, at this
point, nearly all of them.

Third: among her papers was a bundle of correspondence, seventy-one letters
spanning forty-five years, all from the same hand, all signed with the single
initial **T.** The letters respond to questions of Voss's that I do not have.
The earliest is postmarked Reykjavík, 1974. The latest is postmarked from a
town in the Aleutians, ten days before she disappeared. It consists of one
line: *"You were right about the third place. Bring the blue notebook."*

The blue notebook has not been found.

I did not intend to mention any of this in a scholarly preface. My first
draft confined itself to editorial method — transcription conventions, the
treatment of Voss's inconsistent coordinates, the dating of the notebooks. But
I have spent six years inside this book, and I have come to believe the
apparatus and the mystery cannot be separated, because Voss did not separate
them. The Atlas was never a catalogue. It was an investigation, conducted in
public, in plain sight, one impossible place at a time, and its conclusion —
if it has one — is distributed through these entries like a coastline through
fog. My footnotes mark where I think the land is. The reader is free to take
soundings of their own.

A note on method, briefly, for my colleagues, who will be looking for
something to disapprove of: all quotations from primary sources have been
verified against the originals except where noted; coordinates are given as
Voss gave them, even where they drift[^2]; translations are Voss's unless
attributed. The forty-one entries are complete. Nothing has been omitted.

One thing has been added. It is the last item in the appendices, and it is
mine, and I ask the reader — as Voss once asked me, in a different context,
in a map room in Oxford with the rain coming down — to withhold judgement
until they have seen the evidence.

She said that to me about an island that does not exist.

I found the island persuasive. I find it more persuasive now.

— E.M., Cambridge, January 2026

[^1]: Numbered footnotes throughout are the editor's. Voss's own notes, where
they survive, are printed as indented passages marked *IV*.

[^2]: The reader will notice that certain coordinates in Parts III and IV do
not stay fixed between entries. This is not a transcription error. It is the
single most consistent inconsistency in the papers, and I address it in the
Afterword.
