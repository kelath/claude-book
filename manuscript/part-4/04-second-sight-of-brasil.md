# 29. The Second Sight of Brasil

**From folder XXX. A notebook of four days on Inishmore, Aran
Islands, September 1991. The folklore allows the island one showing
in seven years. The folklore is off by nothing.**

Three years after Vienna, when I had stopped being invited to things,
a letter came that contained no answer to any question I had asked.
It contained a date, a cliff, and an hour, and the sentence: *Dress
warmly. The showing is long this cycle.* I have received, across
forty-five years, perhaps a dozen sentences from my correspondent
that were instructions rather than replies, and I have obeyed all of
them, and I note — for the file, for the successor, for the court
that will never sit — that obedience was never once demanded. An
instruction from T. has the grammar of a train timetable. One is not
commanded to catch the 14:06. It is merely true that the 14:06 will
run.

I took rooms above the harbour and walked out on the third evening
to the cliffs at the island's back, where the land stands three
hundred feet against the Atlantic in slabs like the minute-books of
a very old firm, and I sat down out of the wind in the socket of
stone I had been directed to, and I waited for the top of the hour,
and at the top of the hour the sea to the west stopped being empty.

I had spent thirty years by then establishing what the look of
nothing is, and I must now put on record the look of the other
thing, and I find the plainest way is the truest: it did not appear.
It was *disclosed*. The horizon did not produce an island; the
horizon proved to have been, all along, a curtain hanging slightly
proud of the wall, and for the length of the showing the hang of it
altered, and behind it stood what the charts of five centuries had
copied from one another with such fidelity: land, round-shouldered,
divided across its middle by a channel that took the last of the
light like a seam of tin. Dalorto's disc. The cleft. The very
punctuation of the coastline agreed with the portolans — agreed, I
mean, the way a face agrees with a portrait, establishing in one
glance which of the two had been sitting for the other for five
hundred years.

I did what I am. I sketched, timed, took bearings I already knew
would not survive the evening; the notebook page is in the folder,
and the reader who consults it will find the bearings written in a
hand that starts as mine and steadies, over four lines, into
something with no tremor in it at all, because — this is the finding
of the first hour, and I set it down against every account of such
things in the folklore — the state that came upon me on that cliff
was not ecstasy and was not fear. It was *competence*. I have felt
it since only once. It is the feeling of an instrument brought at
last into the conditions it was calibrated for, and it is the most
seductive feeling I have ever had, and I believe it is the beginning
of how the post is filled.

At the second hour a man came up the cliff path with a lantern he
did not light and stood at a distance that was exactly courtesy, and
waited to be noticed, and said, when I turned: "There's water under
her tonight. There's time to row it, if we go now."

He was old in the way the second officer of the Herdis was old —
the kindness with the fathom under it — and his currach, he said,
was on the shingle at the gap below, and he said nothing else at
all: no name, no price, no persuasion. The showing stood in the
west. The channel through the island's middle was a road of last
light. I record what I can of the next quarter hour, which I spent
in the ordinary agony of a woman at a departure board, and the
reader — my reader, the one this folder was always for — must
forgive the poverty of the account, because the state of declining
cannot be observed from inside while it is being performed; it can
only be reported afterward, from its wreckage, the way weather is.

I said: not tonight.

He nodded — I will carry the nod to whatever weighing awaits me;
it was the nod of a man confirming a delivery, not accepting a
refusal — and went back down the path, and I stayed on the cliff,
and at moonrise the curtain recovered its hang, and the horizon
resumed the imposture of a wall, and I walked back across the
island in the dark, and in my room I was sick with something that
was not illness, and slept fourteen hours, and woke knowing three
things as facts, the way one knows soundings.

That the offer was real, and had been mine, and was gone.

That it had been made early and made resistible — the lantern
unlit, the price unnamed, the persuasion withheld — because it was
*meant* to be declined; that declining was not the failure of the
appointment but its content; that I had been taught, in one
evening, at the cost of one island, what declining feels like, and
that nothing about the lesson was cruel except its accuracy.

And that there would be a second, in its own year, in another
place, dressed — I did not yet know the dressing — as an errand;
and that between the first offer and the second the only work I
would find worth doing was the work of this Atlas: the keeping of
my column, the earning of my sentence, the long discipline of
writing down the differences of others while my own difference
stood open on the page, accruing, patient, entered against a name
I have spent fifty years learning is spelled the way mine is.

    Margin, later, black ink: The folklore says the island shows
    once in seven years, and the folklore is exact, and the
    scholars who collected it never once asked the question their
    own material begged. A thing that keeps a schedule is not a
    ghost. It is a SERVICE. The showing is not an apparition; it
    is a sailing, and it has been leaving on time, from the same
    berth, for as long as the west of Ireland has kept records —
    and the record of a service is not its legend. It is its
    ridership.[^51]

[^51]: The arithmetic, since Voss for once does not do it for us:
from her showing of 1991, the seven-year schedule gives 1998, 2005,
2012, and 2019 — the reader may consult the preface for what Iris
Voss, eighty-eight years old and four thousand miles from Aran,
chose to do in the autumn of the 2019 sailing — and then 2026. I
made my own visit to those cliffs in the winter of 2021, and I
recorded in my first footnotes, with an editor's complacency, that
I saw nothing. I understand the complacency better now: 2021 was
not a showing year. I went in the wrong year, which is, I have come
to think, why I was allowed to go. The next sailing is 2026. This
edition, the reader may care to know, is published in 2026; the
date was set eighteen months ago by the publisher, on grounds of
paper supply and the autumn lists; I have checked, because by now I
check everything, and the schedule is entirely innocent, and I have
stopped believing that innocence and coincidence are different
words for anything.
