# 27. The Anchorage

**From folder XXVIII. A notebook of the m/s Herdis, Hobart toward
Lyttelton and beyond, southern winter, 1982. No position is given
anywhere in the folder. That is the entry.**

I have spent eleven entries of this Atlas teaching the reader to
distrust single witnesses, and now I must ask for the courtesy I have
spent a book undermining. What follows happened to one woman, at
night, at sea, in her fifty-first year, and she wrote it down within
the day, and she has checked it against nothing because there is
nothing to check it against. Weigh it accordingly. I have.

We were nine days out in weather the mate called ordinary and the
cook called biblical, and on the ninth evening the engine note
changed. Not trouble — a helm order. I had stood enough watches by
then, paying my passage in coffee and quiet, to know that we had come
some points north of our course, and I went up, and the master was
on the bridge with the second, and neither spoke, and the ship was
running with a following sea toward a dark that was — I want the
exact word and there is none in English, so I will build it — a dark
that was *administrative*. Not weather. A jurisdiction.

We put in. There is no other verb; the sea organised itself into an
approach; the swell stood down as if a hand had been laid on it; and
the Herdis, nine days from any charted lee, rode at anchor before
midnight in still water, in the shelter of land I could smell — wet
stone, peat smoke, tide-wrack — and could not, in that dark, see.
Around us, at moorings, other vessels: I counted eleven riding
lights, and they were not all of our century. I say that plainly and
will not decorate it. A riding light tells you its era the way a
hand tells you its writer, and two of the eleven were flames.

No one came aboard. The crew did not go ashore, did not lower a
boat, did not speak above the necessary. This was not fear as I
know fear. It was the behaviour of men in a place where they are
known — the punctilio of a rough parish in its cathedral. The cook
brought up food nobody ate. At one bell I asked the second, who had
stood aside for me on the ladder all voyage and never offered three
words together, where we were. He looked at me with a kindness I
still turn over, and said: "In the roads." Which roads, I said. And
he said, "The roads, Professor," as one corrects a child who has
asked which sky, and went below, and left me the bridge wing and
the smell of a country I will not find.

I did what I am. I took bearings on the shore lights — there were
shore lights, low and few, the colour of banked fires — and noted
the anchor bearing and the depth from the sounder, and I wrote the
figures in this notebook by the binnacle lamp. They are on the next
leaf. The reader of my Atlas, if these notes ever have one, may
turn to it now.

*[The next leaf is in the folder. The recto carries, in her field
hand, three compass bearings and a sounding. The ink — I state this
because I must — is the brown ink throughout, and the sounding
reads 1,241 metres, which the reader will observe is given from an
anchored ship, in still water, riding in shelter. I offer no
comment except the one arithmetic requires: no anchorage on earth
lies in twelve hundred metres of water. Ships cannot anchor there.
The Herdis rode to her cable all night. — E.M.][^49]*

Toward four the tide turned, or something did. The riding lights
stood up one by one and went out — not extinguished: *withdrawn*,
the way a shopkeeper takes the lamp from the window at close of
trade — and the smell of land faded first from the west, which is
not a direction smells fade from, and the master said "heave in"
in the voice men use in churches and sickrooms, and we came out as
we had gone in, stern-first through a dark that handed us back to
the weather like a coat returned at a cloakroom. By six the swell
was ordinary and biblical again and the engine had its sea-note,
and the log — I have said the crew were punctilious — the log for
the day reads *hove to in heavy weather, no progress made*, in the
master's own hand, and the noon positions bracketing that day are
eighty miles apart.

I asked no one anything at breakfast. This was the second thing I
learned in the roads: the etiquette travels with you. But when we
raised Lyttelton the master, paying me the compliment of my
profession at the gangway, held my hand a moment too long and said
the only sentence anyone ever offered me freely in fifty years of
this work. "You'll have noticed," he said, "we didn't pay." And
seeing that I had not understood — I understood four years later,
at a weighbridge of another kind — he touched his cap and said it
again, kindly, the whole doctrine in six words, the sentence I
have put at the head of the folder and will put at the head of
the Part if the editors of my estate have any ear at all:

"Anchorage is free. The chart pays."

    Later, aboard, same hand, margin: They keep a roads for the
    working traffic of the world, and the fee is entered not
    against the ships but against the map. Every vessel that has
    ever ridden there is a vessel some chart has lost — an error
    of position, an impossible noon, a day that had to be written
    as weather. We have been reading those log entries for three
    centuries and calling them incompetence. They are receipts.

[^49]: One matter of record, and then I will keep my promise of
brevity. The m/s Herdis existed; Norwegian registry; broken up at
Alang in 1994. I have found her logbook — the line for the
southern winter of 1982 reads as Voss quotes it — and I have found
one crew list, and the second officer's name on it, and the name
is not T. and not Salter and I did not expect it to be. He is
alive. He is ninety-one, in a home outside Bergen, and I visited
him in March, the day before the warehouse, and I asked him about
the roads. He looked at me a long time with the eyes they all
seem to have, the kindness with a fathom under it, and he said:
"She wrote it down, did she." Not a question. I said she had. He
nodded and looked out the window at the rain and said, "Then it's
her entry against ours," and would say nothing else at all.
