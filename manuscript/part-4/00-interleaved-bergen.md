# Interleaved: Bergen

*[Written on the night train from Oslo, 19–20 March 2026, and printed
as written. I have stopped pretending these notes are apparatus. They
are the other half of the book. — E.M.]*

The storage unit stands in a converted herring warehouse on the north
side of the Vågen, and the boxes are as I left them in 2021, which is
the first thing I checked and the last thing I can say plainly.

The facility has a manager, a courteous man near retirement who
remembers Voss — she took the unit in 1987, paid by standing order,
visited, by his ledger, eleven times in thirty-two years. The ledger
is the good kind, bound, initialled. I asked to see it. Three lines
concern this note.

The first: 12 March 2019, six months before Dutch Harbor. *I. Voss* —
her last visit, four hours, "took away one carton" in the manager's
annotation. The same month, the reader will recall without wanting
to, that someone signed the Braidense register in Milan. I checked
the dates against her passport, which is among the papers. The
passport shows no travel after January. Bergen has an airport and I
have no doubt she reached it; I mention the passport because I have
promised to mention everything, and because mentioning it costs me
the last comfortable explanation of the Milan signature.

The second line: 4 October 2019, three weeks after the boat. The
estate was not yet settled; no one had authority; yet the line reads
*fullmakt* — by proxy, papers shown — and the visitor's signature is
an upright, old-fashioned European hand that the reader has now met
three times: on a symposium tape asking about staffing, in a Milanese
register, and here, in a herring warehouse, three weeks after the
death of the woman whose shelf he came to visit. The manager
remembers him imperfectly and warmly, which appears to be the only
way anyone remembers him. "An older colleague of the professor's. Or
younger. He knew the unit. He was inside forty minutes and took
nothing and left the light on." The manager's phrase. *Left the
light on.* I have carried it for a week like a stone in the shoe.

The third line is dated the Tuesday before my own visit. *Halter &
Cie, Basel — inspection under court order.* The order is real; I
have now seen it; one of the heirs — I said in my preface they asked
not to be named individually, and I keep that, but the reader may
know there are four, and that one of the four has been, since
January, in correspondence with Basel that the other three knew
nothing of — one heir has petitioned to force valuation of the
estate "including all documentary assets," which requires
inspection, which requires access, which is how a firm that has
been refused four times walked past every refusal with a folder of
stamped paper. They were inside three hours. The manager, who did
not like them and did not say so, itemised for me what they had
handled, because he had stood in the doorway the whole time on the
strength of a clause in his own contract he may have invented on
the spot. God keep the minor officials of this world; they are its
load walls. What Halter's people photographed was the shelving.
Not the boxes. The *shelving*.

So I audited the shelf. Voss's folders stand in numbered ranks,
I through XXXIV, in the flat grey dust of thirty years, and the
reader knows what I went to look for. Folder VII is not there; that
was never in question. What I went for was the gap, and the gap is
the finding.

There is no dust in it. In a run of shelving furred like a moth's
wing, the width of one folder — I measured it, eleven centimetres —
is clean. Not swept-clean, not the smear of a removal: *kept* clean,
maintained, the way a step is kept by daily use, and at the back of
the clean interval, propped upright against the wall of the shelf,
where you would find the card in a well-run lending library, there
is a card. Cream, unruled, the size of a reader's ticket. I knew the
object before I touched it because I have been describing it for a
hundred and twenty pages.

On the front, in an upright, old-fashioned hand: **VII. Out.**

On the reverse, in brown ink — in the brown ink, the pre-1980 ink,
the ink of a decade in which I was learning to read — my name, in
full, and one sentence:

*When the book is finished, bring it; you know the meridian; decline
if you can. — T.*

I sat on the warehouse floor for some time. The manager brought me
coffee and did not ask, which I record because kindness should be in
the register too.

Here is what I understand, stated as flatly as I can manage on a
train. The slot has been dusted for years. The absence of folder VII
is not a loss; it is a *lending*, administered, current, its card in
place, and the card has been waiting for me by name since before I
could have read it — unless it was written last Tuesday by someone
with a bottle of old ink, which is the fabrication thesis again,
thinner each time I need it and never quite empty, God rest it, it
has been a good rail to hold. And the sentence is the first offer.
The reader of the Vienna entry knows the doctrine: offered twice;
the first time so that it can be declined; there is no other way to
learn what declining feels like. *Decline if you can* is not a
threat. It is, I believe, the syllabus's final course requirement,
and I am being examined on it now, in this carriage, each time I
look at my bag where the card lies flat in a sleeve between my own
notes and hers.

What I have decided, watching the fjords go by in the dark, is
this: I will do what she did, which is the only method I was ever
taught. She was given a sentence and spent fifty years earning it.
I have been given one too. The six entries that follow are Voss's
own — the Blank Quarter, the places with no literature but her.
I will print them exactly, and I will write the afterword I owe,
and then the book will be finished, and the sentence on the card
will come due, and the reader and I will discover together what
I am — a scholar, or a difference about to be struck.

The train reaches Oslo at six. The light in the unit, I should
say, I turned off.
