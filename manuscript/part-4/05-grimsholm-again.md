# 30. Grimsholm, Again

**From folder XXXI. A notebook of two days out of Raufarhöfn,
Iceland, June 2003. The island was struck from the charts in 1820.
The island was not consulted.**

The reader of Part I will remember Grimsholm: the black-sided holm
north-east of Iceland with the sound through its middle, a century
and a half of Danish sightings, a furnished cairn, an Admiralty that
declared it not for stations, and an erasure after 1817 so complete
that the record does not even class it among the errors. My entry on
it closed with a sounding and a suspicion. This folder is the
suspicion, paid in person.

The conditions came, as conditions come, by letter: a port, a month,
the name of a skipper who would be going out for lumpfish and would
not mind company, and one instruction of the timetable kind — *Take
a stone from your own ground. You will know the plate when you see
it.* I carried a grey beach pebble from below the Bergen warehouse
in my coat pocket for three weeks, and I have never in my life been
so continuously aware of eleven grams.

The skipper was the age they are, with the hands they have, and he
asked me nothing at all until the second dawn, when the fog came
down — the reader knows by now what the fog is; the gap under the
door; I have stopped explaining my own weather — and he throttled
back and said, conversationally, watching the grey: "You'll have
brought your stone." Not a question; the countersignature again. I
said I had. He nodded and put us in.

It is exactly as the Jægeren's men deposed in 1688, and I mean
exactly, and the exactness is the first finding: black sides with
the rust in seams — *ruddled*, the old word, the right word — the
sound running north and south through the island's middle like the
slot of a money-box, the beach of black sand warm underfoot, not
volcanically, not explicably, warm the way a chair is warm when
someone has risen from it. Three hundred and fifteen years between
their landing and mine, and no drift in the description at all. The
reader of this Atlas has been taught that errors do not degrade
only when something maintains them. The doctrine holds ashore. The
island is kept like a lighthouse lens.

I walked the sound first, because I am what I am. It is perhaps
four hundred metres of water between walls of dark stone, and I
paced the western shore of it end to end in nine minutes going in.
I will state the second finding as navigation and let it lie: the
walk out, by the same shore, at the same pace, took twenty-one, and
at the midpoint going out — only going out — the light through the
northern mouth was the flat white light of high summer over open
sea, and the light through the southern mouth, two hundred metres
away, was low, and amber, and lay on the water like evening in a
latitude I could not name. The sound is longer inside than the
island is wide, and it does not everywhere open on the same day.
I did not linger. There is a competence that comes on me in such
places; I have described it at Aran; I have learned to ration it
like a drug, because it is one.

The cairn stands above the eastern shore where the depositions put
it, and before it in the turf lies the flat stone, and on the flat
stone, cut deep and kept clean — kept, the reader understands;
nothing on that island suffers moss — the sign: the circle divided
by a line. The beam and the two pans, seen end-on. I had drawn it
for fifty years. I had never once stood on the pan.

The cairn itself is the manifest. I counted — of course I counted;
it is the only instrument I brought that cannot lie — three hundred
and forty-one stones, and they are not local: grey Baltic granite
and red Devon sandstone and a flint from some chalk coast and one
palm-sized piece of what I believe was ship's ballast, basalt,
dressed. Three hundred and forty-one landings. The ridership,
banked. The 1688 men found the cairn already standing, and now I
could read the sentence I wrote about them with its true stress:
the island came furnished — *because the furniture is the ledger*.

I did what the letter said. Stone on the plate, then on the pile.

I will not do here what I have done all my life, which is reach for
the instruments, because the instruments have nothing to say about
the interval — I think it was an interval; the fog neither
thickened nor thinned, and the waterfall behind me did not pause,
and yet between the pebble's touching the flat stone and its
settling there, something was subtracted from me and something was
added, in quantities I could not then and cannot now name, and the
nearest I can come after twenty years is this: that I arrived on
that island carrying fifty years of open questions, and I left
carrying the same questions closed — not answered; *closed*, the
way an account is closed, the way a file is closed, rolled shut
into a figure and carried forward — and the figure is in me, and I
do not know its sign, and I walked down the black beach to the
boat with my hands smelling of iron though I had touched only my
own clean pebble, and the smell has never entirely washed out, and
I have learned to be grateful that ledgers do not explain
themselves to their entries.

    Margin, much later, the shaking hand: Iron in the seams of the
    island; iron in the gall of the ink; iron on the tongue at
    every threshold; rust the colour of every door — Bermeja red,
    the ruddled walls, the amber light in the sound going out. The
    ledger of the world is kept in the one metal that remembers
    water. They have weighed me twice now. This was the first, and
    I did not know it until the second, which is the way of
    weighings, and of offers, and of everything else that is done
    to us twice.[^52]

The skipper took us off at slack water. He looked at my hands once
— I had said nothing — and gave me the nod, the delivery nod, and
we ran south-west out of the fog into a bright ordinary sea with
Iceland standing on the horizon like a fact. On the sounder, as
the island went from sight, the depth: 1,205 metres. The 1817
survey found the same figure over the position it then struck from
the charts. Two hundred years of hydrography have improved every
number in the northern ocean except that one. It does not need
improving. It is not a measurement. It is a registration.

[^52]: The folder contains, besides the notebook, a small cotton
bag of the kind coin-collectors use, machine-sewn, not old. In it
is a black pebble, basalt, sea-worn, twenty-two grams — not the
grey Bergen pebble, whose fate the entry accounts for. The bag is
labelled in the upright hand, not hers: "For the successor. Return
if declining." I have weighed it three times on the scale whose
ticket I keep. It has not changed. I carry it now in my left coat
pocket, and I am aware of it, as she was of hers, continuously;
and I note, because the reader and I no longer pretend otherwise,
that a stone from Grimsholm is a stone from my own ground in no
sense I can construct — unless the phrase was never about geology,
and one's own ground is wherever one's difference is banked, in
which case the labelling is exact, and the island is expecting me.
