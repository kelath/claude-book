# 32. The Approaches

**From folder XXXIII: a single notebook, bought — the price sticker
is still on the board — in Dutch Harbor, Alaska. The first page is
headed 13 September 2019. The reader knows the date of the boat.
This is the night before.**

The last entry of an atlas of errors should be written in the form
the whole book has been correcting, and I am eighty-eight and
entitled to my joke. What follows are sailing directions. They are
accurate. That has always been the trouble with this subject: not
that the accounts are false, but that they are pilotage.

*APPROACHES. The place is not closed. Nothing in this Atlas has
ever been closed; the reader has my life's work for it: struck
from the chart is not struck from the world. The approaches are
maintained and buoyed, after their fashion, and the sailing
directions are these.*

*Take departure from any port that keeps a shelf. The reader who
does not know whether their port keeps a shelf has not yet placed
a letter.*

*Shape your course for the meridian of one hundred sixty-eight
west. Any latitude will serve that the fog will serve; the
position drifts, as working positions do — it is a berth, not a
monument — but the meridian is constant, because a meridian is
the one line on earth that men agreed to draw from nothing, by
convention, out of clean air: the purest error of the whole
inventory, the promise with no terrain under it at all, and
therefore — the reader has had thirty-one entries to see it
coming — the soundest ground in the world to build a door on.*

*Await the fog as you would await a tide. It makes up from the
west against the wind's grain; the local men will glance at it
twice and say nothing; when the glass stands steady and the fog
comes anyway, that is the ebb you work.*

*The hour is the hour of the offices. At sea, 0600 has been kept
by every station in this book. Keep it.*

*Carry: a stone from your own ground. The blue notebook — I speak
now to one reader — or its successor, whatever ledger holds your
column entire, because the errand is a deposit, and this is the
last doctrine and I will state it in the plainest words I own.
They do not want us. They want the DIFFERENCES. Fifty years I
feared the reconciliation as a summons on my person; it is
nothing of the kind; the person is merely the vessel of carriage,
as a ship is to a cargo, and the cargo is the column — the
lifetime of gaps between the world as filed and the world as
found, which cannot be banked by post, which must come by hand,
as everything of weight in this economy has always come by hand.
El Dorado taught the whole of it and I was forty years slow: the
gold goes INTO the water. The ceremony is not an execution and
never was. It is an accession.*

*Soundings on the final approach: the lead will find the shelf at
six hundred and seventy-eight fathoms. When the arm brings up
iron, stand on.*

*Of the place itself I write nothing, and not from ignorance. Its
folder is where folders of its kind are kept: out, lent forward,
its card in the rack. I have read it. I returned it. Some entries
are not for the general shelf, and mine — I have earned the right
to say this once, in my own book, on my last night of keeping it
— mine is one of them.*

There. The directions are given, and the reader who takes them is
not my reader anymore but my relief, and I am glad I will not be
there to see it, and I am lying, and an atlas should contain one
plain lie, clearly marked, so that the reader knows what the truth
was wearing all those years. That was it.

Housekeeping, then, for the estate. The papers go to the first of
my students who, on being telephoned by the solicitor, asks about
the papers before asking about the money. I have watched them all
for forty years; I know which one it will be; I taught her the
look of nothing and she went to the cliffs and looked anyway. The
letters stay with the papers. The catalogue of my failings is in
the notebooks and may be published entire. The unit in Bergen is
paid forward; whoever inherits the shelf: leave the light on. It
is not for finding things. It is so the shelf can see the room.

I have kept every appointment of a long life except the ones that
mattered, which kept themselves, and came to me, wearing fog and
courtesy. Tomorrow I keep one properly, on my own keel, by my own
pilotage, with my column under my arm. The Alma K. lies at the
fish dock; her skipper has the bearing and thinks I am a mad old
woman verifying a sounding, which is, as it happens, the exact
truth. High water is at 05:40. The fog is making up now over the
harbour against the grain of the wind, and the glass, I have just
gone out onto the balcony to tap it, stands steady as a kept
promise.

I was told, at the very beginning: most stop at the sum. I did
not stop. Let the record show — she will see to the record; it is
why I chose her — that the difference column of Iris Voss, begun
Reykjavík 1974, closes tomorrow, in fair weather, at the hour of
the offices, paid in full, by hand.

The tools go back on the shelf.

*[Here the notebooks end. — E.M.]*[^54]

[^54]: The inquest record, for completeness. The Alma K. returned
to Dutch Harbor at 1900 on 14 September 2019. The skipper's
testimony: she asked him to hold the bearing and lie to at the
position from 0545; fog closed in at 0555 "with the glass steady";
at 0610 he secured the engine at her request, "for the quiet";
when the fog lifted at 0640 the afterdeck was empty. Her coat was
folded on the transom rail with her boots squared beside it, in
the manner, the report notes with the inquest's unconscious
poetry, "of a bather who intends to return." No notebook was
found aboard, blue or otherwise; no stone; and the carton she had
removed from Bergen that March was not among her effects in the
hotel, which inventoried as: one bed unslept-in, one barometer
tapped so often its owner's fingerprint was recoverable from the
glass, and, on the desk, addressed to no one, the notebook this
entry is printed from — open, weighted flat, against the wind
from the balcony door, which she had left, the chambermaid
deposed, wide open onto the fog. The weight holding the pages
was a grey beach pebble. I have it. It is Bergen shore-stone,
and it is the one object from that room the reader now knows the
provenance of: it is the stone she carried to Grimsholm in 2003
and set on the plate and left on the cairn. It came back. They
gave it back. The manifest, I have to conclude, is not where her
stone is banked anymore, because on the fourteenth of September
2019, at the hour of the offices, on the meridian of clean air,
Iris Voss stopped being a passenger of this economy and was
entered — I can find no other reading of the pebble on the desk
— into its fixtures. The successor's stone, the black one, the
twenty-two grams in my left pocket: I understand at last what it
is. It is not a token of invitation. It is stock, issued in
advance, from the shelf — and the label did not say "keep if
accepting." It said return if declining, because the stone, like
the post, like the papers, like the book the reader is nearly
done holding, was never the candidate's to keep. It was the
house's to lend.
