# 28. The Carrier

**From folder XXIX. A notebook of nine days in Tórshavn, Faroe
Islands, October 1996. The usual place.**

Sixty of the letters came by hand, and for twenty-two years I did not
ask whose. This was not incuriosity. It was the etiquette, which I
had learned at sea before I could have named it: in the roads, no one
goes ashore. But in the autumn of 1996 a letter ended with a
postscript that was, I understood much later, itself the lesson —
*You may watch, if you like. Watching is free* — and so I went to the
usual place, and I watched, and this folder is what I have to show
for the only surveillance I ever conducted in fifty years of being,
myself, so plainly watched.

The usual place is a bookshop by the harbour in Tórshavn: low
ceilings, peat-brown light, sailing directions and psalm books and
the strata of a reading town. My arrangement was twelve years old by
then and had never varied. Replies to my correspondent went inside
the back cover of a particular copy of the *Norwegian Sea Pilot*,
third edition, a book so obsolete and so unlovely that its patience
on the shelf was its own explanation. Answers came to my hotel, or
to Bergen, or once — I have never printed this — to a cabin I had
told no one I had taken, on a ship I had boarded under a name that
was not mine. I placed my letter on the first evening, and then I
did what the trade of watching requires: I took the window table in
the café opposite, and I kept the shop's door in view for nine days.

I will spare the reader the field notes, which record, hour upon
hour, the retail life of a small northern bookshop in October:
schoolchildren, clergy, a man who came four days running to fail to
buy the same atlas. No one went to the shelf. On the fifth day I
varied the method, as one does when the instrument may be the error:
I checked the Pilot at opening and at noon and at closing, standing
in the aisle myself with my thumb in the back cover. My letter,
patient. On the ninth evening, at ten minutes to closing, with the
shop empty and the owner at his ledger and my own eyes on the shelf
— I state it so, flatly, and the reader may bank it with the rest of
my account — the letter was not there, and an answer was: four
pages, the upright hand, the ink still smelling faintly of iron,
answering points I had raised in a letter that had left my hands
two hundred and twenty hours before, in a room I had not stopped
watching.

I did not go back to the café. I went to the ledger, and the owner
— Jespersen, since he is dead now and his name was his own — looked
up at me and closed the book, and I saw that he had been waiting
nine days for me to stop being a scholar, with the specific patience
of a man who has waited longer for stranger things.

What he told me, over the shop's peat stove, with the sign turned
and the harbour going black in the window, I set down nearly in his
words, because his words were better than mine.

He said he had held the office thirty-one years. He said it came to
him the way it comes: an envelope with his name on it, found in the
effects of the man whose shop he had inherited — the name written,
he said without theatre, in an ink older than he was, and he had
known what the envelope meant before he opened it because — and
here he looked at me — "one always knows; the reading of it is
only the countersignature." I asked what the office required. He
said: nothing. He said: "I have never carried a letter in my life.
I keep the shelf." I asked how, then, the letters moved. And he
gave me the doctrine that I have carried since like a coin sewn in
a coat, the sentence the whole Atlas had been circling for forty
entries without the wit to land on:

"The letters do not travel, Professor. The shelves do the
travelling. A kept shelf is one place, wherever it stands. There
are not many of us, but there do not need to be many of one
place."

    Margin, same night: The office is not a person and was never
    a person; Philip was not the first carrier but the first
    PREMISES — a man made into an address. The network is not a
    web of couriers. It is a single room, distributed. Tórshavn's
    back cover and a hotel desk in Veracruz and a mail sack on a
    dead ship are one shelf, and the letters lie still on it while
    the world's positions change around them. Of course the
    replies are prompt. Nothing has any distance to cross. I asked
    him what happens when a keeper dies. He said, "The shelf is
    kept," in the grammar the Admiralty used about stations, and
    put another brick of peat on the stove.

One thing more, and then the folder closes. As I stood to leave he
opened the drawer of his ledger-desk — I had not asked; this was
given, and I believe it was the actual appointment for which my
nine days of watching were the fee — and showed me what it held. A
shallow tray of letters, perhaps twenty, each in the upright hand,
each sealed. "The uncollected," he said. Letters that had reached
the shelf and never been fetched: the addressees had declined, or
died before their date, or — his phrase — "were re-addressed in
transit." He let me look; he did not let me touch; and I record,
because this book is a ledger and a ledger does not flinch, that
the topmost envelope carried a name I knew, and that the name was
not mine, and that I have spent more nights than I will confess
deciding whether to print it, and I will not. She may yet collect
it. It is not for me to strike her difference.[^50]

I took the boat south the next morning. At the rail, in a wind
like a slammed door, I understood the postscript at last. *Watching
is free.* So it was: nine days, and no toll taken, and nothing
shown to me that was not freely given. But I had placed a letter
and received a letter, and between those two acts I had sat at a
window counting the traffic of a kept place for two hundred and
twenty hours, and somewhere — I knew it as I knew my own name; the
knowledge has the taste of iron — my count of the shop had been
entered, in a fair hand, against my page in a ledger I had been
shown precisely once, in the form of a drawer I was not permitted
to touch. Anchorage is free. The chart pays. Watching is free.

The watcher pays.

[^50]: I have read this entry more times than any other in the
papers, for a reason the reader of my Bergen note will supply
without help. In February I wrote to the shop — it survives, under
Jespersen's grandson — asking, as a scholar does, dressed in my
college's letterhead, whether the business held any correspondence
relating to Professor Voss. The reply came in nine days. It is
typed, courteous, one sentence, and I give it whole: "Your letter
has been placed." I have not written again. The book you are
holding is my second letter, and I have begun to suspect it will
be delivered the same way — that publication is not an announcement
but a *placement*, and that somewhere a shelf has been kept for it
a long time, and that the reader now holding it open has, in the
only sense that has ever mattered to this Atlas, just walked into
the shop.
