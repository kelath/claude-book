# 3. Saint Brendan's Isle

**Position: variable. West of Ireland; later south-west of the Canaries;
later gone.**

Every science has ancestors it would rather not produce at parties, and the
study of phantom islands descends from a sixth-century monk in a boat made
of hide.

The *Navigatio Sancti Brendani Abbatis* — the Voyage of Saint Brendan the
Abbot — was medieval Europe's most successful travel book. It survives in
over a hundred manuscripts, which for the Middle Ages is not popularity but
saturation. It recounts how Brendan of Clonfert, hearing reports of a
Promised Land of the Saints far out in the western ocean, built a currach,
provisioned it for forty days, embarked with fourteen monks, and sailed for
seven years among wonders: an island of enormous white sheep; a paradise of
birds who were, it emerges, neutral angels serving out the Fall; a pillar of
crystal in the sea; a rain of fire from an island of smiths, which a reader
tracing the route notices is a fair description of Iceland doing what
Iceland does.

And the whale. The episode everyone remembers, the one carried on charts
and altarpieces alike: the monks land on a low grey island to celebrate
Easter Mass, kindle their fire, and the island shudders and begins to swim.
They scramble for the boat. Brendan alone is unafraid, because Brendan has
understood, and he blesses the beast — the manuscripts name it Jasconius —
and each Easter thereafter the whale surfaces to serve as the company's
church. The monks return annually to hold Mass on the back of the thing
they once mistook for land.

    IV: Note that the error is not corrected. The error is *domesticated*.
    They knew it was a whale and they held Easter on it for seven years.
    This is the healthiest relationship with a phantom place in the whole
    literature, and it was achieved in the sixth century, and everything
    since has been decline.

The *Navigatio* is a monastic fable, patterned on older Irish voyage tales,
never meant as a sailing report — that much scholarship agrees on. But the
mapmakers were not scholars, and the Promised Land of the Saints was too
good a destination to waste. It appears — as *Insula Sancti Brendani*,
usually generous in size — on the Hereford *mappa mundi* around 1300, near
the Fortunate Isles. Behaim's globe of 1492, the oldest surviving globe on
earth, carries it in mid-Atlantic with a long inscription. And here the
island does something Hy-Brasil never managed, the thing that earns it its
entry in the Atlas: it *moves*.

Charts of the fourteenth century place Saint Brendan's Isle west of
Ireland. By the sixteenth it has slid south, to the latitude of the
Canaries. By the seventeenth it is a specific hidden neighbour of the
Canaries themselves — the islanders called it San Borondón, and claimed to
see it from the western cliffs of La Palma and El Hierro: a mountainous
profile on the horizon that clouds always closed over before a boat could
close with it. As the frontier of the known Atlantic expanded, the island
retreated ahead of it, always one province beyond the surveyed, like a
figure keeping its distance in a fog.

    IV: Brasil sat still and let the world's belief wash over it for five
    centuries. Brendan's island kept exactly one horizon ahead of the
    admiralties for a thousand years. Stationary phantoms die by sounding.
    Migratory phantoms do not die. — Third law. I can find no chart on
    which San Borondón was ever *disproved*. The category was simply
    closed.

The Spanish took San Borondón seriously enough to search. Expeditions went
out from the Canaries in 1526, in 1570, in 1604, in 1721 — that is two
hundred years of official voyages to an island whose original source
document features a service held on a whale. The 1570 inquiry gathered
sworn testimony: a Portuguese pilot who had landed and found cattle
tracks; a hermit who watched the island through an entire afternoon. The
1721 expedition under Gaspar Domínguez carried two friars for the landing
Mass. All returned with weather stories. The judicial tone of the archives,
Voss noted, never falters; nobody laughs; the island is treated throughout
as an administrative problem, a parish that had wandered off and needed to
be re-enrolled.[^7]

Sightings from La Palma continued into the twentieth century. There is a
photograph, taken in 1958 by one Manuel Rodríguez Quintero, that shows —
something; a dark mass under cloud on the sea-line; the reader may consult
it and join every previous consulter in deciding that it proves what they
already believed. The consensus explanation for all of it is a mirage:
under certain conditions the image of El Hierro, or of a cloud bank, is
refracted and displaced, and the eye, which abhors an unlabelled thing,
files it as land. The explanation is almost certainly right. Voss accepted
it without resistance, which the reader of this Atlas will by now recognise
as her habit just before she says the thing she actually means:

    IV: A mirage is a real image in a wrong place. The light from
    somewhere is genuinely arriving. We call it false because we privilege
    the object over the image, but the image is the only part anyone ever
    touched. San Borondón is a delivery with no return address. It would
    be worth learning what is doing the sending. — cf. M., again. The
    displacement is the signature.[^8]

Brendan himself, the *Navigatio* says, reached the Promised Land at last: a
country walled in mist, rich beyond report, through which the company
walked for forty days without finding the far side. A young man met them
there and told them, kindly, to go home — the land would be shown to
Brendan's successors *in the latter days*. The monks returned to Ireland,
and Brendan died soon after, full of years, having seen the place and been
sent back from it.

It is, as the medievalists say, a conventional eschatological ending. I
note only, as the person responsible for this edition, that it is also a
precise description of the itinerary I have come to fear Voss followed: the
report heard late in life; the small boat; the visit from which one is not
returned, or from which one is returned changed; the promise that the place
will be shown to the successors. I was her student. I am, as far as the
estate's solicitor could determine, the successor.

The latter days, I assume, are on their own schedule.
