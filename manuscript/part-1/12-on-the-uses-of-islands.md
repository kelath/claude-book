# 12. On the Uses of Islands

*[Found as six loose leaves at the end of notebook IX, headed in Voss's
hand "for the Atlas — end of the islands." Printed without alteration.
— E.M.]*

I have now spent eleven entries dismantling islands, and before we go
ashore I owe the reader an accounting of what the wreckage is for.

Begin with what we have established, stated together for the first time,
since the entries planted the laws one at a time like navigational marks
and a mark is only useful when you can raise two at once.

First: an error is a promise the map makes before the world is ready to
keep it. The men of Bristol sailed west for a false island and struck a
true continent. The promise was kept — merely not to the men, and not by
the island.

Second: an error is an honest observation that has outlived its weather.
The *Velocity*'s pumice was really there on the Tuesday it was seen. The
chart, which has one tense, embalmed the Tuesday and issued it to
shipping for a hundred and thirty-six years.

Third: stationary phantoms die by sounding; migratory phantoms do not
die. What holds still can be audited. What keeps one horizon ahead of
the audit is never disproved, only decatalogued — a bureaucratic death,
which is the kind you come back from.

And running beneath all three, unnumbered because I cannot yet state it
honestly: the appointments. Phantoms are unkillable in space and mortal
in time. Name an hour — a sounding, a treaty, an eclipse — and the
island must produce itself or die. I distrust this law because it has
an exception, and the exception is the entry this book is missing, and
the reader will have to take on faith, for some chapters yet, that I
know an island that keeps its appointments.

Now the accounting. What is a phantom island *for*? I do not mean what
caused it; causes bore me, and the causes are anyway always the same
five — mirage, pumice, ice, longing, and money. I mean what it is for:
what work it does, that the world keeps making them.

The uses are three, and they ascend.

The low use is navigational, and the Emerald entry states it: an island
is an event, and empty seas are unendurable without events. The mark to
be made or missed, the name in the log, the coin of shared belief
passed hand to hand. Sailors did not believe in these islands the way
theologians believe; they believed the way players believe in the rules
of a game, which is to say completely, and without the least concern
for what is under the board.

The middle use is jurisdictional. An island is a place where writ can
run — a deed, a bond, a baseline, a station. The Admiralty that wrote
"the island is not for stations" and the Company that docketed the same
phrase understood something we have forgotten: that the paper category
comes first and the geology is optional. Sarah Ann was sovereign
American territory for eighty years on the strength of bird dung nobody
saw. Do not laugh at the eighty years. You live inside arrangements
exactly as well founded, and they hold your weight daily.

The high use — and here I must be careful, because this is the hinge of
the Atlas and I have watched better scholars than I slide off it — the
high use is *custodial*. Some of these places are not errors at all.
They are the durable, well-attested, load-bearing marks of something
the charts cannot carry and therefore carry wrongly, the way a ledger
carries a debt it cannot describe as anything but a number. The depth
that repeats. The sign that recurs in archives that never corresponded.
The phrase that surfaces in two languages, sixty years apart, applied
to the same category of place. The singing that ends like a door shut
on company. I have spent fifty years sorting the merely dead islands
from these others, and the sorting is the Atlas, and the others are
few — but they are not none, and one of them has a name I have not yet
written in any entry, and its folder is where my concordance says it
is, and if it is not there when these papers are read, then that too
is a finding.

So much for islands. Now observe what we lose when we go ashore, as
the next Part must, for the interiors have their phantoms too — the
mountain range that walled a continent, the kingdom that answered
letters, the city of gold that ate expeditions. We lose the sounding.
At sea the audit is clean: sail to the position, put down the line,
and the water answers yes or no. But you cannot sound a mountain. A
false range dies only when someone walks through it, and walking
through is slow, and mortal, and the walker can be argued with in a
way the lead-line cannot. Interior phantoms therefore live longer,
lie deeper, and die harder than any island — and when at last they are
walked through and found empty, the space they occupied does not close
over like water. It stays. There are provinces on the modern map that
are still, functionally, the hole left by a phantom, and we have built
real roads around imaginary mountains and the roads are still there.

The sea forgives the chart its errors; it was never listening. The
land remembers. Go ashore expecting the worse behaviour, and you will
not be disappointed.

One thing more. A colleague to whom I showed the Grimsholm file — the
only living person who has read it entire — asked me the question I
have been asked at intervals for fifty years, in tones ranging from
concern to professional malice: *do you believe these places exist?*
I set down here the answer I have arrived at, at the end of the
islands, in the eighty-fourth year of my age, so that it cannot be
softened later by people editing me kindly.

Existence is the wrong verb and it was always the wrong verb. The
chart does not ask whether an island exists; the chart asks whether to
*carry* it. The sea does not ask; the sea is not a party to the
question. Only we ask, and we ask because we have confused the ledger
with the goods. What I believe is this: that the world keeps more
than one set of books; that the charts we sail by are the public set;
and that everything this Part has catalogued — the depths in the band,
the furnished cairn, the sealed orders, the kept hours — is what an
audit looks like when it brushes, without knowing it, against the
private set. I intend to see the private set before I die. I have
been advised that this is arranged by appointment only.

I have an appointment.
