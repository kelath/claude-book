# 2. Sandy Island

**19°13′S, 159°56′E. Coral Sea, between Australia and New Caledonia.**

If Hy-Brasil shows how long an error can live, Sandy Island shows how
recently one has died — and how little the instruments had to do with it.

It was reported in 1876 by the whaling ship *Velocity*, whose master noted a
line of "heavy breakers" and some "sandy islets" along his track through the
Coral Sea. The report was honest, as these things go; the sea there is
scattered with reefs, and a whaler had no reason to invent hazards on a route
he hoped to use again. The Admiralty took the sighting into its charts with
the customary caution — *reported, 1876* — and there the island sat: a long
sliver, twenty-four kilometres by five, like a paring from a fingernail.

Then the copying began, and the copying is the whole story.

The island passed from Admiralty charts into national hydrographic offices,
from hydrographic offices into commercial atlases, from atlases into the
digital gazetteers assembled in the 1980s and 1990s, and from those
gazetteers into the databases that, by the early twenty-first century, had
quietly become the world's memory. The World Vector Shoreline, compiled by
the United States military, carried it. From there it entered the coastline
datasets used by oceanographers. From there, Google Earth, where anyone could
look down on Sandy Island from a simulated orbit and see it plainly: a black
sliver in the blue.

Black, it should be said. Not sand-coloured. The island appeared on the
satellite mosaic as a dark blank, because there was no imagery of land to
paste there — the satellites, which photographed the actual sea, had
photographed actual sea. The database said *island*; the pixels said
*nothing*; and the rendering, forced to reconcile them, produced a shape
filled with the colour of no data. Voss kept a printout of it in notebook
IX, and beneath it, in the shaky post-2015 hand:

    IV: For a decade the whole world could see the third place with its own
    eyes. It looks like this. A hole the shape of a promise. Nobody asked.

In October 2012 the RV *Southern Surveyor*, an Australian research vessel,
was working in the Coral Sea when the scientists aboard noticed the
discrepancy: their charts gave 1,400 metres of water where the world's
databases gave an island. They were scientists; discrepancies are food.
They diverted. Voss's file contains a photocopy of the ship's log for
22 November 2012, and I reproduce her transcription of it here because the
Atlas contains few documents this clean — a place being unmade in real
time, in the standard eight columns:[^5]

> **RV Southern Surveyor, log extract, 22 Nov 2012**
> 0930 — Altered course to N to transect charted position "Sandy Is."
> 1100 — Depth 1300 m. No radar return. No change in swell.
> 1230 — Crossing charted SW extremity. Depth 1380 m.
> 1300 — Vessel at charted centre of island. Depth 1400 m. Open water
> to horizon all bearings. Weather fine, vis. excellent.
> 1330 — Completed transect N end. No shoaling at any point.
> Ship's company mustered on deck to observe. Nil sighted.

*Ship's company mustered on deck to observe. Nil sighted.* Voss underlined
this twice. A crew called up from below to look, together, at nothing — the
log entry does not say whether it was meant as ceremony or as verification,
and it does not matter; at sea the two have never been different things.
Twenty-five people watched an island fail to be there. It is the largest
attendance any event in this Atlas can claim.

The unmaking was announced, the newspapers enjoyed themselves —
"undiscovered," they wrote, a word the language turned out to have been
keeping in a drawer for a hundred years — and the corrections propagated
through the databases within weeks. That, too, Voss noted: the error had
taken 136 years to spread and 26 days to retract.

    IV: Belief moves at the speed of copying. Doubt moves at the speed of
    embarrassment.

What had the *Velocity* seen in 1876? The respectable answer is a pumice
raft — a floating field of volcanic stone, spat from an eruption in the
Tonga arc, which can ride the currents for months in exactly those waters,
looking precisely like low sandy islets with surf on them. The raft drifts,
disperses, sinks. The sighting was true; only the permanence was invented,
and the permanence was invented not by the whaler but by the chart. A map
cannot hold a temporary island. It has one tense.

    IV: The Velocity's master reported honestly on a Tuesday. The chart
    repeated him for 136 years. An error is an honest observation that has
    outlived its weather. — Second law.

This entry ought to end there; it is where Voss's published argument ends,
and it is tidy. But I am obliged to report what else is in the file.

Notebook IX gives Sandy Island four pages. Three concern the pumice raft,
the databases, the log. The fourth page is a list, in two columns, headed
*seen / sounded*. The left column names phantom islands with credible
sighting reports: Sandy, Bermeja, Crocker Land, others this Part will come
to. The right column records the depth of water found at each position when
it was finally surveyed. At the bottom of the page Voss has done something I
do not understand: she has averaged the depths — as if the water over
vanished islands were a dataset — and circled the result, 1,240 metres, and
written beside it:

    IV: Compare M. — displacement consistent. They are not missing. They
    are stowed.[^6]

[^5]: Voss's transcription differs from the published cruise summary in
minor abbreviations only. I have not been able to examine the original log;
the CSIRO archive responded to my inquiry with courtesy and then, to my
follow-up, not at all.

[^6]: "M." is Meridian; the brown-ink cross-references make this certain.
"Stowed" is the word I have thought about most in six years of editing.
One stows a thing deliberately, in a known place, intending retrieval. The
entry for Bermeja (No. 4) will make it harder to dismiss this note than I
would like it to be.
