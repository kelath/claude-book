# 5. Grimsholm

**66°04′N, 12°31′W (Kall chart, 1786). Norwegian Sea, north-east of Iceland.**

Between Iceland and Jan Mayen, where the charts of three whaling nations
overlapped and disagreed for two centuries, the Danish ones carried an
island the others never adopted: Grimsholm, a name that admits it belongs
to somebody — Grim's islet — without preserving who Grim was.

The first report is dated 1653, from the whaler *Enhiörningen* out of
Copenhagen, whose master, Claus Rützow, recorded two days sheltering in the
lee of "an unknown holm, black-sided, with a sound through its middle
running north and south, where we rode out the weather and took on water
from a fall on the eastern shore." The log survives; the position does not
— Rützow's longitude, like every seventeenth-century longitude, is a guess
wearing a number. The Danish Admiralty entered the island with a cautionary
mark. It remained on Danish charts, cautionary mark and all, for one
hundred and forty-eight years.

What distinguishes Grimsholm in the literature — the reason Voss ranks a
minor Norwegian Sea phantom fifth in her index, ahead of far more famous
absences — is that Grimsholm accumulated *interior detail*. Phantom islands
are usually coastline only: a profile sighted, a surf line, a bearing. But
the Grimsholm file thickens. In 1671 the *Sofia Amalia* reported the sound
through the middle and added that its walls were "ruddled," streaked red as
if with ochre. In 1688 a landing party from the whaler *Jægeren* spent, by
their account, six hours ashore: they found the waterfall, a beach of black
sand "warm underfoot," and — the detail the reader of this Part has been
trained to receive calmly — a cairn already standing, with a flat stone at
its base into which was cut a circle divided by a single line.[^11]

    IV: Note what the cairn means. Every visitor to Grimsholm found it
    already visited. There is no first landing in the file. The island
    came furnished.

The eighteenth century domesticated it further. The 1786 chart of Nicolai
Kall gives Grimsholm a settled outline and the coordinate at the head of
this entry. A Bergen manuscript of natural theology, the *Speculum
Boreale*, devotes a chapter to the island's peculiar climate — fogs that
"stand off its shores as men stand from a door" — and records that the
Hanseatic skippers would neither anchor there nor say why. In 1749 the
missionary Hans Egede Saabye the elder is said to have proposed Grimsholm
as a station for the Greenland trade and to have been refused by the
Admiralty in a letter of a single sentence: "The island is not for
stations." Voss quotes the sentence twice in the notebooks, once in each
of her two inks.

    IV: "The island is not for stations." Not *unsuitable*. Not
    *unverified*. The grammar is the grammar of a rule. Somewhere in the
    Admiralty a clerk knew the category, and the category had usage.

The end, when it came, was administrative and total. The survey voyages of
1801 and 1817 crossed the position in good weather and found deep water;
the island was struck from the charts without ceremony; and here Grimsholm
does the thing that earns its rank in the index. Other phantoms linger
after deletion — in gazetteers, in reprints, in the cheap atlases that
copy old plates for decades. Grimsholm does not. After 1820 it is gone
from the record so completely that standard histories of Arctic
cartography do not list it even among the errors. An island with a
century and a half of sightings, landings, soundings, ruddled cliffs, a
warm beach, a furnished cairn — and no afterlife at all. The file ends
with a sounding Voss transcribed from the 1817 survey, taken at Kall's
coordinate: **1,205 metres**.

    IV: Brasil died and left a legend. Kong will die and leave a
    lecture-hall anecdote. Grimsholm died and left nothing, which is not
    how deaths work. Deaths leave estates. Removals leave nothing. The
    completeness of the forgetting is itself the anomaly: the record has
    not decayed, it has been *tidied*. — And the depth is in the band.
    Always now the depth is in the band.

I have delayed my footnote to the end of the entry because it will not be
brief, and because the reader deserves the entry first, as I had it, in
its untroubled state.[^12]

[^11]: The reader will recall the same sign — a circle divided by a line —
reported as marginalia in the La Palma inquiry file (Entry 3, note 7). When
I first cross-checked these I assumed Voss's transcription of one had
contaminated the other. The Jægeren account, however, gives the sign a
context the La Palma drawing lacks: cut into stone, at a cairn, on a shore.
It is the difference between a doodle and an inscription, and it is not a
difference that comforts.

[^12]: Here is the difficulty. I cannot find Grimsholm. Not the island —
the *file*. Rützow's Enhiörningen log: the Danish national archive has the
vessel, the voyage, the master, and no surviving log. The Speculum Boreale:
no such manuscript is catalogued in Bergen, or Copenhagen, or anywhere the
title ought to live; the one scholar of Norwegian natural theology who
answered my letter had not heard of it and asked, reasonably, for my
source. Kall's 1786 chart exists and is digitised: the sheet covering
66°N 12°W shows open sea. The Saabye refusal letter is in no calendar of
Admiralty correspondence. Every other entry in this book I have verified
down to its shelfmarks. For this one there are no shelfmarks — there is
only Voss, citing with her usual precision documents that are not there,
in the confident hand of a woman who never in fifty years of published
work miscited a source. I record the possibilities as I see them. Either
she invented the island, complete with its apparatus, for reasons the
later parts of this book may declare; or she consulted an archive I do not
have access to. I have begun to suspect these are the same possibility
phrased at two different depths.
