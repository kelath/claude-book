# 10. Sarah Ann

**c. 0°35′N, 175°10′W. Central Pacific, north of the equator. Property
of the United States of America, 1858–1937.**

The phantoms of this Part have been believed in, steered by, searched
for, drowned, and shot over. Sarah Ann Island is the only one that was
*owned*.

The instrument of ownership was the Guano Islands Act of 1856, one of
the strangest statutes ever passed by the American Congress: any citizen
discovering an unclaimed island bearing guano — the seabird manure that
was, for two decades, the most sought fertiliser on earth — might file a
bond with the State Department, whereupon the island "may, at the
discretion of the President, be considered as appertaining to the United
States." Under this law American firms claimed dozens of Pacific
specks, some real, some real-ish, and some — Voss's file lists nine —
that no one has ever seen at all. Sarah Ann was claimed in 1858 by the
United States Guano Company of New York, on the strength of a whaler's
report from decades earlier. The paperwork was filed. The bond was
posted. The island entered the Department's schedule of guano islands,
which is to say it entered the sovereignty of a great power, without any
human being having verifiably stood on it, seen it clearly, or fixed it.

    IV: With Sarah Ann the chart is no longer even the senior document.
    The island's best evidence of existence was a *deed*. Here the
    third place acquires what it had lacked since Dulcert: a
    jurisdiction. One could not visit Sarah Ann, but one could, in
    principle, sue over her — and the suit would have been heard. The
    law, unlike the lead-line, never touches bottom.

For eighty years nothing whatever happened, which was the island's
métier. It appeared in the hydrographic lists with the usual drifting
coordinates; the guano boom collapsed; the company dissolved; the claim,
being no trouble to anyone, was never withdrawn. Sarah Ann persisted the
way an entry persists in a ledger no one audits — until, in the 1930s,
she was audited by the least corruptible instrument imaginable: the
moon.

On 8 June 1937 the shadow of a total solar eclipse — one of the longest
of the century, totality over seven minutes — was scheduled to cross the
empty central Pacific. Eclipse astronomy in that era was a hunt for
land: instruments of the period wanted bedrock under them, and the
computed track of totality is indifferent to the convenience of
astronomers. The track for 1937 crossed almost nothing. The planners
went down the schedules island by island, and there, squarely inside
the path of the moon's shadow, the United States found that it owned
one: Sarah Ann. Expeditions were sketched; the Navy was consulted; and
ships went out ahead of the eclipse to establish the observatory — on an
island whose entire documentary existence was a whaler's hearsay and a
guano bond.

They found the Pacific. They searched along the listed position and its
variants — the reports of the search, which Voss collected, have the
methodical melancholy the reader now knows from Weddell and Davis —
and Sarah Ann was quietly struck from the lists that year. The
astronomers observed the eclipse from Canton Island, hundreds of miles
away. The seven minutes of darkness crossed the position of Sarah Ann
on schedule, and no one and nothing watched it pass.

    IV: I ask the reader to sit with the mechanics of the unmaking. For
    eighty years the island was safe: unvisited, unneeded, a deed in a
    drawer. What killed it was an appointment — the one event in nature
    that cannot be rescheduled, missed, or fudged, moving on rails
    computed to the second, requiring the island to *be there at a
    particular time*. Phantoms survive space effortlessly; the chart is
    on their side. What they cannot survive is time. Every phantom
    dies of an appointment. Hy-Brasil's was a sounding, Bermeja's a
    treaty, Sarah Ann's an eclipse — the form varies, but the sentence
    is the same: the world named an hour, and the island did not keep
    it.[^21]

The file closes with an oddity of the kind the reader is now trained to
receive without my assistance. Among the 1937 papers is a clipping from
a Honolulu newspaper of that July — a wire-service squib reporting the
failed search, four sentences, jocular in the period manner. Voss has
kept it for the last sentence, which reads: "The Navy is satisfied that
the island, if it ever existed, has gone to join the fleet of vanished
isles, which sail under sealed orders."[^22]

[^21]: "Every phantom dies of an appointment" is the closest thing in
the notebooks to a fourth law, though Voss never numbers it. I flag it
because the reader will want it at hand in Part III, where the polar
entries concern places that were given appointments and, according to
the files, kept them.

[^22]: I found the original. Honolulu Star-Bulletin, 14 July 1937, page
9. The squib is there, word for word — except the last sentence, which
in the printed original reads simply: "The Navy is satisfied that the
island does not exist." The version in Voss's clipping is typeset
identically to the rest, same face, same measure, and it is not in the
paper. I have stopped reporting these discrepancies to the archives
concerned. The archives are not where the discrepancy lives.
