# 4. Bermeja

**22°33′N, 91°22′W. Gulf of Mexico, north-west of the Yucatán.**

Most phantom islands die owing nothing. Bermeja died owing Mexico several
billion dollars, which is why it is the only island in this Part whose
non-existence was debated in a national senate.

It enters the record early and respectably: *El Yucatán e Islas Adyacentes*,
1539, a Spanish portolan of the new Gulf, shows a small island north-west of
the peninsula and names it Bermeja — "reddish," for the colour a sailor
reported or a copyist supplied, no one knows which. Alonso de Santa Cruz
lists it in 1560 in his *Islario*; Antonio García de Cubas carries it into
the national cartography of independent Mexico in the nineteenth century. It
appears, with its colour and its coordinates, in atlas after atlas for four
hundred and fifty years — small, dutiful, unvisited. There was no reason to
visit it. It was a mark a helmsman steered wide of. Phantom islands survive
longest, Voss observed, in exactly this niche:

    IV: An island avoided is an island preserved. Verification is a
    predator, and the reef-marked corners of a chart are where nothing
    goes hunting.

What changed was oil. In the 1990s, Mexico and the United States sat down to
partition the deep Gulf — the region the negotiators called the *Hoyos de
Dona*, the Doughnut Holes, two lens-shaped gaps of high sea beyond both
nations' two-hundred-mile limits, under which seismic surveys suggested very
large reserves. Two hundred miles from *what* is the entire game in such
negotiations. A Mexican island at 22°33′N — even a rock, even a reef awash —
would push Mexico's baseline north and swing tens of thousands of square
kilometres of oil-bearing seabed into Mexican hands.

Suddenly the small dutiful mark had a job. And so, four and a half centuries
after its first appearance, somebody finally went to look.

The Mexican navy searched in 1997. The published account is brief: the
vessel reached the charted position and found open water. No island; no
shoal; no bottom anomaly at the depths a foundered island might leave. The
treaty was signed in 2000 without Bermeja's help. But the finding sat badly
— an island does not serve four hundred and fifty years and then decline to
exist at the exact moment its existence would be worth billions — and in
2008 the Mexican Senate, prodded by legislators who said as much aloud,
commissioned the National Autonomous University to settle it. The 2009 UNAM
survey was thorough: multibeam sonar, magnetometry, aerial reconnaissance,
archival review, a search box generous enough to catch any plausible
misplotting. The report's conclusion has the special flatness of scientists
who know their audience wanted a different sentence: no island exists, and
no evidence suggests one ever did.[^9]

Under the charted position the survey found rolling abyssal sediment. Voss,
who obtained the bathymetry, noted the sounded depth at the coordinate
itself and copied it onto an index card in the Bermeja folder: **1,190
metres**. The reader who recalls the *seen/sounded* list from the Sandy
Island entry — the averaged depth of water over vanished islands, 1,240
metres, circled — will understand why I sat for some time with that card in
my hand. On its reverse, in the late shaky script, underlined three times:

    IV: The displacement is the signature.

The senators, for their part, reached for a different explanation, and it is
the one Bermeja is famous for: the island had existed, and the Americans had
removed it. The CIA was proposed; dynamite was proposed; one deputy
suggested, apparently in earnest, that rising seas had been assisted. The
theory had everything a theory needs except a mechanism — islands are not
removed; the seabed remembers a demolished island the way a jaw remembers a
pulled tooth, and the sonar showed no socket. Voss gave the conspiracy a
single paragraph and a verdict she clearly enjoyed writing:

    IV: The senators are wrong in every particular and right in the only
    respect that matters. They have understood that the disappearance of
    Bermeja is an *event with interests attached*, not a clerical
    correction. Their error is doctrinal: they believe removal requires an
    agency with boats. The chart-makers put the island there without ever
    touching the water. Why assume the water was needed to take it away?

I have read that paragraph many times, and I want to record the stages,
because I suspect they are the stages the reader will pass through too.
First one takes it as wit. Then one notices it is not structured as wit; it
is structured as an argument, with a premise and a rebuttal and a standing
conclusion. Then one notices the conclusion: that placement and removal are
operations of the same kind, performed in the same medium — performed, that
is, in the third place, where Voss believed errors live — and that the
Bermeja file is not in her cabinet of curiosities at all. It is in her
physics.

    IV: Four hundred and fifty years is too long for a copying error and
    too short for an island. Bermeja's tenure is exactly the tenure of a
    *deposit*. Cf. the list. The depths agree within noise. What is placed
    in the third place is placed at a depth, and the depth is
    conserved.[^10]

There is one more item in the folder, and I give it without comment, because
my comments have begun to frighten me. It is a letter, the forty-third in
the T. correspondence, postmarked Veracruz, March 2010 — five months after
the UNAM report. It reads, in full:

*"They looked properly this time, so it has gone properly. You asked once
what the red colour was. Rust, Iris. Things kept in water rust. — T."*
