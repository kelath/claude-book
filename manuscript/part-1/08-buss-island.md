# 8. Buss Island

**c. 57°N, 28°W. North Atlantic, south-east of Greenland. Later: beneath it.**

Every other island in this Part was eventually declared never to have
existed. Buss Island alone was granted a death certificate. When the sea
where it stood proved empty, the chart-makers did not erase it; they
drowned it. For a century the charts of the North Atlantic carried, in the
space where the island had failed, the inscription *The Sunken Land of
Buss* — the only geological event in history to be invented retroactively
to save an error's reputation.

It began, like much North Atlantic apocrypha, with the Frobisher voyages.
In September 1578, the fleet straggling home from the third expedition,
the *Emmanuel* — a busse of Bridgwater, "busse" being the herring-boat
build that gave the island its unlovely name — reported passing a new
land south-east of Frisland: two days along it, "seeming fruitful, full
of woods, and a champaign country." That Frisland itself was a phantom
(it has its own literature, and Voss's index sends the reader there with
a weary *q.v.*) troubled nobody: one imaginary island was seen from
another, an arrangement the sixteenth century found unremarkable and
which Voss, characteristically, found the most instructive fact in the
file.

    IV: Phantoms sight phantoms. The Emmanuel's landfall is bearing-true
    from a place that is not there. Errors are not points; they are
    *networks*, and they survey one another with perfect internal
    consistency. A chart is a conspiracy of witnesses who have never
    met.

The island took root. Seventeenth-century charts gave it shape and
extent; in 1671 a Captain Thomas Shepard claimed not only to have found
Buss but to have sailed around it, and produced a map — harbours named,
capes christened, an anchorage recommended — precise enough that the
Hudson's Bay Company briefly considered how the island might serve its
trade. Shepard's map is the high-water mark of Buss: a complete
cartographic biography of nothing, drawn from nothing, adopted by the
most hard-headed commercial enterprise of its age.

    IV: Note the sequence, because it is the standard sequence run in
    reverse. Usually a place is seen vaguely and mapped vaguely and the
    detail accretes. Buss was seen vaguely and mapped *precisely*,
    at once, by a man who needed it findable. Precision is not evidence.
    Precision is appetite.

Then the failures began — ships crossing the position in daylight and
finding water — and with them the remarkable adjustment. The island was
not struck. Instead, the eighteenth-century charts began to explain: the
land had *sunk*. Van Keulen's Dutch atlas of 1745 states it as plain
fact: "the submerged land of Buss, nowadays nothing but surf and
shoaling water." Mariners reported discoloured sea, breakers, a shoal
with bottom at a few dozen fathoms — the corpse of the island, decently
interred just below the surface, still capable of wrecking you. The
Sunken Land of Buss remained a charted hazard into the nineteenth
century. In 1818 John Ross, outbound for Baffin Bay, paused over the
position and put down a sounding line: one hundred and eighty fathoms,
no bottom. He tried again further on. Deep water everywhere. The sunken
land was not sunken; it was absent, which the charts had never been
willing to say.

    IV: Here the record shows its hand. Confronted with an empty
    position, the chart did not amend itself; it amended the *world* —
    invented a catastrophe, sank a landmass, planted surf on the grave —
    rather than write the true sentence, "we were wrong." An error will
    spend any amount of geology to avoid an erratum. And observe what
    the amendment purchased: a century of soundings. Ships that would
    never have stopped for open water stopped for a drowned island and
    put lines down over it, attending to the position, keeping it
    *visited*. The drowning was not an exit story. It was a maintenance
    contract.[^17]

The modern explanation is serviceable: Frisland's ghost geography
displaced the *Emmanuel*'s honest sighting of some real coast —
Greenland's, likely — and the shoal reports were the usual North
Atlantic repertoire of whale-backs, ice-blink, and wishful lead-lines.
The file could close there, and in every archive but one, it does.

The exception is the reason Buss stands eighth in Voss's index. Among
her papers is a photostat of a Hudson's Bay Company memorandum of 1675,
declining, finally, to pursue Shepard's island. It gives no reason.
But some clerk — the hand is contemporary — has docketed the decision
in the margin, and the docket reads: *not for stations*.[^18]

[^17]: The reader will hear in "maintenance contract" a rhyme with the
Bermeja entry's talk of deposits, and may reasonably ask whether Voss is
building a doctrine across these files. She is. I have chosen to let it
assemble at her pace rather than summarise it ahead of the evidence,
because summarised ahead of the evidence it sounds like what it perhaps
is, and there are four more entries in this Part.

[^18]: Entry 5, note 12, records my failure to find the Grimsholm
documents; I will not rehearse the feeling here. The HBC archive in
Winnipeg is superbly catalogued. The memorandum exists. The marginal
docket exists. I have held the photostat against the window and against
better judgement, and the hand of the docket is seventeenth-century, and
the phrase is the Danish Admiralty's phrase, sixty years early and an
ocean away. Institutions do not share marginalia. Categories do.
