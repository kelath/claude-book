# 11. Isla del Coro

**15°50′N, 76°20′W. Caribbean Sea, on the old pearl route between
Jamaica and the Main.**

Every island in this Part until now was seen. This one was heard.

The first report is from 1528, in the deposition of the three survivors
of the *Santa Engracia*, a pearl carrack lost on the run from Cubagua.
Adrift in the ship's boat, becalmed in fog before dawn, they heard —
all three deposed to it separately, and the depositions agree in the
particulars — the sound of a choir. Not one voice; many, "well ordered,"
singing what the eldest survivor, who had been a sacristan, identified
as the office of matins. The singing continued, he said, the length of
the office. They rowed toward it, and the fog lightened, and they made
out a low island, dark, without surf, "at no great distance." Then the
sun took the fog and the island with it, and three days later they were
picked up by a coaster out of Santiago whose master recorded their
account with the dry notation: *others also have heard this choir*.

Others had. The rutters of the pearl route — the working sailing
directions, copied hand to hand among pilots, closer to the water than
any chart — carry the island for a century and a half under the name
Isla del Coro, the Island of the Choir, with the most practical
instruction in the literature of phantom places: *she is not seen but
in thick weather, and is known by the singing; the prudent will alter
course upon hearing it, and not toward.*

    IV: The rutter does not ask what the singing is. The rutter is a
    genre without curiosity; it wants you alive, not informed. But note
    what the instruction concedes: that the island's sensible sign — the
    only sign by which she is "known" — arrives by ear, in fog, at the
    hours of the night offices. A place whose entire interface with the
    world is a sound. The pilots mapped an acoustics.

She was charted, small and tentative, by the Seville school in the
1580s; she appears on Dutch charts of the Main as I. del Coro or I. de
Choro through the seventeenth century, sometimes with the annotation
*duyster* — dark, obscure. No landing is recorded anywhere in the file.
No one ever brought back so much as a branch. The island produced
nothing but testimony, and the testimony is strangely uniform across
three centuries: fog, stillness, the ordered voices, the low dark
shore, the dissolution by daylight. A French privateer's journal of
1697 adds the single variant detail in the whole dossier: that as his
ship stood away — his crew "much afraid, though the singing was not
terrible but sweet" — the voices did not fade with distance as sound
fades over water, but ended all at once, "as when a door is shut upon
a room where there is company."

    IV: As when a door is shut. The privateer's image is exact and he
    did not know what he was being exact about. Sound does not carry
    from the third place; it *leaks*, when something is open that is
    ordinarily closed. The fog is not concealment. The fog is the gap
    under the door.

The island's death was the quietest in the Atlas. No search, no
sounding, no eclipse, no senate. The pearl fisheries failed; the route
moved north; the rutters stopped being copied because the pilots who
needed them stopped existing; and Isla del Coro, which had never been
anything but a sound in the mouths of working men, went out of the
world when the last of them did. The nineteenth-century hydrographic
offices, sweeping the Caribbean sheets for doubtful marks, removed her
without discussion — there is no minute, no E.D. period, no obituary.
The reader who consults the modern chart will find the position clean,
and the water beneath it, for what it is now worth to anyone, twelve
hundred and forty metres deep.

Voss's entry ends with a passage that I give as it stands in the
notebook, because it is the only place in the papers where she writes
about the island in the first person, and because the reader will
understand, by the end of this book, why I have chosen to let it stand
without comment.

    IV: I heard it once. October of 1981, from the deck of a fruit
    boat, in fog, in the right latitudes, at four in the morning. I had
    arranged to be there; T. had told me the conditions; I had waited
    through nine nights of clear weather for the fog. It was matins. It
    was well ordered. It was, as the privateer says, sweet, and it
    ended as he says it ended, all at once, and the fog burned off by
    seven and there was nothing to the horizon anywhere. I record what
    I concluded, standing at the rail with the sun coming up on empty
    water: that the island was not gone. That none of them are gone.
    That what we struck from the charts we did not strike from the
    world; we only stopped answering the door. The singing goes on
    behind it at the appointed hours, whether or not anyone is in the
    fog to hear — the office does not require a congregation. It
    requires only that the hours be kept. Somewhere the hours are being
    kept, Elena. The Atlas is my attempt to find out where, and the
    finding out has taken my life, and it was a life well spent.
