# 6. Crocker Land

**c. 83°N, 100°W. Arctic Ocean, north-west of Axel Heiberg Island.**

In June 1906, from the summit of Cape Thomas Hubbard at the top of Axel
Heiberg Island, Robert Peary looked north-west across the frozen polar sea
and saw, by his later account, "the faint white summits of a distant land."
He named it Crocker Land, for George Crocker of San Francisco, who had given
his last expedition fifty thousand dollars.

That is the entire observational basis of Crocker Land: one man, one
afternoon, one donor in need of flattering. It is the thinnest sighting
report in this Part, and Voss's file opens with the reason it is thinner
still: Peary's diary. The diary survives, and the entries for the days at
Cape Thomas Hubbard record the view, the cairn he built, the tin he left —
and no land. No white summits. The first mention of Crocker Land occurs in
the book Peary published the following year, inserted into a landscape his
own field notes had left empty.

    IV: The other phantoms in this Part were transmission errors. Crocker
    Land was composed. It is the control specimen — the island we know
    was made of motive rather than mist — and the reader should hold on
    to what happens next, because what happens next is that it behaves
    exactly like the others.

What happens next is that America believed him. Peary was the pole-winner,
or claimed to be; his geography was national property; and a new landmass
the size of a rumor, sitting in the last unmapped quarter of the Arctic,
was not going to sit there unclaimed. In July 1913 the Crocker Land
Expedition sailed from Brooklyn — seven men under Donald MacMillan, backed
by the American Museum of Natural History, provisioned for two years, sent
to survey a place that existed in one paragraph of a fundraising memoir.

MacMillan's party reached northwest Greenland, wintered, and in the spring
of 1914 crossed the sea ice toward Peary's coordinates. And here the Arctic
did the cruelest thing available to it. It showed them Crocker Land.

On 21 April, from the ice north-west of Cape Thomas Hubbard, the party saw
land filling the horizon — "hills, valleys, snow-capped peaks," MacMillan
wrote, "extending through at least one hundred and twenty degrees of the
horizon." Fitzhugh Green swore to it. The party's Inughuit guide,
Piugaattoq, a man with twenty years' knowledge of that ice, looked at the
hills and valleys and said it was *pujoq* — mist. He was overruled. They
marched toward it for five days, nearly fifty kilometres over active sea
ice, and the land stood always ahead of them, detailed and patient, until
the evening the sun dropped and it went out like a lamp. MacMillan's
journal for that day contains the flattest sentence in Arctic literature:
"We were convinced we were in pursuit of a will-o'-the-wisp, ever receding,
ever changing, ever beckoning."

    IV: A Fata Morgana: cold dense air lying under warm, a duct in the
    atmosphere, and light from beyond the horizon guided along it and
    stood upright in stacked, wavering strata. The physics is not in
    dispute and I do not dispute it. I note only its manners. The
    superior mirage does not show you abstractions. It shows you
    *terrain* — hills, valleys, headlands — assembled from ice you cannot
    otherwise see. It is the sea showing you a place from somewhere else
    and letting you decide whose.

The retreat is where the entry stops being about optics. The party turned
back barely ahead of the spring break-up; the ice they had crossed was
opening behind them. In the weeks that followed, Green and Piugaattoq,
travelling as a pair to survey the coast, were caught by storm; the dogs
began to die; and in circumstances that have never been honestly excavated
— Green's own account is the only account — Fitzhugh Green shot Piugaattoq
and took his team. He recorded it in his diary with a brevity that has
appalled every reader since: *"I shot once in the air. He did not stop. I
then killed him with a shot through the shoulder and another through the
head."* No inquiry was ever held. The Museum was informed and elected not
to understand. Green retired, eventually, an admiral.

    IV: Every phantom in this Atlas has a cost column. Usually it is
    money, ships' time, a treaty line. Crocker Land is the entry where
    the column is a man — and the man it took was the one member of the
    party who had correctly identified the phantom on sight. I have
    turned this fact over for forty years and it has no comfortable
    position. The mirage did not kill Piugaattoq. But the expedition was
    the mirage's body, walking; it was motive dressed as geography from
    the day Peary needed a landmass to thank a banker with; and when it
    was contradicted, it behaved as motive behaves.[^13]

The expedition itself, planned for two years, was trapped for four — relief
ships crushed or turned back by ice, the party wintering on at Etah while
the war swallowed the world's attention. They came home in 1917 to a
country that had stopped caring about the Arctic. Aerial surveys in the
1930s closed the question: there is no land north-west of Axel Heiberg;
there is the Arctic Ocean, two thousand metres deep, and ice.

Voss's entry ends with the diary — Peary's, the one with no land in it.
She examined it at the National Archives in 1983, and her note is
uncharacteristically quiet:

    IV: The pages for June 1906 are clean. He saw nothing and wrote
    nothing, and the nothing is right there, holding its shape, the way
    water holds its shape over the position of an island. Composed or
    copied, flattery or fog — the Atlas does not actually care how a
    place gets in. Neither, I have come to believe, does the place.[^14]

[^13]: Piugaattoq's name is variously transliterated in the expedition
records and barely appears in the Museum's official publications. His
descendants were still in Qaanaaq when the archives were finally opened to
scholars. The fullest accounting is in the modern literature on the
expedition, to which I refer the reader; this book's concern is the
phantom, but I decline to let the phantom have the last word on him.

[^14]: One item in the Crocker Land folder is out of place, and I record
it here for completeness. It is a bearing — "CTH 337° true" — in Voss's
brown ink, on the back of her 1983 archive reader's ticket. Cape Thomas
Hubbard to Peary's reported summits is approximately 337 degrees true.
The brown ink went out of use around 1980. I have checked with the
National Archives: her 1983 visit was her only visit. I have no theory,
and I am observing the discipline, which this Part has taught me, of
saying so plainly.
