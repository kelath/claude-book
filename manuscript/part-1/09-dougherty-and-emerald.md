# 9. Dougherty & Emerald

**Dougherty: 59°20′S, 120°20′W. Emerald: 57°15′S, 162°12′E.
The far southern ocean, which is to say: nowhere, twice.**

The southern ocean below the fiftieth parallel is the emptiest surface on
the planet. Between New Zealand and Cape Horn a ship in the old days might
run seven thousand miles of easting and raise nothing — no sail, no land,
no gull that meant land — only the grey procession of the swells coming
round the world unobstructed. Voss gives the two phantoms of this entry a
shared file, and opens it with the observation that explains the pairing:

    IV: Elsewhere phantom islands are errors of perception. Below fifty
    south they are errors of *need*. The eye is a social organ; it was
    not built for a week of horizon; and what it cannot find it will
    begin, out of courtesy, to provide. The emptiest sea on earth is
    precisely where the charts grew islands, and this is not a paradox
    but a diagnosis.

**Dougherty** was reported in 1841 by the captain of the English whaler
*James Stewart*, who described an island five or six miles long, high at
the north-east end, covered with snow, in the desolate longitudes
midway between New Zealand and the Horn. It was a good, sober report from
a competent master, and it did not stand alone: Captain Keates of the
*Louise* confirmed the island in 1860 — passed within a few miles, noted
the high bluff — and Captain Stannard of the *Thurso* saw it again in
1886. Three sightings, forty-five years, one position. Dougherty Island
entered the Admiralty charts and the sailing directions; it was, for two
generations, the only mark on the emptiest sheet in the folio.

Its unmaking was a career. Captain John King Davis — later the great
navigator of the Antarctic expeditions, then master of the little
*Nimrod* returning from Shackleton's first venture — went out of his way
in 1909 to settle Dougherty, and did it with a thoroughness Voss's file
lays out sounding by sounding: he ran the *Nimrod* along the reported
latitude through the position and eighty miles beyond, in weather clear
enough to see a fulmar at five miles, and took deep-sea soundings as he
went. Over the island's charted heart the line paid out and paid out and
found bottom at 2,318 fathoms. Davis wrote afterwards, with a navigator's
tact, that he considered the island "to have been an iceberg" — three
icebergs, presumably, on station across half a century.

    IV: Davis's sounding is the deepest water in this Part — 4,240
    metres, if the reader is keeping the column I know the reader is
    keeping. It is far outside the band. I record this against myself,
    and I am more grateful for it than for any figure in the Atlas: a
    doctrine that cannot find a hostile number is not a doctrine but a
    mood. Dougherty is my control. Whatever was banked in the shallow
    band, nothing was banked here. Some islands are only lonely eyes.[^19]

**Emerald** is the older and stranger sister. Reported in 1821 by Captain
Nockells of the British ship *Emerald*, south of Macquarie Island: a
high, bold land, mountainous, its peaks estimated at several thousand
feet. It went on the charts and stayed for a century, accumulating the
usual biography of failed appointments — ships passing the position in
clear weather, finding water, logging the absence, and the island
persisting anyway, protected by the excellent alibi of southern-ocean
fog. It was searched for deliberately at least three times. In 1840 the
United States Exploring Expedition under Wilkes passed over it. In 1909
the same relentless Davis crossed the position — water. As late as 1949 a
New Zealand naval vessel made a sweep; the Emerald Island of the charts
finally came off them in the age of the echo-sounder, having outlived
its reporter by more than a hundred years.

What preserved it so long, Voss argues, was not the fog but the
*usefulness*. Emerald sat athwart the route from Australia toward the
ice, and for the whole heroic age it served as a mark to be "made" or
"missed" — a thing to steer for, a thing to name in the log, an event in
seas that otherwise offered none. Mawson's men looked for it going
south; the log of the *Aurora* (the Australasian expedition's ship, no
relation to the islands of Entry 7, though Voss circled the coincidence
of the name twice) records the search as a break in the monotony,
half-scientific, half a game. Nobody depended on Emerald. Everybody
was slightly better off with it there.

    IV: The economists have a word for a good like this — one whose
    value is produced entirely by shared belief in it — and the word is
    "currency." Emerald was small change for the loneliest sea:
    passed hand to hand, log to log, never redeemable, endlessly
    negotiable. You cannot land on it, but you can *miss* it, and
    missing it is an experience, and experience is what the fiftieth
    parallel is starving of. The southern ocean kept a coin in
    circulation for 128 years because the alternative was that nothing
    should ever happen at all.[^20]

The diptych closes the ledger of honest phantoms. What remains of Part I
is stranger: an island the United States annexed sight unseen; an island
I can no longer discuss in footnotes; and Voss's own summing-up. The
reader who has been keeping accounts — of depths, of phrases, of inks —
is advised to bring them forward. They are about to be audited.

[^19]: This is the first time in the papers that Voss volunteers evidence
against the depth pattern, and the reader should know that the file
supports her: Davis's sounding is genuine and genuinely deep. I have
verified it in his published narrative. I note — as observation, not
rebuttal, since she made the observation first — that she did not remove
Dougherty from the Atlas for failing the pattern. A control belongs to
the experiment.

[^20]: The word "currency" is underlined in the manuscript, and in the
margin beside it, in the late hand, Voss has written: "then what is the
bank." The question mark is absent in the original and I have not
supplied one.
