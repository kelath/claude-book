# 9. Dougherty & Emerald

**Dougherty: 59°20′S, 120°20′W. Emerald: 57°15′S, 162°12′E.
The far southern ocean, which is to say: nowhere, twice.**

The southern ocean below the fiftieth parallel is the emptiest
surface on the planet — seven thousand miles of easting, in the old
days, without a sail, a coast, or a gull that meant land. Voss gives
its two phantoms a shared file, and opens it with the observation
that explains the pairing:

    IV: Elsewhere phantom islands are errors of perception. Below
    fifty south they are errors of *need*. The eye is a social organ;
    it was not built for a week of horizon; and what it cannot find
    it will begin, out of courtesy, to provide.

**Dougherty** was reported in 1841 by the master of the whaler
*James Stewart* — five or six miles long, high at the north-east
end, snow-covered — and confirmed twice across the next forty-five
years by masters with no acquaintance and nothing to gain. Three
sober sightings, one position: it entered the Admiralty charts as
the only mark on the emptiest sheet in the folio. Its unmaking was a
career. In 1909 John King Davis, homeward with Shackleton's *Nimrod*,
went out of his way to settle it: he ran the reported latitude
through the position and eighty miles beyond, in weather clear
enough to see a fulmar at five miles, and over the island's charted
heart his deep-sea line paid out and found bottom at 2,318 fathoms.
He allowed afterwards that the island had been an iceberg — three
icebergs, presumably, on station across half a century.

    IV: Davis's sounding is the deepest water in this Part — 4,240
    metres, far outside the band. I record this against myself, and
    I am more grateful for it than for any figure in the Atlas: a
    doctrine that cannot find a hostile number is not a doctrine but
    a mood. Dougherty is my control. Whatever was banked in the
    shallow band, nothing was banked here. Some islands are only
    lonely eyes.[^19]

**Emerald**, the older sister, was reported in 1821 south of
Macquarie Island — high, bold, mountainous — and stayed on the
charts for a hundred and twenty-eight years, surviving Wilkes in
1840, Davis again in 1909, and a New Zealand naval sweep as late as
1949. What preserved it, Voss argues, was not the fog but the
usefulness: it sat athwart the route toward the ice, a mark to be
"made" or "missed," an event in seas that otherwise offered none.
Mawson's men looked for it going south, half science, half a game.
Nobody depended on Emerald. Everybody was slightly better off with
it there.

    IV: The economists have a word for a good whose value is
    produced entirely by shared belief in it, and the word is
    "currency." Emerald was small change for the loneliest sea:
    passed log to log, never redeemable, endlessly negotiable. The
    southern ocean kept a coin in circulation for 128 years because
    the alternative was that nothing should ever happen at all.[^20]

The diptych closes the ledger of honest phantoms. What remains of
Part I is stranger: an island the United States annexed sight
unseen; an island I can no longer discuss in footnotes; and Voss's
own summing-up. The reader who has been keeping accounts — of
depths, of phrases, of inks — is advised to bring them forward.
They are about to be audited.

[^19]: This is the first time in the papers that Voss volunteers
evidence against the depth pattern, and the file supports her:
Davis's sounding is genuine and genuinely deep. I note — as
observation, not rebuttal, since she made the observation first —
that she did not remove Dougherty from the Atlas for failing the
pattern. A control belongs to the experiment.

[^20]: The word "currency" is underlined in the manuscript, and in
the margin beside it, in the late hand, Voss has written: "then
what is the bank." The question mark is absent in the original and
I have not supplied one.
