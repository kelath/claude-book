# 1. Hy-Brasil

**51°N, 17°W (as first charted). Later 51°10′N, 15°50′W. Later elsewhere.**

Begin with the most patient of them.

Hy-Brasil enters the record in 1325, on the portolan chart of Angelino
Dalorto, a Genoese working in Majorca, who set down a neat disc of land west
of Ireland and labelled it *Bracile*. He gave it no legend and no
explanation. He did not need to. Everyone knew it was there.

It was still there in 1339, on the chart of Angelino Dulcert, who may have
been the same man under a different spelling of himself — cartographers, as
this Atlas will have many occasions to observe, are not always fixed points.
It was there in 1375 on the great Catalan Atlas, the finest map of its
century. It was there in 1436, in 1480, in 1513. It survived the discovery of
the actual Americas without embarrassment, as an elderly relative survives a
scandal. It was on English Admiralty charts in the eighteenth century. It was
last printed as real in 1865, on a chart issued to mariners, five hundred and
forty years after Dalorto — a career longer than most empires can manage, and
conducted entirely without the inconvenience of existing.

The island was round, or nearly round, and divided by a channel or river
running east to west. This is remarkably consistent across five centuries of
charts drawn by men who had never seen it and could not have seen it, and the
consistency is the first thing Voss asks us to notice. Errors are supposed to
degrade. Copy a manuscript enough times and the words drift; whisper a
sentence around a room and it comes back changed. Hy-Brasil was copied for
half a millennium and came back the same: round, cleft by water, west of
Ireland, slightly out of reach. Whatever process was transmitting it had
error-correction.

    IV: The Church transmitted the shape of the Trinity with less fidelity
    than the chart-makers transmitted the shape of Brasil. One concludes
    that the chart-makers believed in it more.

The name is not Brazilian; the nation is a coincidence, or the coincidence
runs the other direction, through the red dye-wood *brasile* that gave the
country its name. The older etymology is Irish: *Uí Breasail*, the clan of
Breasal, the Isle of the Blessed — a land that in the folklore of the Aran
Islands rises from the sea once every seven years, visible from the cliffs,
and cannot be reached, because it is only visible. To see it was common. To
land on it was the trick.

People tried. This is the part of the record Voss loved, and her file on the
expeditions is the thickest in notebook II. In 1480, John Jay the younger of
Bristol sent a ship west to find Brasil; it wandered nine weeks and came home
empty. Bristol sent more ships in 1481, and there is a document suggesting
these searches went on annually, quietly, for years — indeed a Spanish envoy
wrote to Columbus, after the fact, that the men of Bristol had been looking
for something out there all along. Voss noted the direction of these
voyages and set down one of her flat, disorienting sentences:

    IV: They sailed west looking for an island that did not exist and found,
    eventually, a continent that did. The error was load-bearing. This is
    the first law of the Atlas: an error is a promise the map makes before
    the world is ready to keep it.

In 1674 a Captain John Nisbet of Killybegs claimed not merely to have seen
Brasil but to have landed: his men, he reported, spent a day on the island
and were given gold and silver by an old gentleman who lived there with
enormous black rabbits. A follow-up expedition under a Captain Oliver
confirmed everything except the rabbits, which should have been the easy
part. The account is a fabrication — it can be traced to a pamphleteer, and
Voss traced it — but she kept it in the file anyway, with a note that
fabrications about a place are still *about the place*, and that the island
was by then sufficiently real to be lied about, which is a kind of citizenship.

The last serious observation belongs to Roderick O'Flaherty, the Galway
historian, who in 1684 interviewed a man named Morrough O'Ley. O'Ley claimed
he had been carried to O'Brasil in a trance and returned with the gift of
medicine — he practised for years afterward, unlicensed and, by the accounts,
competently. Voss's marginal note here is the first appearance in the papers
of a phrase that will trouble this edition considerably:

    IV: O'Ley returned with something. The question is never whether the
    place exists. The question is what the traffic consists of. — cf.
    Meridian, folder VII.[^3]

By the nineteenth century the island was dying in the only way phantom
islands die: not disproved but *demoted*. The 1830 charts mark "Brasil Rock
(doubtful)." The 1865 charts mark "Brazil Rock (E.D.)" — existence doubtful —
which is the hydrographer's way of saying *we are no longer willing to be
blamed for this*. Then the position was searched, and sounded, and there was
water where the promise had been, three thousand metres of it, and the
Admiralty removed the name. There is no rock. There is a seamount in the
general region, the Porcupine Bank, whose summit lies two hundred metres
below the surface, and those who need Hy-Brasil to have been *something*
point to it the way one points to a swirl of dressing-gown on a chair the
morning after a ghost.

Voss did not need it to have been something. Her interest was in the five
hundred and forty years — in the durability of the disc-shaped island as an
object of transmission. The entry in her final index reads simply:
*Hy-Brasil. Type specimen.* As a naturalist chooses one preserved skin to
define a species, she chose Hy-Brasil to define the phantom island: born of
report, stabilised by copying, defended by hope, killed by sounding. Every
entry that follows in Part I is a variation on that life cycle, and the
variations, she believed, were where the truth was hiding.

She visited the coordinates once, in 1988, on a research vessel out of
Galway, and recorded the visit in notebook II with uncharacteristic brevity:

    IV: Calm. Overcast. 51°N 17°W at 1400 hrs. Nothing, as expected.
    Nothing has a look to it, though. One learns the look.

I have stood on the cliffs of Aran, where the island was seen once every
seven years. I went in the winter of 2021, the second year of my editing,
when I still believed I was assembling a book about the history of
cartography.[^4] The horizon from those cliffs is a long, hard line, and the
eye works it the way a tongue works a broken tooth. I saw nothing. But I
understood, standing there, how seeing would go: the particular low cloud,
the particular light, the readiness. The islanders did not think Hy-Brasil
was always there. They thought it was *sometimes* there. Of all the beliefs
in this book, I have come to regard that one as the most precise.

[^3]: This is the earliest reference to Meridian in the notebooks by date of
composition (the note is in the brown ink Voss used before 1980). Folder VII
is the missing folder. I record its absence here once and will not keep
mentioning it; the reader should assume it is absent behind every entry, the
way the sea is absent behind a chart.

[^4]: Travel for this edition was supported by no grant, sponsor, or
institution, a fact I mention not as a complaint but as a disclosure: every

the Coral Sea, then Dutch Harbor — is tracking the progress of a condition.
Voss's condition, I would have called it once. The papers came with it, the
way a house comes with its damp.
