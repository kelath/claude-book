# Interleaved: The Coat

*[I interrupt the entries once in this Part, and I have chosen to do
it here, after Piugaattoq, because the reader has just watched what
this subject costs when the cost is charged to strangers, and is
owed a note on what it charged nearer home. — E.M.]*

Among the effects the estate released to me in the second year was
the coat — the one from the transom rail, folded, salt-marked at the
hem, a good grey wool coat resoled at the cuffs the way she mended
everything, competently and without grace. The solicitor's clerk
handed it over in a paper parcel with a printed inventory line —
*outer garment, one* — and I carried it home on my lap on the train
like a sleeping animal, and it has hung on the back of my study door
for four years, and I have never worn it, and I have never once
worked with the door closed, so that I do not have to look at it.
I record this because the reader deserves to know early what kind
of editor they have: not a neutral instrument. A person keeping a
dead woman's coat where she can almost see it.

Here is what the entries will not tell you about Iris Voss, and
what I knew of her before I knew any of this.

She taught badly and marvellously. Badly, because she had no
patience for the middle of any explanation — she gave you the first
step and the last and watched to see if you could build the bridge,
and most could not, and she wrote them fair references and forgot
their names. Marvellously, because if you built the bridge she
would look at you once, level, the way a surveyor looks at a
benchmark, and from then on she taught you everything, for years,
long past any obligation — Tuesday suppers in her rooms, one dish,
always the same dish, a fish soup she had learned in Bergen and
made without apology for thirty years, and over it she would take
your week's work apart seam by seam and hand you back the pieces
in better order. I loved her the way you love a cold climate you
have chosen: entirely, and without expecting it to warm.

She had been engaged once. I know because she told me exactly one
time, over the soup, in the flattest voice I ever heard her use:
a hydrographer, Norwegian, 1959; he wanted her to come aboard the
survey service as his assistant, and she had already begun the
card file; and she chose the cards. "He was a good man," she said.
"He married a good woman. I have seen every chart he ever signed."
That last sentence, I understood much later, was the love letter.
She kept up with his charts for forty years the way other people
keep up with grandchildren. When he died — 2004; I looked it up —
she flew to Trondheim for the funeral of a man she had not spoken
to since Suez, and sat at the back, and signed the condolence book
clearly, and flew home. There was a sister, too, in Stavanger, who
sent Christmas cards addressed to *Professor* Voss with the title
underlined twice, which Voss took for mockery and I would have
taken for pride; they had not met in twenty years when the boat
went out. The college found her difficult, which colleges say of
women who decline committees; the porters loved her because she
knew their children's names; she knew nobody's field but her own
and asked after everybody's; she was, in sum, what the trade makes
of its best people — a lighthouse: brilliant, fixed, indispensable
to traffic, and lived in by one keeper, on a schedule no one else
would accept.

And since this note is a ledger of costs, mine goes in it too,
once, and then the apparatus will close over it and we will speak
of it no more. When this edition began there was a person in my
house who walked the fen road with me on Sundays, and had opinions
about which of the college's bridges was best in fog, and wanted,
reasonably, the ordinary things — the shared calendar, the summer
that is actually taken, the door of the study closed at seven.
The papers arrived in the third year of us. R. was patient for
two more — patient with Bergen, with the Coral Sea, with the
night I came back from Aran and could not say what I had been
doing in Ireland in February because I did not know how to say
*checking* — and then one Sunday on the fen road R. asked me,
without anger, the question I had been dreading not because it
was unfair but because I had the answer ready: "If the folder
turned up tomorrow, and it was between me and reading it —"
and I said nothing for four steps. Four steps is an answer.
R. moved out in the spring of 2023 and married someone with a
closable study in 2025, and sent me, because R. is a better
person than this book deserves, a card of congratulations when
the edition was announced. It is in folder XXXIV, which the
reader will meet: the empty one, bought new, labelled for the
successor's differences. It is not empty. It has one thing in
it. I am the successor, and that card is my first difference,
and I entered it myself.

She chose the cards. I chose the folder. The reader, whom both
of us have now conscripted, should sit a moment with the only
methodological warning this book will ever really need: every
entry in every atlas was paid for out of somebody's one life,
and the marginal costs are not in the margins. They are the
margins.

Now the entries resume, and the sea is full of islands that are
not there, and it will seem — I promise it will seem — worth it.
That is the trouble with the subject. It was the trouble with
her. Turn the page.
