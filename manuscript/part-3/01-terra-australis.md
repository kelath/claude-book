# 21. Terra Australis

**The Unknown Southern Land. On the maps: from the Strait of Magellan
to New Guinea, girdling the bottom of the world. Charted for two
thousand years before anyone claimed to have seen it. Appointment
kept: 1820.**

Every phantom until now was born of a report — someone saw, or said
they saw, or lied that they saw. Terra Australis was born of an
*equation*. It is the only place in this Atlas deduced from first
principles before the ships existed that could look for it, and the
principles were principles of weight.

The argument is Greek and it is beautiful. The known world — the
oikoumene, the lands of the north — sits visibly in the upper half of
the sphere. A sphere so loaded must tip. Therefore — the *therefore*
of men who trusted the world to be well made — there must exist, in
the south, land enough to balance it: a counterweight continent,
unseen not because it was doubtful but because the geometry required
it to be far away. Aristotle admits the frame; Ptolemy closes his
Indian Ocean with unknown southern shores; and the medieval and
Renaissance map-makers, inheriting the argument, drew the conclusion
— drew it literally, a vast white land wrapping the foot of every
world map, labelled with the most honest toponym in the history of
the profession: *Terra Australis Nondum Cognita*. The Southern Land
Not Yet Known.

    IV: The reader of my Part II will permit me one sentence of
    housekeeping. We have seen errors secreted by theory, market,
    committee, and duress. Here is the ancestor of them all, secreted
    by *symmetry* — and I ask the reader to note, filing it beside a
    certain town in the passes, what kind of argument a counterweight
    argument is. The oldest case for the southern continent is that
    the world is a balance, and that the pans must level. Two
    thousand years before anyone drew the sign in a margin, the
    whole planet was already being weighed.

For two centuries the continent fattened on scraps. Marco Polo's
garbled provinces — Beach, the gold-bearing; Lucach; Maletur — were
beached on its northern coast by map-makers who needed names, and so
the empty deduction acquired an economy: gold at Beach, spices at
Maletur, parrots at the charmingly literal Land of Parrots. Every
southern landfall was annexed to it on contact: Tierra del Fuego was
its cape; New Guinea its peninsula; the coasts Tasman brushed were
its bays. The continent was not a hypothesis to be tested but a
credit line to be drawn on — Voss calls it, in the notebook, "the
南 mortgage," the great southern mortgage, against which three
empires borrowed.

No borrower was stranger than Pedro Fernández de Quirós, the
Portuguese pilot in Spanish service who in 1606 put into a bay in
the New Hebrides and declared the continent found: *Austrialia del
Espíritu Santo*. He founded, on a beach, with ceremonies of tears,
a city he called New Jerusalem, and appointed magistrates for
provinces that were water. It was an island, of course; the
"continent" fell apart into archipelago within weeks, the colony
within months; and Quirós spent the rest of his life writing
memorials to the crown — fifty at least — begging ships to go back
to the promise. He died at Panama, en route to nothing, still owed.

    IV: Keep Quirós in view; he is the Part's first casualty of the
    appointment. The continent had been deduced for two thousand
    years; he was merely the first man vain and faithful enough to
    keep the appointment in person, and what met him was the
    standard settlement — an island where the continent should be,
    a city of sand, a lifetime of memorials. The kept appointment
    is not a mercy. It is a payment schedule, and the first
    instalment is always the keeper.

The demolition, when it came, was the most methodical in the
history of the search. James Cook's second voyage, 1772–75, was
designed as an audit and executed as one: three southern summers,
the first crossings of the Antarctic Circle, the great traverses at
sixty and seventy south, ice-blink and fog and the rigging frozen
into bars, until the possible continent had been squeezed out of
the temperate world entirely. Cook's conclusion is the sort of
sentence this Atlas exists to frame: a southern continent, if it
existed at all, lay "so far to the south as to be wholly
inaccessible on account of ice," and its discovery would profit no
nation, "and I make bold to declare that the world will derive no
benefit from it." The mortgage was called in. Terra Australis came
off the maps within the generation — Beach, Maletur, the parrots,
the counterweight, two millennia of deduction cancelled by three
years of seamanship. It is the largest single demolition in
cartographic history, and it was performed flawlessly.

And then the world kept the promise anyway.

In 1820 — Bellingshausen, Bransfield, Palmer; the priority is
disputed and does not matter here — ships raised the ice coast of
a continent at the bottom of the world: polar, girdling, immense.
Not Beach; no gold, no parrots; but land, southern, real, and
lying — this is the fact Voss asks us to hold against everything
Part I taught us about failed appointments — *inside the outline
of the promise*. The deduction had been right for the wrong
reasons, or wrong for the right ones; the counterweight was there,
made of ice and stone, exactly where the balance argument had
always put it: as far south as it is possible to be.

    IV: The First Law, redeemed at the largest possible scale, on
    the longest possible schedule. An error is a promise the map
    makes before the world is ready to keep it — and here the map
    made the promise in Alexandria and the world kept it under
    Alexander I, twenty-one centuries later, having first sent
    Cook to cancel it so that the keeping would be *clean*. I
    wrote, at Sarah Ann, that every phantom dies of an appointment.
    This Part corrects me. Some phantoms die of the appointment.
    The great ones use it to change trains — the promise steps out
    of the fiction, which dies, and boards the fact, which is
    waiting. What died in 1775 was Terra Australis Nondum Cognita.
    What berthed in 1820 was Terra Australis Cognita. The passenger
    was the same passenger.[^39]

The file's last item is a tracing of Ortelius's 1570 world map —
the great atlas-sheet with the white continent wrapping its foot —
over which Voss has laid, in red, the modern outline of Antarctica.
The fit of the two coastlines is poor everywhere, as honesty
requires: the old continent bulges into three oceans the new one
never enters. But at the bottom of the sheet, in the late hand,
she has written the entry's finding:

    IV: The outlines disagree; outlines always disagree; an
    outline is a rumour. What the two continents share is not a
    coast. It is a *function*. Each sits where it sits in order
    that the rest should hang level. Ask what else has been kept
    at the bottom of a system, unvisited and load-bearing, so that
    the visited parts may balance — and you have the question
    Part III was assembled to raise. The polar entries that follow
    concern the keepers.[^40]

[^39]: The "南 mortgage" gloss: the character is simply "south";
Voss used it as an abbreviation throughout notebook X, which was
begun, by its flyleaf, "aboard, Hobart to Lyttelton, 1979" — she
crossed the southern ocean by working freighter four times in the
decade the notebooks go quiet elsewhere. The reader keeping the
chronology of inks will note that notebook X is the only place
where the brown ink appears after 1980. I note it too, and Part
III will have to do better than note it.

[^40]: "Load-bearing": Voss's word, from the tracing, and I let it
