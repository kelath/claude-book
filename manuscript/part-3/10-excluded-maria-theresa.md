## The Third Excluded Entry: María Theresa Reef

**South Pacific, east of New Zealand. Reported 1843 by the whaler
Maria Theresa of New Bedford. Never found. Position formally
recomputed 1983. Present on electronic charts within living memory.
Account: current.**

The reader has attended, by now, a great many funerals: islands
dead of soundings, of walks, of eclipses, of decrees, of memoranda.
This last entry concerns the one that is not dead — the phantom
still in service, the working instrument the reader's own century
has continued, with its satellites and its databases, to carry.

The facts are brief because the file is still accruing them. In
November 1843 Asaph Taber, master of the New Bedford whaler María
Theresa, reported breakers — a low reef awash — in the empty ocean
east of New Zealand, and named them for his ship. No vessel has
ever confirmed them. The position was searched in the ordinary
course of a century's traffic; the reef declined every appointment;
and by the rules this Atlas has established the reader knows the
biography that should follow: doubtful, E.D., struck. It never
came. The reef sailed through the great chart-cleansings of the
nineteenth century, through the hydrographic rationalisations of
the twentieth, through the conversion of the world's charts to
databases — the same conversion that carried Sandy Island to its
2012 embarrassment — and when scholars in 1983 re-examined Taber's
log and found his longitude miscomputed, the offices did not
delete the reef. They MOVED it: recalculated, repositioned,
reissued — a formal correction of the coordinates of a place no
one has ever seen, one hundred and forty years after its single
sighting. It appears on electronic charts thereafter at its new
address, annotated with the eleven letters the reader has met:
for the avoidance of doubt.

Fiction, which audits nothing, understood the reef's value first.
Jules Verne — who read the hydrographic literature the way Poe
read the voyages — twice marooned his castaways on it, as Tabor
Island: gave it grass, goats, a castaway, a rescue; used it, in
two novels, as the one fixed point of salvation in the empty
quarter of the map. Generations of readers have known precisely
one fact about that stretch of ocean, and the fact is the reef,
and the fact is false, and the fact has saved fictional lives in
the only economy where phantoms are allowed to pay out openly.

    And so it cannot be indexed, and the reason completes the
    lesson of these three refused entries, and I will state it
    as the teller would. Croker's account was voided; Thompson's
    was annulled, or may have been; María Theresa's is OPEN — not
    open as a mystery is open, but open as a shop is open. Watch
    the behaviour, as I have watched it from three ships' bridges
    across forty years, the reef present each time in the glowing
    chart, its depth unsurveyed, its existence unbelieved by the
    very officers navigating around it. The modern office keeps
    it for a reason it states plainly and does not understand:
    deletion is the one act the sea never confirms. Just so. The
    hydrographer of the satellite age has arrived, by pure
    institutional caution, at the whole doctrine of this Atlas:
    that removal is the risky operation; that absence must be
    maintained; that it is cheaper to carry a phantom than to
    close its account wrongly — and so they carry it, one
    working instrument retained against the avoidance of doubt,
    which is to say FOR the maintenance of doubt, a licensed
    crack in the world's certainty, eleven letters wide, kept
    current by the least mystical profession on earth. I checked
    it last from the bridge of a container ship in 2009. It was
    there, at its corrected address, patient as a teller's
    window. It will be there when this book is read. It has a
    job, and the job is not finished, and I know of no better
    definition of the living. I have outlived every phantom in
    my index. This one will outlive me — and the schedules in
    whose folder I file it will say, to whoever reads them in
    the order I have left them: the appointments do not end with
    the appointee. The till is open. The queue moves.
