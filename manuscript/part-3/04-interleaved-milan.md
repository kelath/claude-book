# Interleaved: The Milanese File

*[This note stands outside Voss's numbering. It is mine, and it is
longer than an editor's note has any right to be, and I ask the
indulgence once. Three threads knotted in the same fortnight, and the
reader is owed all three. — E.M.]*

The Braidense answered in eleven days, with the thoroughness Italian
librarians reserve for questions nobody has asked in a century. The
atlas is the *Atlante universale* of the house of Salterio, Milan,
issued in parts, 1846–1849; a mid-market production, competent,
derivative, printed in modest numbers for the schools and the
seminaries of Lombardy. The firm dissolved in 1851 on the death of
its proprietor. The library's set is complete, and its file on the
firm contains the usual sediment — invoices, a privilege from the
Austrian censor, an inventory drawn up at dissolution.

Three items from the librarian's summary, in ascending order of the
trouble they have cost me.

First: the plates. The dissolution inventory lists the copper for
every sheet of the atlas, valued for scrap or resale, except the
three northern sheets — the sheets, the reader will already have
moved ahead of me, that carry the pass at Kong, the sounded strait
of California, and the drowned Frisland. Against these the inventory
records: *ceduti* — made over, transferred — with no buyer named and
no price entered. In a document whose entire purpose is the entering
of prices.

Second: the engraver. The firm's sheets are signed in the colophon
by its regular hands, Anfossi and Ronchetti, except the three
northern sheets, which carry no name. They carry instead, in the
bottom margin, very small, a device the librarian described — she
had no reason to describe it otherwise — as "a small circle divided
by a horizontal line, perhaps a printer's balance mark."

Third: the register of consultation. The Braidense, blessedly, has
kept its readers' book. The Salterio atlas has been called up
eleven times since 1900. Nine were cataloguers and thesis-writers,
accounted for. The tenth was in October 1969: *I. Voss, Bergen*.
The eleventh was in March 2019, six months before Dutch Harbor. The
name in the book for that day is not hers. The hand, the librarian
writes, is "old-fashioned, upright, certainly European"; the name
reads **T. Salter**.

I sat with the letter for an evening, and I will write down what I
thought, in the order I thought it, because the order is the point
of this note.

My first thought was the one this edition has been building toward
for a hundred pages: *Salter. Salterio. The house that engraved
the doors has a name, and the name initials to T.* — and for an hour
I walked the fen road behind my college in a state I decline to
dress up: something between terror and the specific joy of a fit,
the joy Rennell must have felt when the mountains explained the
river.

My second thought arrived near the bridge, and it was colder, and I
give it to the reader with both hands because an editor who buries
it is no longer an editor. *Voss was there in 1969.* She was at the
La Palma archive in 1979, at the Delisle drawer in 1968, in the HBC
files at Winnipeg; she handled the Bodleian's Armenian manual
decades before I found folio 214 present against the catalogue; she
had fifty years, the skills of the trade, and the run of every
archive in this book. Iron-gall ink cannot be dated to the decade.
A pass, a sounding, a marginal balance — a competent hand could add
any of them to a nineteenth-century sheet in an afternoon, and the
Braidense's conservator, whom I asked, confirms only that the
annotations are "consistent with the plate period, not otherwise
dateable." Every impossible document in this Atlas lies on Iris
Voss's own itinerary. I have been verifying, it may be, not the
world but her路线 — her route; forgive me, her word for it was in
the notebooks and I have caught her habit of reaching for other
alphabets when the English is too plain. The thesis assembles
itself with horrible ease: a brilliant, thwarted scholar, refused
by the referees in 1971, scandalised out of Vienna in 1988, spends
her half-century salting the world's archives with the evidence her
doctrine required, invents three islands outright for her own
catalogue, addresses her future editor by name from a fruit boat
she may never have boarded, and dies leaving the whole apparatus
primed — a posthumous machine for capturing exactly the kind of
reader I have been. Under this reading there is no house, no bank,
no weighing; there is only a mind of the first order, wounded in
1971, revenging itself on the profession by way of its own
posterity. I record that this reading explains nearly everything.
I record that it is the most economical account of the file. I
record that for three days I believed it, and they were the most
restful days I have spent since Bergen.

What ended the rest was arithmetic, not faith. The reading explains
nearly everything. It does not explain the eleventh line of the
Braidense register, written when Iris Voss was eighty-seven and six
months from her boat, in a hand that is not hers, under a name that
did not exist for me to notice until I had done four years of work
she could not have known I would do. It does not explain the first
Basel offer, lodged before the papers were catalogued. And it does
not explain — I have checked, with the meanness of a woman hoping
to be wrong — how a forger working before 1969 could enter, beside
a drowned island, a sounding in metres that agrees with a bathymetry
nobody had yet surveyed. The economical account has a hole below
the waterline, and the hole is shaped like the thing it was built
to keep out.

Which brings me to the fortnight's third thread. The fourth offer
arrived on the Tuesday: the Basel firm — it has a name now, and I
will spend it: **Halter & Cie, Basel**, *Halter*, the reader of
German will note, being what it is — writing this time not of the
papers entire but of "the Milanese materials and associated
correspondence," nine days after my letter to the Braidense, of
which no copy exists outside my college pigeonhole and the
library's registry. When I telephoned, a partner took the call
without being fetched. I asked whom they acted for. He said the
client's interest was not acquisitive but custodial: "The client
wishes the papers *kept*, Dr. Marsh. Kept has two senses in
English, I believe. In our instruction it carries both." I asked
what would happen if I published instead. There was a pause of
some seconds, without breath in it, and then: "Then the client
would wish you kept also," and the line went, not dead, but — I
choose the word — *quiet*, and I sat with the receiver a while,
listening to a silence I could not persuade myself was empty.

I am going to Bergen. The storage unit has been paid up, it
transpires, through 2031, by standing order from an account the
solicitor cannot trace past its Basel clearing. Folder VII is
missing; but folders sit in ranks, and dust is a document, and
nobody has audited the shelf itself. The entries that follow — the
black rock at the Pole, and the station that would not stop
reporting — I have prepared for print before leaving, in case.

In case of what, the apparatus does not say.
