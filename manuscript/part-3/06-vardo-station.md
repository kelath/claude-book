# 25. Vardø-9

**Established 1954 on the islet of Kvitholmen, north-east of Svalbard,
by the Norwegian meteorological service. Evacuated 1957. Reported
until 1960. The islet does not exist. The reports were correct.**

The phantoms of this Atlas have until now been places that failed to
answer. The file before us concerns the opposite defect, and Voss
gives warning of it in the first line of her draft: "What follows is
the only entry I would unwrite if I could."

The paperwork of Vardø-9 is orderly to the point of comedy. In the
expansion of the northern observing network after the war, the
Norwegian service commissioned a subsidiary station — ninth in the
chain administered from Vardø — on Kvitholmen, a low skerry with a
beacon foundation, charted at 80°14′N, 31°40′E since a survey of
1899. Four men and a diesel generator were landed in August 1954.
The station reported weather — pressure, temperature, wind, ice —
four times daily, by radio, in the standard synoptic code, and its
reports were unremarkable and its relief crews rotated on schedule
and nothing whatever occurred. In 1957, in the reshuffling of
stations for the International Geophysical Year, Vardø-9 was closed
as redundant: the last crew was taken off in September, the
instruments crated, the generator — the log survives — drained and
left greased against a reoccupation that was never intended.

The station reported the next morning at 0600, in code, on
schedule, with its own call sign.

    IV: Fix the sequence, because everything depends on it. The
    reports did not begin when the crew was landed. They continued
    when the crew was removed. Nothing announced itself; nothing
    changed; the 0600 synoptic arrived as it had arrived four
    thousand times before, correct in format, plausible in content,
    and the only anomalous thing about it was that the station was
    empty. An error of the eye lasts a watch. An error of the file
    lasts a century. This was neither. This was an *office that
    declined to close*.

The service did what services do. The frequency was monitored: the
transmissions were there, keyed cleanly, on the station's assigned
schedule, from — the bearings agreed — the station's direction. A
patrol vessel was diverted in the summer of 1958 to recover whatever
prankster or castaway was working the set. It is at this point that
the file changes genre, because the vessel, arriving at 80°14′N,
31°40′E in fair weather, found open water. No skerry; no beacon
foundation; sounding after sounding of empty sea. The survey of
1899 was re-examined and found to rest, like so much in this Part,
on a single transit in fog; the beacon foundation had been budgeted
but never confirmed built; the 1954 landing party's photographs
show rock, hut, and aerial, and cannot show where. Kvitholmen was
struck from the charts in 1959 — the standard administrative death,
the reader has seen a dozen — with the sole difference that this
particular non-existent island was, at the hour of its striking,
still reporting the weather.

    IV: The service's dilemma was theological and they solved it
    as bureaucracies solve theology: by filing. The reports could
    not be accepted, since their station of origin did not exist;
    they could not be discarded, since they verified. From October
    1958 the synoptics of Vardø-9 were entered in the registers
    under the annotation "origin unattributed," which I commend to
    the reader as the finest sentence of official prose in this
    Atlas. The weather was good. The address was bad. The ledger
    took the weather and quarantined the address, and in doing so
    conceded — no one appears to have noticed — the whole of my
    doctrine: that the observation and the place are separable
    accounts, and that the world will bank a true report drawn on
    a false position.

And they verified. This is the finding that moved the entry, in
Voss's index, from curiosity to keystone. The phantom synoptics ran
for three years — October 1957 to September 1960, some four
thousand transmissions — and when, decades later, the modern
reanalyses reconstructed the era's Arctic weather from all sources,
a doctoral student in Tromsø ran the orphaned Vardø-9 series
against the reconstruction as a curiosity. The real stations of the
sector scatter about the truth as real stations do: biased
thermometers, iced anemometers, tired men misreading a vernier at
0600. The unattributed series does not scatter. Across three years
its residuals sit inside the instrumental error of the reanalysis
itself — which is to say the file's blunt summary, which I have
verified: *the most accurate observations in the sector were
supplied by a station that was never anywhere.*[^47]

    IV: Every observer adds himself to the observation; that is
    what an observer is. Warm hut, cold vernier, breakfast, hope —
    the human signature is the scatter, and the scatter is how we
    know a man was there. The Vardø-9 series has no signature. It
    is not the record of someone watching the weather. It is the
    record of weather that has learned the code — or say it with
    the sign, since we possess the sign: the reports balance. To
    the gram. Nothing reporting from nowhere, and the pans
    perfectly level. I have spent my life proving that places
    persist after we unlist them. I had not, before this file,
    been made to consider that they might also *work*.

The last transmission is dated 14 September 1960, at 0600. It is
complete and correct: pressure, temperature, wind, nine-tenths ice.
Appended, outside the code groups, in plain-language Norwegian, are
four words — the only plain language in six years of traffic:

*Stenger. Avløsning er kommet.*

Closing down. The relief has come.

    IV: Not "relieved of duty." Not "abandoning station." The
    relief HAS COME — arrival, present tense, someone stepping off
    a boat onto rock that is not there, taking over an instrument
    that no one left. The office did not close. The office changed
    hands. The reader of my Vienna paper asked who advertises the
    post. I have the date of one handover, to the day, and I
    observe — filing it, as the service filed its weather, without
    the capacity to do otherwise — that the day was the fourteenth
    of September.[^48]

[^47]: The Tromsø archive is real and open; the orphaned series is
where the entry says it is; the comparison study exists as a
working paper, unpublished — its author left the field the same
year and did not answer my letters, which after four years of this
edition I no longer characterise. The residual analysis I have
checked myself, with my own copy of the reanalysis fields, on my
own machine, twice. The figures are as Voss gives them. I looked
for the error for a week. There is a class of finding for which
the honest annotation is the service's own: origin unattributed.

[^48]: Voss boarded her boat at Dutch Harbor on 14 September 2019
— fifty-nine years, to the day, after the relief came to Vardø-9.
The station's positions, plotted from its own reports across the
three orphan years, drift — coherently, as a floe drifts — and the
final transmitted position lies on the meridian of 168° west. The
reader has met that meridian twice now without my drawing the
line. I am drawing it. In her concordance, against folder VII,
Voss entered a single cross-reference, and it is this entry.
