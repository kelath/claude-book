# 23. Frisland

**61°N, 22°W (Zeno map, 1558). South of Iceland: an island the size of
Ireland, with thirty-one named towns, seven named headlands, a
monastery, and a king. The best-mapped island that never existed.**

The Buss entry sent the reader here with a weary *q.v.*, and the debt
now falls due. Frisland is the parent phantom of the North Atlantic —
the imaginary island from which a real ship sighted another imaginary
island — and it is the Atlas's masterpiece of a class we have met only
in parts before: the forged geography, complete at birth, that
outperformed honest observation for a hundred and fifty years.

It was published in Venice in 1558, in a slim book by Nicolò Zeno the
younger, senator of the Republic, concerning the voyages of his
ancestors. Around 1380, the book relates, Nicolò and Antonio Zeno
sailed the northern seas in the service of a prince called Zichmni;
were wrecked upon and then made governors of the great island of
Frisland; visited Estland and Icaria and Estotiland and Drogeo, lands
arranged across the ocean like stepping stones to America a century
before Columbus — the point of the exercise, no reader in Venice
missed it, being that Venetian blood had got there first. The book
came with a map: the famous *carta da navegar*, engraved with the
whole archipelago, Frisland rendered in loving detail — its fjords,
its thirty-one towns with their Italianate-Nordic names, Sanestol and
Ocibar and Bondendon — a coastline, Voss notes, with the specific
rhythm of coastlines that have been sailed, which is a rhythm a
draughtsman can learn.

    IV: The Zeno narrative is a forgery; the consensus has held for
    a century and I do not quarrel with it. Note instead what the
    forgery understood. An honest report gives you a bearing and a
    fog-bank; it survives, as we have seen, on hope and copying. A
    forgery gives you thirty-one towns, because the forger — alone
    among the contributors to the map of the world — knows exactly
    what evidence would convince him, and supplies it. Frisland is
    not the record of an error. It is the record of a *standard of
    proof*, met precisely, from the inside.

And it conquered. Mercator took Frisland whole onto the world map of
1569 — the most influential map, it may be, ever drawn — and from
Mercator it passed to Ortelius, and from Ortelius to everyone.
For a century the island sat south of Iceland on the best charts in
Europe, not as a rumour but as a settled, administered fact, its
towns named in the gazetteers, its king remembered in the histories.
The English took it seriously enough to annex: John Dee, compiling
the imperial title of Elizabeth I, listed Frisland among the crown's
Atlantic possessions, on the grounds that a land once ruled by the
mythical conqueror Arthur was England's to resume. A kingdom's claim,
resting on a Venetian family's vanity, to an island that was not
there: the paper empires of Part II, the reader sees, had salt-water
ancestors.

The conquest did not stop at paper. In 1578 Martin Frobisher,
outbound on his third voyage with the largest Arctic fleet the
century assembled, raised a high, glaciered coast where the charts
put Frisland, went ashore in the boats, and took possession for the
queen under the name West England. The coast was Greenland — the
real, enormous, thoroughly inhabited Greenland — misplaced by the
charts and now mistaken, in good faith, for a forged island. For
some decades afterwards English charts carried the south of
Greenland as "Frisland, alias West England," and the reader should
pause on what has happened here, because nothing like it occurs
elsewhere in the Atlas: the fiction did not merely persist alongside
the world. It captured territory. A real coastline spent two
generations enrolled under an invented name, its actual identity —
known to the Norse for six hundred years — administratively
suspended.

    IV: We have watched errors annex blank sea and furnished
    drawers. Frisland annexed *Greenland*. This is the forgery's
    second law: supplied with sufficient detail, the map does not
    check the world against itself; it checks the world against
    the forgery, and corrects the world. Frobisher stood on the
    third-largest landmass of the hemisphere and his instruments
    told him it was a Venetian invention, and he believed the
    instruments, because the invention was better documented than
    the hemisphere.

The dissolution took a further century and proceeded, as Voss's
die-off tracings show, by demotion in stages: Frisland full-sized on
the charts of 1620; halved by 1650; by 1700 a doubtful scrap,
sometimes annotated as sunken — the Buss solution, the decent
burial; gone by the 1740s except in the antiquarian trade. The
scholarship that finally dismantled the Zeno book itself is
nineteenth-century, and it found what such scholarship always
finds: the narrative stitched from identifiable printed sources,
the map compiled from Olaus Magnus and the portolans, the family
archive conveniently burned, the young senator's motive —
precedence over Columbus, Venice over Genoa — standing plainly in
the preface all along. Frisland was demolished as geography and
reclassified as literature, and there, as far as the profession is
concerned, the file closes.

Voss's file does not close there. Two items remain, and I give
them in her order.

The first is a tracing: Frisland's coastline laid over the Faroe
Islands — the standard candidate for its kernel of truth — rotated
and scaled. The fit is suggestive and imperfect, as every such fit
in this book has been; outlines, we have been taught, are rumours.
Beneath it Voss has written not a conclusion but a tabulation: of
the thirty-one town names on the Zeno map, twenty-nine can be
derived, with patience, from real toponyms of the Faroes, Iceland,
and the Olaus Magnus map — the forger's sources, duly identified.
Two cannot: Sorand, on the island's southern cape, and Rane, at
the head of its western fjord. "Sources unlocated," Voss notes,
"in a document whose sources are its entire anatomy. A forger
does not invent freely; invention is expensive; he copies. From
what did he copy Sorand and Rane?"[^43]

The second item is a chart, and the reader who has followed this
edition will know its city before I name it. The Milanese atlas of
1849 — the engraver who kept a pass through the Mountains of Kong,
who alone preserved island-California with a sounded strait —
carries, south of Iceland, in the faint hatching the house used
for submerged features, an outline labelled *Frislanda (sommersa)*.
The outline is not Zeno's. It is smaller, turned some degrees
east, and against its western fjord — at the position of the
unlocatable town of Rane — the engraver has placed his customary
small figure and a sounding: 1,238 metres.[^44]

[^43]: The Zeno philology is real and checkable: the source-
hunting is nineteenth-century scholarship, and Voss's twenty-nine
derivations follow it. Sorand and Rane are indeed the residue; the
standard literature calls them "unexplained" and moves on, which
is what standard literature is for. Voss's question — from what
does a copyist copy the uncopied? — is the question this Atlas has
been asking since a marginal circle in a La Palma inquiry file,
and I no longer pretend it is rhetorical.

[^44]: Third appearance of the Milanese house; third annotation
that could not have been compiled from any source I can trace; and
the sounding sits where the reader knew it would sit. I have
written to the Braidense to ask what is known of the firm — its
name, its plates, its proprietor. That inquiry, and what came back,
belongs to the next interleaved note, and I will not compress it
into a footnote. The reader has been patient with my apparatus;
the apparatus is about to require patience of a different kind.
