# 22. The Open Polar Sea

**Beyond the ice barrier, 82°N to the Pole. Theorised c. 1527–1896.
A warm, navigable sea at the top of the world. Appointment:
rescheduled.**

The theory is older than the instruments that would eventually kill
it, and it survived three and a half centuries on an argument that
is, on its face, not unreasonable: that the pack ice ringing the
Arctic is a barrier, not a floor — that a ship stubborn enough or
lucky enough to force the belt of ice would break through into
clear water beyond it, because ice, everywhere else observed, is a
coastal and seasonal phenomenon, and a sea does not freeze solid to
its own centre. Robert Thorne proposed a navigable short cut over
the pole to Cathay in 1527; Daines Barrington collected supposed
whalers' testimony of open water north of Spitsbergen in the 1770s
and pressed the Royal Society to try for it; and by the middle of
the nineteenth century, with steam and the electric telegraph
making the world feel newly conquerable, the Open Polar Sea had
acquired what Terra Australis had needed two thousand years to earn
and never quite got: two credentialed scientific champions.

Matthew Fontaine Maury, the American superintendent of the Naval
Observatory and the century's foremost oceanographer, argued the
case from ocean physics — that a warm current entering the Arctic
basin could not simply vanish, and must surface somewhere north of
the ice as open water. August Petermann, the German cartographer
whose maps were the most trusted in Europe and who had never been
north of Scotland, argued it more elaborately and more
dangerously: a warm Atlantic current — the true course of the Gulf
Stream — must breach the ice at Spitsbergen; a warm Pacific current,
the Kuro Siwo, must do the same at Bering Strait; the two gateways
converged, on Petermann's charts, on a temperate polar basin drawn
with the calm hydrographic confidence of a man describing a lake he
had personally rowed across.

    IV: Note what is being modelled and what is not. Maury and
    Petermann are not describing an observation. They are describing
    a *conservation law* — heat goes in, heat must come out, ice is
    an accounting error waiting to be corrected. It is the most
    respectable kind of wrongness there is, because the arithmetic
    is sound and only the geography is imaginary. I have met this
    error since in boardrooms wearing better suits. The sum is
    always right. The sum was never the question.

Belief acquired its proof in 1854, and the proof was a single
sentence. Elisha Kent Kane's expedition, wintering at appalling cost
in the channel that now bears his name, sent a party under William
Morton up the Greenland coast in the spring of 1854; Morton climbed
to a headland he named Cape Constitution and reported, looking
north across what he took to be the edge of the ice, open water to
the horizon — "not a speck of ice," in the phrase that did the
work — with swell, with birds, with every visible sign of a living
sea. He had in fact been looking at a local polynya, a patch kept
open by current and wind, of a kind the Arctic produces regularly
and locally and which proves nothing whatsoever about what lies
beyond it. It was received, on both sides of the Atlantic, as
confirmation entire. Isaac Hayes, who had served under Kane, went
back in 1860–61 explicitly to reach the Open Polar Sea and reported,
in 1861, that he had stood upon its shore — a claim built on a
position badly inflated by the sledging conditions of the day, which
later survey moved substantially southward, so that the shore Hayes
stood on and the sea he saw from it both retreated, on
re-examination, into the ordinary pack.

    IV: A polynya is a hole in the ice kept open by current; the
    Open Polar Sea is a hole in the argument kept open by desire.
    Both close eventually. Morton's sentence travelled for forty
    years on four words — not a speck of ice — and I have found, in
    three separate popular accounts printed after 1880, the same
    four words quoted as if Morton were still standing on the
    headland saying them. He was not. He was a name in a book. The
    book had learned to talk.

The invoice fell to George Washington De Long, who sailed the
*Jeannette* from San Francisco in July 1879, on funding raised
substantially on Petermann's authority, to find the warm Bering
gateway. He found pack ice north of Wrangel Island, into which the
ship froze fast within weeks and drifted, locked, for nearly two
years, until the ice crushed her in June 1881. De Long and thirteen
of his men died in the retreat down the Lena Delta; relics of the
wreck, surfacing years later on the coast of Greenland, gave Nansen
the transpolar drift he would stake the *Fram* on. It is the
deadliest single bill the theory presented, and it was drawn on a
German armchair and cashed in Siberia.

    IV: Petermann did not sail. This is not incidental; it is the
    mechanism. The men who draw the gateway are rarely the men who
    are asked to find it standing open, and the distance between the
    drawing hand and the drowning hand is the whole moral geography
    of this Atlas. I keep a shelf for cartographers who paid in
    person and a much longer shelf for those who did not. Petermann
    is on the long shelf. He is in good company there. The company
    is the point.

Nansen closed the account with a design so deliberately contrary to
rescue that it reads, still, as an act of controlled despair: a
ship, the *Fram*, built round-hulled to be squeezed up out of the
ice rather than crushed by it, deliberately frozen into the pack
north of Siberia in September 1893 and left to drift with it, on
the working assumption — De Long's wreckage was the evidence — that
the drift crossed near or over the Pole itself. It did not cross
open water. For nearly three years the *Fram* rode a deep,
ice-covered polar basin, sounding bottom at depths the century had
not imagined lay under permanent ice, proving by the simple
expedient of not sinking that there was no sea to sink into. The
Open Polar Sea came off the charts in the decade after, the last
respectable printing of it dated to the 1880s and lingering, as
a courtesy, into a few atlases of the 1890s that hadn't yet heard.

    IV: One figure from the *Fram*'s legacy, for the file: at the
    shelf break where the theory's warm gateway was supposed to
    open, the basin surveys find the floor near enough twelve
    hundred and forty metres down. The theory promised a surface.
    The world kept, instead, a floor.

Here Voss's essay, in every draft before the last, ends — the
standard close of this Atlas, ice claiming the promise as thoroughly
as sand claimed Quirós's city. But the file did not close in 1896,
because Voss did not stop watching, and the last folder is dated
with a decade the theory's proponents never lived to see. The
satellite record of the September Arctic sea-ice minimum — the
smallest extent the ice reaches each year, after the summer melt —
begins in 1979 and has fallen with an unevenness that nonetheless
reads, plotted, as a slope: 2007 broke every prior record; 2012
broke 2007's; and by the years Voss was annotating the printouts in
the last hand available to her, the modelling centres she clipped
and filed were converging on projections of a seasonally ice-free
September Arctic Ocean within her readers' lifetimes, on a schedule
the century's armchair cartographers had missed by roughly a hundred
and fifty years and one hemisphere's worth of gateway.

    IV: The reader will want the neat sentence and is owed it:
    Maury and Petermann were not wrong. They were early, by about a
    century and a half, and they had the mechanism wrong — no
    hidden strait breathes warmth into a static basin; the basin
    itself is warming — but the picture on Petermann's 1865 sheet,
    a navigable sea where the pole should be, is now a picture with
    a delivery date stamped on it by people who have never heard of
    Petermann. An error is a promise the map makes before the world
    is ready to keep it. No one has ever asked what happens when the
    world becomes, on its own schedule and for its own reasons,
    ready.[^41]

The promise, on the evidence of Voss's own file, did not wait
politely for a reader to arrive and admire the closing of the loop.
It arrived while she was still watching it.[^42]

[^41]: Maury's thermal argument and Petermann's Gulf Stream/Kuro
Siwo gateway theory are documented in both men's published
correspondence with the era's geographical societies; the Morton
sighting and its "not a speck of ice" phrasing are Kane's own
report of the 1854 expedition; the *Jeannette*'s loss and the
recovery of her relics on the Greenland coast, which supplied
Nansen's working premise for the *Fram* drift, are matters of
settled record. The September sea-ice minimum series Voss was
clipping is the same series maintained publicly since 1979 by the
US National Snow and Ice Data Center; I have checked her figures
against it line for line and found her transcription, as usual,
exact to the decimal the source printed. She was not embellishing
the decline. She did not need to.

[^42]: The clippings are annotated in her hand, year by year, a
neat descending column of September minimums, 1979 to 2018, and
beside the projected date-range then current for the first
ice-free September — a range that begins, in the literature she
was reading, in the early 2020s — she has written, in the last
hand the papers contain: *My appointment is earlier.* I have no
gloss to offer for this that improves on leaving it there. What I
can add is a fact I did not check until preparing this entry: the
skipper who took her out of Dutch Harbor on her final booking told
the inquiry she had asked him to hold a bearing so that she might,
in his words, verify a depth sounding. I have now looked up what
lies on that bearing. The charted depth is 1,230 metres.
