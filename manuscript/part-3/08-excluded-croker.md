# The Excluded Entries

*[Between Part III and the Blank Quarter, the reader must pause with
me over a finding I owe to a folder I had misjudged. Folder XXVI,
labelled "Schedules," I catalogued as miscellany: ferry timetables,
tide tables, eclipse predictions, interfiled across ninety years in
an order I could not crack. In February, preparing the catalogue for
Appendix A, I cracked it — or rather I stopped trying, and read the
folder front to back like a book, which is what it is. Interleaved
among the timetables, at intervals that are themselves a schedule I
decline to describe, are three completed entries: finished, fair-
copied, headed and ruled like every entry in this Atlas — and absent
from the index. Not lost; not drafts. Refused. Voss indexed
thirty-three entries and left these three, finished, in the folder
of things that run on time. I print them here, in their folder
order, with no apparatus: they came to me without any, and the
Blank Quarter has taught the reader how to be alone with a text.
But I will answer the question the reader is asking, because I
asked it for a month: why would she exclude finished work? The
answer, I believe, is in the book she taught me to read. The index
is a ledger, and a ledger admits only settled accounts. These
three are unsettled — one voided before it could be banked, one
escaped, and one still running — and a woman who spent her life
respecting the difference column would no more index an open
account than a bank would print a balance mid-transaction. She
filed them with the schedules because that is what they are.
Appointments pending. — E.M.]*

## The First Excluded Entry: The Croker Mountains

**Lancaster Sound, 74°N. Sighted 31 August 1818, mid-afternoon.
Disproved 4 August 1819. Lifespan: eleven months. The shortest-lived
range in the history of geography, and the most expensive glance in
the history of the Arctic.**

In the summer of 1818 the Admiralty, idle after Waterloo and rich in
unemployable heroes, sent John Ross north to find the Northwest
Passage. He was a sound officer with a burnt face and thirty years'
service, and he did everything right until the last afternoon that
mattered, and then he did one thing wrong, and the one thing took
eleven months to disprove and the rest of his life to pay for.

His ships entered Lancaster Sound at the end of August — the broad
strait north of Baffin Island which is, though no living person then
knew it, the true front door of the Passage, the channel through
which every successful transit since has sailed. The whalers
believed in it; his own officers believed in it; the water was open
and the season had days left in it. On the last afternoon of the
month, with the sound running wide and westward before him, Ross
went on deck between drifts of fog, raised his glass, and saw, some
eight leagues distant, a range of mountains drawn clean across the
strait from shore to shore: high land, continuous, unarguable,
closing the passage like a wall at the end of a corridor. He named
it on the spot for the Secretary of the Admiralty — the Croker
Mountains — and, satisfied that the sound was a bay, ordered the
ships about, and sailed for England.

The afternoon's inventory deserves its plainness. His second, Parry,
below at dinner, saw nothing, and wrote home in guarded fury. His
scientific officer saw nothing. The officers of the second ship saw
nothing. No one else on either vessel, at any point, saw the
mountains, and several were watching the same horizon through
comparable glasses. Ross saw them for perhaps ten minutes, once,
alone in the sense that matters — and Ross, this must be said and
the reader of Peary will hear the difference, was not lying. He
put his sighting in the log while the fog was still on the water;
he defended it before a parliamentary committee with the baffled
stubbornness of an honest man; he went to his grave, forty years
on, insisting on his eyes. The Arctic's superior mirage — the
same duct of cold air that built Crocker Land for MacMillan a
century later — had assembled, from the ice and headlands beyond
the horizon, a wall precisely where a tired commander's caution
wanted one, and he had believed his instrument, and his instrument
that afternoon was a sky with an agenda.

The Admiralty, unamused, sent Parry back the following spring
without him. On 4 August 1819 Parry's ships stood into Lancaster
Sound with royals set and a soldier's wind, and the officers
crowded the rails as the ships bore down on the charted position
of the range — there is an account of the silence on deck, every
glass raised, the latitude called off — and sailed through the
Croker Mountains without wetting a topsail, and onward two hundred
leagues into the Passage, and into the promotion lists. The range
was struck from the charts before the ink of its first printing
was dry. Croker, the Secretary, kept his name on a bay in
Devon Island — a demotion from a mountain range to an indentation,
which the wits of the wardrooms did not fail to price.

Ross was never given an Admiralty command again. He went north at
last in 1829 in a paddle steamer paid for by a gin distiller, spent
four winters frozen in, lost the ship, saved his men, and came home
a public hero and a professional ghost: a knighthood, and no ship,
ever. The mirage had cost the Passage a year, and years were cheap.
What it cost Ross was the rest of his career, paid out in annual
instalments of hope for thirty years.

    And here is why this entry, finished and fair-copied, is not
    in my index. Follow the money as this Atlas has taught. Every
    phantom in the ledger banked something: Brasil banked five
    centuries of belief; Kong banked a continent's closure;
    Sandy banked a hundred and thirty-six years of copying. The
    Croker Mountains banked nothing. Eleven months is too short a
    tenure; the account was voided before the first deposit
    cleared; the charts were clean again while the engraver's
    apprentice was still owed for the plate. By every rule I have
    derived in thirty entries, the error should have left no
    residue at all. It left a residue. It left it in a man. The
    difference that could not be banked against the position was
    struck, in full, against John Ross — carried on his person,
    compounding, for three decades; audited at every dinner
    party; still open at his death. I have found no other case
    where the ledger, denied a place, took a person instead, and
    I decline to index what I cannot close, because there is a
    possibility in this file I have never printed and will print
    now, in the folder where the timetables live: that the
    account is still open. That a difference struck against a
    person does not lapse with the person. That somewhere in the
    system that keeps the shelf and dusts the slot and prints the
    second line, there is a page with Ross's name at its head,
    accruing since 1818, waiting for an heir, an executor, a
    reader — someone standing at the rail of the record with a
    glass, honest, tired, and ready to see a wall. The mirage
    is not the phantom, you see. The mirage was only the teller.
    The debt is the phantom, and debts of that kind do not
    advertise for collection. They wait for the account-holder's
    estate to come asking after the papers.
