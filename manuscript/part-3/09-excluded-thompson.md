## The Second Excluded Entry: Thompson Island

**South Atlantic, 45 miles north-north-east of Bouvetøya, the
remotest island on earth. Reported 1825. Searched for by science
for a century. Struck 1943. Cause of death: unknown — and that is
the entry.**

In December 1825 George Norris, master of the sealer Sprightly,
working the loneliest water in the world, charted two islands: the
one we now call Bouvetøya, which he landed on and claimed, and a
second, low and black, some forty-five miles to the north-north-
east, which he named Thompson. Norris was a working sealer with no
Secretary to flatter and no book to sell; he logged the island the
way he logged weather, in a hand that has been examined by a
century of skeptics and gives them nothing. Of all the single
witnesses in this Atlas — and the reader has met the liars, the
tired, the hopeful, and the flattered — Norris is the one the
prosecution has never laid a glove on.

Thompson Island entered the charts and behaved, at first, like a
respectable phantom: elusive, weather-protected, credited. A
sealer reported it again in 1893. Then the age of scientific
voyaging arrived in those waters, and Thompson began to fail its
appointments — the Valdivia expedition swept for it in 1898 and
found water; ships of the whaling surveys crossed and recrossed
the position; in 1943 a British warship conducted the formal
search that ended it, and the island came off the charts by the
usual administrative death.

So far, the reader will say, an ordinary file: a Dougherty, an
Emerald, a lonely eye. But Thompson's file will not lie flat, for
one reason the twentieth century itself supplied. The seafloor
around Bouvetøya is volcanic and alive; and the hypothesis stands
in the sober literature to this day that Norris logged a real
island — young, black, volcanic — which in the decades after 1825
was destroyed: blown out or foundered by the same forces that
built it, leaving nothing for the Valdivia to find because
nothing remained. Against this stands the bathymetry, which finds
the water deep where an island's stump should be; and between
the honest witness and the deep sounding the question has stood
open for a hundred years, unresolvable, a coroner's verdict
permanently deferred.

    Which is why this entry is finished and will not be indexed.
    Consider what the two verdicts mean in the ledger, because
    they are not two versions of one death; they are two
    different economies. If Thompson never existed, it died our
    ordinary death — audited, voided, its difference struck and
    carried to the column like all the others. But if Norris was
    right — and I have read his log, and I would ship with him —
    then Thompson existed and was unmade by the earth itself,
    without an audit, without an appointment, without a clerk:
    subtracted from the world by the world, while both sets of
    books were looking elsewhere. No difference was struck,
    because the chart and the sea changed together, in step, and
    there was never a gap for the column to record. Do you see
    it? The one clean exit in three hundred years of records.
    Not a transfer. Not a demotion. Not a stowing at the working
    depth. An island that may have left both ledgers at once,
    owing nothing, owed nothing, its account not closed but
    ANNULLED — and I have spent more hours with this thin folder
    than with Kong's forty tracings, because it is the only
    evidence I possess that the system of shelves and weighings
    and carried differences, which I have spent my life mapping
    and will shortly enter, has an outside. I cannot index it
    because I cannot certify the death, and I will not certify
    the death because — I am eighty-seven, and the folder of
    schedules is where the truth goes — because as long as the
    verdict stands open, so does the possibility. Somewhere in
    the file of the world there may be one column for the free.
    Norris saw it, if anyone did. It was black, and young, and
    it did not last, and nothing weighed it, and nothing keeps
    it, and no one will ever be sent to verify its sounding.
    Let the reader hold the thought for as long as it will hold.
    I have never managed a whole night.
