# 24. Rupes Nigra

**The North Pole itself. A black rock of lodestone, thirty-three
leagues about, ringed by an indrawing sea. Charted 1569–c. 1700.
Every compass on earth pointed at it daily.**

It is the best-attested place in this Atlas that nobody ever claimed
to have seen. Of the whole company of promises kept and broken, the
Rupes Nigra is the one furnished with the least evidence and the most
confidence: a rock no one measured, on an island no one reached,
reported by a source that no longer exists, citing a source that no
longer existed even then — and for all that, it possessed what no
other phantom here can boast, a permanent, planet-wide instrument
dedicated to its worship.

The geography, as it stood on the best charts of Europe for two
hundred years, was tidy to the point of theology. At the pole: a
black magnetic rock, the Rupes Nigra, thirty-three leagues in
circumference, standing at the exact crown of the world. Around it, a
sea rushing inward from four directions through four channels, drawn
between four great islands set at the cardinal points — and the sea
did not simply flow to the rock. It descended, at the centre, into
the earth: the waters of the world's oceans, the account had it,
poured continuously into a whirlpool at the top of the globe and were
swallowed into the bowels of the planet, to surface, some geographers
supposed, at the equally hypothetical antipodal vent of the south. It
explained, in one stroke, two things sailors actually needed
explained: why compasses everywhere on earth pointed one way, and
where the tides went. It was elegant. It was operational. It was
false in every particular, and it sat on the world's most trusted
maps for longer than the United States has existed.

    IV: For three centuries every compass on every ship on every sea
    was read as testimony — a finger pointing at the appointment. No
    other phantom in this Atlas commanded a witness on this scale.
    Millions of needles, twice daily, on every ocean, confirming the
    bearing. And not one of those witnesses had been anywhere near
    the destination. They had verified a direction and been credited
    with verifying a place. This is the single most efficient
    manufacture of false certainty I have found in forty years of
    looking, and it required no forger at all. It required only that
    no one ask what a needle actually knows.

The source chain is the part that should have stopped the whole
enterprise at the door, and instead built the door. The account of
the polar rock and its four islands descends from a book called the
*Inventio Fortunata* — "the fortunate discovery" — said to have been
written by an English Franciscan friar, possibly one Nicholas of
Lynn, who had sailed north sometime before 1360 and observed the
pole's geography at first hand. That book is lost. It survives, as
far as anyone in the sixteenth century could establish, only through
a second book, the *Itinerarium* of a Zeeland traveller named Jacobus
Cnoyen, who claimed to have read the *Inventio* or spoken with men
who had. That book is also lost. It survives only through a letter —
the letter survives; it is the single solid plank in the whole
structure — written in 1577 by Gerard Mercator to John Dee, in which
Mercator, from memory, summarised what he recalled of Cnoyen's book
years after reading it, for the private benefit of a friend compiling
England's imperial claims. Every witness in the chain except the
final one is missing. The friar's book is gone. The Zeelander's book
is gone. What remains is a cartographer's recollection of a
traveller's account of a friar's voyage, and on that recollection
Mercator built the most influential geography of the polar world ever
printed.

    IV: The chain of lost books is not, on inspection, an accident of
    poor archiving. Compare it with Frisland, where a forger supplied
    every town because invention, done well, is cheap. Here the
    mechanism runs backward: nothing was invented to fill the gaps,
    because the sources did the filling by leaving. Each link
    survives one step further from the origin than the link before
    it, and the losses are not random — every book that could have
    been checked is the one that is gone. The file on the pole is a
    stair with every step missing but the top one. I have climbed it
    anyway, because it is the only stair offered, and I no longer
    trust myself on the question of whether that is scholarship or a
    kind of complicity.

Mercator did not merely record the geography; he built it, twice,
into the two most consulted images of the world his century produced.
On the great world map of 1569 — the projection that still, unfairly
or not, carries his name — the north polar regions occupy a small
inset in the lower corner, because the projection itself cannot show
the pole, and there, in that inset, are the rock and the four islands
and the four indrawing channels, drawn with the same unblinking
confidence he gave the coastlines he had actually surveyed. In 1595,
the year after his death, his son published the map Mercator had
prepared specifically to correct the inset's smallness: a dedicated
polar projection, the first of its kind, in which the geography could
finally be shown at proper scale — the rock at the centre, the sea
pouring into its four gates, and on one of the four islands, duly
labelled, a nation of *Pygmei*, men four feet tall, whom Cnoyen's
account (via Mercator's memory of it) placed there living out their
short violent lives at the top of the world. The polar map was copied
into atlas after atlas for a century. It is, by any measure of
influence, one of the most successful maps ever made of a place that
was not there.

    IV: I set it down at Sandy Island and the pole is where I first
    learned it: belief moves at the speed of copying, doubt at the
    speed of embarrassment. A map is reprinted the year after it
    appears; a correction is reprinted a generation after it is
    proven, and only once the men who signed the original are safely
    unavailable to be asked about it. Mercator's polar inset took six
    years to become his polar map. It took a hundred and thirty-six
    to come off the atlases in general use. No fact in this Atlas has
    ever moved at the second speed. None ever will.

The dismantling was slower than the building and considerably less
dramatic, because no expedition ever produced a single decisive wreck
against the rock the way Cook's Antarctic traverses demolished the
southern continent in three seasons. Ships that pushed into the four
supposed channels in the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries — after
Frobisher, after Davis, after the long Dutch and English hunt for a
northern passage — found no indraught, no whirlpool devouring the
sea, nothing but the ordinary intransigence of ice, current, and
coast, in places that did not correspond to the four gates at all.
Meanwhile the instrument that had supposedly proved the rock's
existence was quietly turning against it: as magnetic observations
accumulated across the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries, the
point the needles actually pointed toward was shown to wander, to sit
well south of the geographic pole, and eventually to be not a point
at all but a shifting locus with no fixed home — which meant the
compass had never been reporting the pole's location in the first
place, only a magnetic field's, and the field and the axis of the
earth's rotation had never been required to agree. The two poles,
which the *Inventio*'s geography had fused into a single black rock,
came apart in the record books as two separate and independently
moving entities.

    IV: The demotion is the quiet violence in this file. The rock had
    promised one appointment — a single point, magnetic and
    geographic and physical, all three at once, holding the compass
    and the axis and the island together in one black mass. When it
    failed, the promise did not die outright, the way the Open Polar
    Sea's did not die outright. It changed trains. It split into two
    appointments running on separate schedules, magnetic and
    geographic, and released itself from ever again being asked to
    keep both at once. I have watched this manoeuvre before in this
    Part. I did not expect to watch it here, at the one place theory
    had insisted, for three centuries, could not be two things.

It fell to James Clark Ross to deliver the physical verdict, and he
delivered it in person, in a way none of the polar theorists ever
risked. In June 1831, on the Boothia Peninsula in the Canadian
Arctic — a coast some fourteen degrees of latitude from the
geographic pole and nowhere near the rock's charted position — Ross's
dip-needle readings converged on vertical, and he planted a flag at
the point his instruments identified as the North Magnetic Pole,
having walked to it across ordinary tundra, in ordinary weather, and
found there no rock, no whirlpool, no islands, no pygmies: a
featureless, low, undistinguished shore, which he described in his
own account with the flatness of a man who had expected, at some
level he does not admit to, something more. He had reached the
appointment that mattered, and it kept nothing at all.

The last item in Voss's file is not narrative but a plot, and it is
the entry's true ending.[^45] Across a single sheet she has charted
the measured positions of the North Magnetic Pole from Ross's 1831
fix to a 2019 reading — the pole's long, slow nineteenth- and
twentieth-century drift north-northwest across the Canadian Arctic,
and then, plainly marked on her graph with a change in the slope of
the line, its acceleration after roughly 1990, from something near
fifteen kilometres a year to something near fifty, racing now not
toward the pole but past it, out over the Arctic Ocean, on a bearing
for Siberia. Beside the post-1990 segment, in the last hand the file
contains, Voss has written five words: *the rock is being moved.*[^46]

[^45]: The verification here is, for once, straightforward, and I
give it without editorial hedging because the papers deserve one
entry where I can. Mercator's 1577 letter to Dee survives and is
held among the Cotton manuscripts at the British Library; it is the
sole source, transcribed and re-transcribed by generations of polar
historians, for everything the sixteenth century believed it knew of
Cnoyen and, through him, of the *Inventio Fortunata*. The *Inventio*
itself is genuinely lost — no copy, fragment, or reliable independent
citation of it has ever surfaced, and the scholarship has long since
stopped expecting one. Ross's attainment of the North Magnetic Pole
on the Boothia Peninsula on 1 June 1831 is documented in his own
published narrative and in the Admiralty's records of the voyage, and
its location — nowhere near 90°N — has never been seriously disputed.
Voss's chain, tedious as it is to state, is accurate at every joint.
I have checked it because it is the one chain in this Atlas I could
still hope would break.

[^46]: I have stopped assuming the late annotations are metaphors,
and I record what follows without further comment because the entry
has earned a plain close. The drift data are real and public: the
World Magnetic Model, maintained jointly by British and American
geological surveys, has tracked the North Magnetic Pole's position
continuously since the 1990s and shows exactly the acceleration Voss
plotted, a shift from roughly 15 km/yr to 50–55 km/yr, the fastest
sustained movement in the historical record. The "geomagnetic jerks"
that punctuate the readings — abrupt, brief changes in the field's
rate of change, clustered more thickly in the recent record than the
old models allow for — are likewise real, published, and, as of this
writing, unexplained. I took the graph to a colleague in earth
sciences at Bristol, gave her nothing but the coordinates, and asked
her to extend Voss's line. The projected track, on current heading,
crosses the meridian 168° west.
