# 26. On Appointments

*[Found, like its predecessor, as loose leaves — laid inside the back
cover of notebook X, in the late hand throughout, with corrections in
no hand I recognise. I print the corrected text. — E.M.]*

At Sarah Ann I wrote that every phantom dies of an appointment, and I
have spent this Part documenting the exceptions, which is the honest
name for a career. Let me now say what an appointment is, since the
word has been doing quiet work for two hundred pages and deserves its
wages.

An appointment is a reconciliation of accounts. The chart says one
thing; the world is presumed to say another; and an hour is named at
which the two ledgers are brought to the same table and the
difference is struck. A sounding is an appointment. An eclipse is an
appointment kept by proxy, with the moon standing auditor. A royal
decree is an appointment at which only one ledger bothers to appear,
and wins. We imagine these reconciliations as moments of truth, and
they are — but note the accounting sense of the word *strike*. The
difference between the ledgers does not evaporate at the audit. It
is struck: written off in one book, written *into* another. Part I
asked where an error lives. I can now answer with the precision of
fifty years: an error lives in the difference column, and the
difference column is conserved, and the appointment is not where the
error dies. It is where the error is transferred.

Consider, as evidence, what this Part has shown the appointment
doing when the stakes were large enough to watch.

The southern continent was audited by the best seaman of his age,
found absent, and written off — and the write-off cleared the ground
so completely that when the genuine article surfaced forty-five
years later it arrived unencumbered, debt-free, wearing the same
name with the *nondum* deleted. The promise did not die at Cook's
audit. It was transferred to a cleaner account.

The polar sea was audited by Nansen with three years of his life,
found absent, written off — and the difference sat in the column for
a century, accruing, until the world warmed enough to pay it. My
successors will sail it. The appointment was kept; we had merely
misread which party owed.

The rock at the pole was audited by the needle itself and found to
be two things rather than one, and the promise changed trains at the
platform, as the great ones do — and the difference column in that
file is still open, and still moving, at fifty kilometres in the
year.

And the station — the station is the entry I would unwrite, because
it shows the machinery from the far side, and there are readers I
would have spared this. The weather was true and the address was
false, and the service, forced to choose, banked the weather and
wrote the address into the difference column, where it has sat since
1960, reporting. Nothing in the record suggests the arrangement is
distressed. The books balance. That is what distresses.

I said at Vienna that the walls are maintained, and was not thanked
for it. This Part completes the sentence I did not finish in that
room. The walls are maintained *because the accounts must balance* —
because somewhere the sum of all the differences ever struck at all
the appointments ever kept is carried on a single ledger, and that
ledger, like any ledger, has a house that keeps it, and the house,
like any house, has premises. The islands were the coins. The
interiors were the walls of the counting-house. The appointments are
the clerks' hours. I have been describing a single institution from
three sides for twenty-six entries, the way a blind woman describes
a building by walking around it, and there remains only the door —
and the reader who has stayed with me knows by now that the doors
are labelled, and knows the label, and knows that I know it.

Two admissions, then, before the last Part, which is not like the
others.

The first. I have implied, by arranging this book as a scholar
arranges evidence, that I assembled the Atlas and then drew the
conclusion. The truth is otherwise and the reader has earned it. In
1974 I received a letter I had no reason to expect, from a
correspondent I have never met, who has signed forty-five years of
answers with a single initial, and whose first letter contained a
sentence I have never printed and will now print once. It read: *You
have been reading the difference column, Professor. Most stop at the
sum.* Everything since — the freighters, the archives, the fifty
years — has been my attempt to earn a sentence I was given free. A
scholar is supposed to follow the evidence. I have spent my life
following an *acknowledgement*, and I set this down because the
distinction is the most important methodological fact about this
book, and because the reader deserves to know that the Atlas is not
a proof. It is a correspondence course, and I was not its author.
I was its best student. There is a difference, and late at night,
aboard, with the engine trembling in the plating, I could sometimes
hear it.

The second admission concerns the blue notebook, whose absence from
my papers the reader will by now have registered, since everything
else I ever wrote is accounted for. The blue notebook is the ledger
of my own differences. Every archive I have entered, every folio
that was present against its catalogue, every depth I have written
down: each one struck a difference between the world as filed and
the world as found, and I have kept the column, as I was taught —
entered in my own book, against my own name. Fifty years of
differences, and I am told the pans must level, and I am told there
is a town where the levelling is done. I do not know what I weigh.
I know the appointment is set, and that it is not an execution; no
audit is. It is a reconciliation. What is left of me after the
difference is struck will depend on which of my entries were true,
and I have been, on the whole and at some cost, a truthful woman.

The last Part of this Atlas concerns the places that have no other
literature: the ones that are not in the archives because they are
mine — found not by reading but by being, at last, read. Six
entries, and a seventh that the reader must not expect to find,
because it is not missing. It is *out*. A book can be out of a
library and still belong to it; the borrower's name is in the
register; the due date is set. I have seen the register. The hand
is old-fashioned, upright, certainly European.

The due date, if the reader must know, is the fourteenth of
September.
