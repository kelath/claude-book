# 17. The Island of California

**From the maps of 1622–1747: an island the length of a subcontinent,
lying off New Spain, separated from the mainland by a strait that is
now several of the United States. Birth certificate: a novel. Death
certificate: a law.**

After the choir and the scales, an entry to lower the pulse — though
the reader should watch it closely precisely because nothing uncanny
happens in it. The Island of California is the control specimen of the
interiors, as Hy-Brasil was of the islands: every mechanism of this
Part operating in the open, on the largest scale in the Atlas, with
all the documents signed and dated. It is the biggest thing ever to be
wrong on a map of the earth, and we know exactly who wrote it, who
believed it, who walked its refutation, and who, finally, repealed it.

It was written — the verb is exact — around 1510, by Garci Rodríguez
de Montalvo, in a chivalric potboiler called *Las Sergas de
Esplandián*: "Know that on the right hand of the Indies there is an
island called California, very close to the side of the Terrestrial
Paradise, peopled by black women without any man among them… their
arms all of gold, as is the harness of the wild beasts they ride."
Queen Calafia, the griffins, the gold — airport fiction of the
conquest, read to rags by exactly the men who were sailing west. When
Cortés's captains touched the tip of Baja in the 1530s and took its
arid thumb for an island, the sailors reached for the name every
literate man aboard already knew. The joke — Voss insists we register
that it began as one, the way a crew names a leaky boat *Invincible* —
stuck, and hardened, and outlived its own punchline by two centuries.

Here is the sequence, and its strangeness. The sixteenth century got
it *right*. Ulloa sailed the gulf to its head in 1539 and found it
closed; the great charts of the century show California correctly, a
peninsula, soldered to the continent. For sixty years the truth held
the maps. And then it lost them.

    IV: This is the entry's gift to the doctrine and I know of no
    other case so clean. We speak of errors as invaders and truth as
    the recovering army, and assume the traffic runs one way. Here
    the truth held the territory first, for two generations, with
    the surveys on its side — and was *dispossessed*. An error
    re-took charted, verified, settled coastline and held it for a
    hundred and twenty years. Correction is not a ratchet. Nothing
    in the record ratchets. The reader who takes one sentence from
    my Atlas into their own affairs is advised to take that one.

The dispossession has a name and a motive. Father Antonio de la
Ascensión, a Carmelite who sailed with Vizcaíno in 1602, came home
persuaded — on hope, on hearsay, on the mirage-grade evidence this
Part has taught the reader to price — that the gulf ran through to
the ocean and California swung free. His account fed the Amsterdam
engravers, who owed the Spanish surveys nothing and the market for
novelty everything; in 1622 the island appears on a Dutch title-page,
and within a generation it is orthodox from London to Rome. The
island California of the seventeenth century is a masterpiece of
confident detail: a coastline of named bays facing a strait no ship
had ever passed, soundings in water no lead had ever touched. Voss's
file preserves her favourite: a chart whose cartouche certifies the
island's outline was "corrected by the latest and best observations,"
which, she notes, was in a sense true — the latest and best
observations of other maps.

    IV: Kong was secreted by a theory; California was secreted by a
    *market*. The engraver's shop is a habitat, and in it an island
    outcompetes a peninsula for the plainest commercial reason: it
    is more interesting, and no customer in Amsterdam will ever test
    the strait. Multiply that shop by a century and you have the
    mechanism — not credulity but *unaccountability*, the gap
    between where a map is made and where it can hurt you. Every
    era builds instruments in that gap. Ours are faster.

The refutation, when it came, came on foot. The Jesuit Eusebio Kino,
missionary in the Sonoran desert, grew suspicious in the 1690s on
grounds that do him honour as an empiricist: the Cocomaricopa Indians
of the Gila, who had no stake whatever in European hydrography, kept
giving him blue shells from the Pacific coast, and trading them along
routes that crossed no water. Shells do not swim straits. Kino made
five overland journeys, reached the head of the gulf, watched the
land close it like a proof concluding, and drew the map he published
under the title that says everything with Jesuit economy: *California:
Land of Passage, Not an Island*. That was 1705 or so. The reader,
trained by Binger and Humboldt, will now expect the island to die of
the walk.

It did not. That is the entry's second lesson: the island survived
its own walk-through by forty years. Kino's map circulated; the
engravers thanked him and reprinted the island; atlases offered both
versions, sometimes in the same volume, priced identically. It took
the bluntest instrument in the history of geography to end the
matter: in 1747 Ferdinand VI of Spain issued a royal decree — an
actual law, over an actual signature — proclaiming, in four words
that have delighted every historian who has ever quoted them,
*California no es isla*. California is not an island. The strait was
repealed. The maps, which had ignored the surveys and ignored the
missionary, obeyed the king within a decade or two — though Voss,
who audited these deaths, found the island alive on a French map of
1765, and on a Japanese one, faithfully copied from old Dutch stock,
as late as 1865.

    IV: Set the death certificates of this Atlas side by side. A
    sounding killed Brasil; an eclipse killed Sarah Ann; a walk
    killed Kong. California — bigger than all of them together —
    could not be killed by evidence at all. It required an act of
    sovereignty: the one instrument whose authority operates in the
    same medium as the error's own, paper upon paper, seal against
    seal. The king did not inform the map-makers. He *outranked*
    them. And the entry closes the circle the guano act opened at
    Sarah Ann: paper can annex a phantom, and paper can execute
    one, and at no point in the two hundred and fifty years of the
    island's life — this is the fact I set the entry down to
    preserve — was the land itself a party to the proceedings. The
    strait was opened by a novelist and closed by a king, and
    between them the desert lay where it had always lain, unwatered
    and unconsulted, bearing its blue shells.[^31]

One item from the file, for the long accounts.
Among Voss's tracings is the strait itself, isolated — just the
water, the imaginary Mediterranean between island and main, which
seventeenth-century convention labelled the Mar Vermejo or Mar de
California. On her tracing Voss has written the width of the strait
as the principal charts give it, and the figure varies, chart to
chart, between one hundred and one hundred and sixty leagues — with
one exception. The Milanese atlas of 1849, the reader's old
acquaintance, which everywhere else copies its coastlines from
sober French stock, alone preserves the island California a
century after the decree, and alone gives the strait a sounding.
The sounding is 680 fathoms.[^32]

[^31]: The decree is real and the wording is as quoted; the 1765
French and 1865 Japanese survivals are documented in the standard
census of California-as-island maps, which runs to several hundred
distinct plates. All shelfmarks in this entry check. I note it
because I have noticed, reviewing my own apparatus, that I now
report verification the way a nervous man reports his pulse.

[^32]: 680 fathoms is 1,244 metres. I have nothing to add that the
reader has not already done in the margin.
