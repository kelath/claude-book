# 19. The Kingdom of Saguenay

The interior phantoms so far were composed by theorists, markets,
committees, and kings. The Kingdom of Saguenay was composed by a
prisoner, for an audience of one, as the price of going home — and it
is the entry where the Atlas's cost column and its authorship column
finally meet in a single man.

The man was Donnacona, headman of Stadacona, the Iroquoian town that
stood where Quebec City stands. In 1535 Jacques Cartier wintered his
ships below the town, uninvited; a quarter of his crew died of scurvy
and the rest were saved by the town's spruce-bark remedy; and in the
spring, wanting something to lay before his king besides survival,
Cartier took the remedy's givers with him. He lured Donnacona and
several of his people aboard and sailed for France. None of them ever
saw the St. Lawrence again.

What Donnacona did in France is the entry. Presented at the court of
François I — a captive expected to perform his captor's justification
— the old man discovered, with a diplomat's precision, the only
currency that could buy a westbound passage: geography. He testified
— through interpreters, before notaries, to the king himself — of the
kingdom that lay up the dark river that entered the great one at
Tadoussac: Saguenay, a realm of "immense quantities of gold, rubies
and other rich things," where the men were white as Frenchmen and
dressed in wool. Pressed, he furnished the marvels the age required —
lands of one-legged people, of pygmies, of men with wings — always,
the notarial records agree, with unshakable composure; he was, said
one witness, more believed than a prophet. The king, at war and in
debt, believed what it was profitable to believe. A great expedition
was prepared. Donnacona was promised to it as guide.

He died first. In France, around 1539, of what the records do not
dignify with a cause — an old man four years into an exile that had
begun as a kidnapping, his ransom-kingdom still unredeemed. The French
buried him, the record does not say where, and sailed anyway.

    IV: Consider the transaction with the coldness it deserves,
    because pity obscures the mechanism. Every phantom in this Part
    was secreted by an interest — a theory, a market, a family, a
    crown. Saguenay was secreted by *duress*: its author was the
    hostage of its audience. Donnacona drew a kingdom at the exact
    bearing that pointed his captors' appetite away from his own
    town and along the road home; its wealth was calibrated to be
    worth a fleet; its marvels were quoted from the captors' own
    library — he had heard what Frenchmen believed the world
    contained, and he sold it back to them at the price of a
    passage. It was testimony as cartography and cartography as
    ransom, and it failed only because the hostage's body gave out
    before the fleet did. I know of no purer demonstration that the
    third place is, among its other functions, the bank of last
    resort of the powerless: a man with nothing left to draw on may
    still draw a map.

The Cartier–Roberval expedition of 1541–43 — France's largest
transatlantic effort to that date — went up the river to find the
kingdom without its author. It found the rapids above Montreal
island, which it could not pass; it wintered badly and quarrelled
worse; and at Cap Rouge its men loaded the ships with the famous
cargo: barrels of gold that assayed in Paris as iron pyrite, and
diamonds by the basket that were quartz. The kingdom of Saguenay
had converted, at the moment of audit, into the only wealth a
phantom can deliver — the appearance of wealth, in commercial
quantities. France, disgusted, abandoned Canada for two
generations, and the expression *faux comme un diamant du Canada* —
false as a Canadian diamond — entered the language, where it
remains: the one entry in this Atlas that survives as an idiom.

    IV: And note what the false diamonds purchased, since the
    account must be read to its foot. Two generations of French
    absence — two generations in which the towns of the river lived
    unconquered. If Saguenay was a fiction spent to buy time, it
    bought sixty years, which is more than most true kingdoms have
    bought with real armies. The map-makers kept the kingdom on the
    charts for another century, rich and wool-clad, up its dark
    river; the people who had actually paid for it are not named on
    any of those sheets. The reader may decide whose error it was,
    and I give my finding: it was not Donnacona's. His geography
    performed to specification. Ours is the profession that filed
    a ransom note under "discoveries."[^35]

The kingdom faded from the maps as New France filled in — no decree,
no walk-through; it simply had nowhere left to be, and by 1700 the
name had shrunk to what it is today: a river, a fjord, a region of
granite and cold water north of Quebec, which tourists visit for
the whales. The fjord is, incidentally and finally, the deepest
water of its kind on the continent: the drowned valley plunges,
within sight of the villages, to some 275 metres — dark, cold,
layered, its bottom water renewed only rarely from the sea. Voss
closes the file with the sounding and, beneath it, the last line of
the entry proper:

    IV: Not in the band; the band was never the point; the point,
    as the next paper argues, is what the walls are for. He put his
    kingdom up a river with a wall of rapids at its door, where no
    keel he had ever seen could follow. He understood the grammar
    of the category before we had named it: a place is safe in
    exact proportion as it is unvisitable. File under walls. The
    essay follows.[^36]

[^35]: The notarial record of Donnacona's testimony survives in
summary; the pyrite-and-quartz débâcle is standard history; the
idiom is in Littré. All shelfmarks check. The reader will notice
the entry contains nothing impossible — no misfiled ink, no
anomalous folio. I notice it too. The files that concern what
powerful men did to the defenceless are, in my experience of this
archive, always in perfect order.

[^36]: "The essay follows": Voss's own cross-reference, and the
