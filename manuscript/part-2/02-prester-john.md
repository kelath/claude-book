# 14. The Kingdom of Prester John

**Position: the Three Indias (12th c.); beyond the Tigris (13th); the
Mountains of the Moon (14th); Ethiopia (15th–17th). A kingdom with a
forwarding address.**

The Mountains of Kong were a phantom you could not reach. The kingdom
of Prester John was stranger: a phantom you could not reach, but which
you could *write to* — and which, once, wrote first.

It began with a letter. Around the year 1165 there appeared in Europe —
"appeared" is the scholars' word, and it is doing the work of a
confession — a long epistle addressed to the Emperor of Byzantium,
purporting to come from one Presbyter Johannes: priest and king,
Christian of the East, ruler of the Three Indias. The letter is a
gorgeous, preposterous object. Its author governs seventy-two kings; in
his lands are the Fountain of Youth and the sandy sea that flows without
water; before his palace hangs a mirror in which he beholds everything
done in every province; his table is emerald; among his subjects are
the Amazons and the Brahmins and men with horns; and he is served at
dinner, in the detail every medieval reader remembered, by seven kings,
sixty dukes, and three hundred and sixty-five counts, one for each day
of the year — a bureaucracy of time itself waiting at table.

No original exists. No author was ever identified. The Greek text it
claims descent from has never been found; the thing surfaces already
translated, already copied, already believed. Within a generation there
were hundreds of manuscripts; within two, versions in every literary
language of Europe, each translator adding provinces and marvels the
way the atlas-makers would later add snow to Kong.

    IV: Compare the birth certificates. Kong was signed by the finest
    geographer of his age, deducing in good faith. The Letter of
    Prester John is signed by its own subject — the phantom introduces
    *itself*, unforwarded, in the first person, with an inventory of
    its contents. In five centuries of material I know no other case
    where the third place opens the correspondence.

Christendom answered. That is the fact around which Voss builds the
entry, and she asks the reader to sit with its full administrative
weight. In 1177, Pope Alexander III — a pope in the middle of his wars
with an emperor, a man with no leisure for whimsy — composed a formal
reply to Prester John: cordial, cautious on points of doctrine,
proposing closer relations. He entrusted it to his personal physician,
Philip, who departed to deliver it.

To deliver it *where* is the question the file turns on. The Letter
bears no address. The Three Indias were a rhetorical region, not a
route. Philip went east with a letter for a kingdom whose location was
the letter itself — and Philip does not return. No record of his
arrival anywhere; no record of his death; the correspondence of the
papal court simply ceases to mention him. The reply to Prester John is
the only document in this Atlas that was successfully dispatched to a
phantom place, and its carrier is the only man who can be said,
strictly, to have been delivered with it.

    IV: The office is instructive. A letter to an unplaceable address
    requires a carrier willing to make the address exist by arriving —
    the delivery is the discovery, or it is nothing. Alexander
    understood this; it is why he sent a man he trusted with his own
    body. I have wondered for forty years what instructions Philip
    carried beside the letter. I have wondered longer than that
    whether the office was ever afterward vacant.[^25]

For the next five hundred years, the kingdom behaved exactly as the
Third Law requires of a migratory phantom: it kept one province beyond
the surveyed. When the Mongol embassies opened Asia and no priest-king
sat beyond the Tigris, the kingdom was rediscovered further east, in
the person of various steppe khans garbled into Christians by hope and
distance. When Asia filled in entirely, the kingdom executed the most
remarkable manoeuvre in the history of imaginary geography: it changed
continents. Fourteenth-century map-makers, needing somewhere for a
Christian empire that letters had established beyond argument, settled
on Ethiopia — where, by the grace of coincidence, a genuinely ancient
Christian kingdom genuinely was. For three centuries the maps of
Africa carry *Preste Juan* enthroned among the sources of the Nile,
and the fiction now had a real state to wear as a body.

The Portuguese, being the century's most literal-minded people, sent
embassies. Pêro da Covilhã reached the Ethiopian court in the 1490s,
overland, in disguise, carrying his king's letters to Prester John —
and here the file produces its second detained correspondent, for the
Ethiopian court received Covilhã graciously, found him useful, and
declined, for the remaining thirty years of his life, to let him
leave. A later embassy found him old, honoured, married, weeping at
the sight of countrymen, and still not permitted to go home. The
kingdom that could not be reached had become a kingdom that could not
be left — Voss notes that these are the same property, observed from
opposite banks.

    IV: The Ethiopians, for their part, were bewildered and then
    diplomatic. Letters kept arriving for a man who was not their
    king, from monarchs too powerful to correct. So they answered
    them. For two centuries a real state conducted the correspondence
    of an imaginary one — signing, in effect, the phantom's mail —
    because geopolitics had made the fiction indispensable to both
    parties. The reader who thinks this quaint is invited to consider
    how much of the world's current mail is answered on behalf of
    entities that exist in precisely this sense.

The kingdom died, as interior phantoms do, slowly and of scholarship.
The Jesuits in Ethiopia reported a poor, embattled, entirely human
realm; the philologist Hiob Ludolf demonstrated in the 1680s that
"Prester John" had never been an Ethiopian title at all; the name
retreated from the maps a province at a time, and by the eighteenth
century the priest-king had completed the standard descent from
geography to literature. What did not die was the Letter. It had
always been the kingdom's true territory — the one province no
expedition could enter and no philologist could empty — and Voss's
entry ends not with the maps but with the manuscript count, which is
its own kind of chart: over four hundred copies surviving, in a dozen
languages, which is to say four hundred surviving instances of an
address at which, for half a millennium, the largest civilisation on
earth genuinely expected an answer.

    IV: The Letter is the purest phantom in the Atlas because it
    dispenses with terrain altogether. No coast, no sounding, no
    walk-through can touch it; it is all interior. And it teaches
    the final mechanism, the one the islands could only hint at:
    a place needs no position if it can maintain a correspondence.
    Answer promptly, in a fair hand, with convincing detail, and
    you may omit to exist for five hundred years. — I set this down
    and my own files reproach me. I am aware, none better, of what
    it is to trust a correspondent one has never located.[^26]

[^25]: Voss's question about the vacancy of Philip's office is
rhetorical in the notebook. I am unable to leave it rhetorical. Of the
seventy-one letters from T. in the Voss papers, eleven bear postmarks
— it is by these that the correspondence can be dated and placed at
all, Reykjavík to Veracruz to the Aleutians. The other sixty were
carried, not posted: no stamps, no cancellations, envelopes of varying
national manufactures, tucked — according to the annotations Voss
sometimes made on the versos — into hotel pigeonholes, ships' mail
sacks, a returned library book, and once "the usual place, Tórshavn."
A correspondence conducted five parts in six by hand delivery implies
either proximity I can find no other trace of, or carriers. I record the two possibilities in order of the comfort
they offer.

[^26]: The last sentence of this extract is the only place in the
notebooks where Voss acknowledges what her own Atlas repeatedly
demonstrates: that the T. correspondence conforms, point for point,
to the anatomy of the Prester John file — the unplaceable address,
the convincing detail, the answers that arrive by hands unseen. She
saw the resemblance; she wrote it down; she continued the
correspondence for another two decades. I used to read that as
credulity. Editing has taught me the other reading: she had checked
the anatomy against the specimen and concluded, as I am slowly
concluding, that the resemblance is not a warning about the
correspondent. It is information about the address.
