# 13. The Mountains of Kong

**c. 10°N, from the Gulf of Guinea eastward toward the Nile. Length as
charted: up to four thousand kilometres. Height as charted: "stupendous."
Actual: —**

The islands of Part I were, at their grandest, the size of a county.
We begin the interiors with a phantom the size of a mountain system —
a range that ran like a wall across the whole of West Africa, on
essentially every serious map of that continent published in the
nineteenth century, and that does not exist, and never did, at any
point along its four thousand charted kilometres.

The Mountains of Kong were born in 1798, and unusually for this Atlas,
they have a birth certificate with a signature on it. The signer was
Major James Rennell — the first Surveyor-General of Bengal, the father
of English geography, the most respected map-maker of his age; it
matters to everything that follows that he was not a fraud and not a
fool. Rennell was engaged to draw the maps accompanying the narrative
of Mungo Park, then newly returned as the first European to have laid
eyes on the middle Niger. Park's journal mentioned, in passing,
distant high ground south of his route — he had seen hills; there are
hills. Rennell took the hills and did to them what a theorist does to
a fact that fits: he promoted them. His accompanying memoir reasoned
that the Niger, flowing eastward as Park confirmed, could not possibly
reach the Atlantic — some barrier must pen it into the interior; and a
barrier being needed, the hills Park glimpsed became its western
outriders, and the range extended eastward across the continent
because the theory required it to be long. He named it from the
trading city of Kong, north of the Gulf coast, and drew it with the
confident hachures of the period — and here the reader must understand
what Rennell's hachures meant to the trade: they meant *settled*.
Lesser houses copied Rennell the way parishes copy scripture.

    IV: Note the mechanism, because it is new to us. The island
    phantoms of Part I were errors of the eye — pumice, mirage, ice,
    longing. Kong is an error of the *argument*. Nobody ever reported
    the range. The range was deduced. It is the purest specimen I know
    of a class that rules the interiors: geography secreted by theory,
    as a shell is secreted by the animal — from inside.

And it grew. Follow the range through the century's atlases, as Voss
did — her Kong file contains tracings from over forty, arranged by
date, and flipping them in sequence is like watching a culture in a
petri dish. In 1802 the range is a modest ridge behind the Guinea
coast. By the 1820s it has thickened and sprouted spurs. In some
atlases of the 1830s and 40s it marches east until it fuses with that
other great inherited fiction, the Mountains of the Moon, so that a
single unbroken wall crosses Africa from the Atlantic to the Nile —
you may see it so on maps sold in the year the telegraph reached
India. Textbooks gave the Kong range snow-caps. Gazetteers gave it
gold. One French geography of mid-century, in a detail Voss underlined
twice, gave it inhabitants: hardy mountaineers, of whom precisely as
much was known as of the granite they herded their goats on, which is
to say the sum of European knowledge of both was zero.

    IV: Snow at ten degrees north, on a range no one had seen, in
    books used to examine schoolboys. I want the reader to feel how
    little malice was involved. Every copyist improved the range in
    good conscience, because mountains ought to have snow, and a wall
    across Africa ought to be tall enough to deserve the name. An
    error, once load-bearing, attracts ornament. We do not merely
    tolerate our fictions; we *maintain* them, the way a town maintains
    its bridge.

The bridge was load-bearing in the strictest sense. For ninety years
the Mountains of Kong did real work in the world's understanding of
Africa: they explained the Niger (falsely); they discouraged trade
routes inland from the Guinea coast (routes that were, in fact, open
and walked by African merchants the whole while); they anchored the
century's confident division of the continent into climatic and racial
"zones" (the less said of which the better, though Voss says it, at
length, in a passage I have placed in the appendices). Real expeditions
budgeted for crossing them. Real geographies taught generations that
the interior was sealed. The wall was imaginary and it kept people out.

    IV: Part I asked what a phantom is for, and the interiors sharpen
    the question, because an interior phantom is not a mark to steer
    by; it is a *premise*. The islands were coins. The ranges are
    walls, and a wall's work is done precisely when nobody tests it.
    Kong stood for ninety years because its function was to make the
    journey that would disprove it seem not worth the taking. The
    best-defended fictions are the ones that guard their own
    counter-evidence.[^23]

The man who finally took the journey was Louis-Gustave Binger, a
French officer of no great fame, who in 1887–89 descended from the
upper Niger southward — directly through the coordinates where every
atlas in Europe stacked its hachures — on his way to the city of Kong
itself. His account of the crossing is the great anticlimax of
nineteenth-century exploration, and Voss quotes it with visible
relish: "On the horizon, not even a ridge of hills!" Binger walked
for weeks across open savanna, gentle rises, a country you could
drive a cart over, occupied by farmers and traders who were
understandably unaware they lived beneath a stupendous snow-capped
cordillera. He reached Kong the city — real, prosperous, a market
town of clay mosques and cloth merchants — was received courteously,
and wrote the sentence that killed the mountains: there are none.
He announced it to the Geographical Society in Paris in 1890, and
the range that had taken ninety years to grow came off the maps in
roughly five — though Voss, who audited the die-off atlas by atlas,
found stragglers: a Rand McNally of 1905 still carries the range,
and she traced one English school atlas reprinting a ghost of it
into the 1920s, teaching the wall to children whose fathers could
have taken the train past its coordinates.

    IV: Observe the asymmetry of the two deaths in this file. The
    mountains died of one man's walk and were mourned by no one.
    The city — the real thing, the market and the mosques and the
    name that had been lent to all that imaginary granite — was
    burned to the ground seven years after Binger's visit, in the
    wars of Samory Touré, and rebuilt small, and it faded from the
    European map hand in hand with the range. By 1930 an educated
    European asked about "Kong" would recall neither. The fiction
    and its collateral were disposed of together, and the map-makers
    kept what they always keep: the blank, and the confidence.

There the published argument ends, and the file should close. The
reader of this edition has learned to wait a paragraph longer.

Among the tracings in the Kong folder — the forty atlases, arranged
by date — there is one Voss marks with a red slip. It is her tracing
of the range from a Milanese atlas of 1849, and it differs from its
neighbours in one particular: a pass. Where every other atlas draws
the wall unbroken, the Milanese engraver has left a gap — narrow,
deliberate, flanked by the range's only two named peaks in any
edition — and through the gap he has drawn, in the faint double line
that the period used for caravan routes, a road. No route into the
interior existed on any source map he could have copied. The pass has
a label, in type so small Voss's tracing reproduces it with a
magnifier's note. It reads *Passo del Coro*.[^24]

[^23]: This footnote records a debt. Voss's argument here — that
interior phantoms persist by making their own falsification
expensive — was published by her in a 1971 paper that was badly
received, in terms her file of referee reports preserves. One
referee wrote that she "mistakes the sociology of error for a
property of the places themselves," which the reader will recognise
as precisely the confusion, or precisely the insight, on which this
entire Atlas balances. The referee signed with an initial only. The
initial was not T. I checked.

[^24]: Coro: the choir. The reader met the word four entries ago,
attached to an island two oceans away, in an entry I let pass
without apparatus. I had a reason then and it has not improved with
keeping: the reason is that I first read the Isla del Coro entry in
Bergen, in 2021, and I verified the Milanese atlas — it exists;
the pass is there; the label is there, 6-point, in the copy held at
the Braidense — in the spring of 2024. Three years apart, an
invented island and an unbroken wall's only gap, wearing the same
name. Voss's red slip carries one line in the late hand: "the doors
are labelled. They were always labelled. We catalogued the walls."
