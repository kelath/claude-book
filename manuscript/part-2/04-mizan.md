# 16. Mīzān

**Reported between the passes: Persia to China by the southern silk
routes, four to eleven days beyond the last town whose name the
witnesses agree on. Altitude "where the water boils quiet." Never
sought successfully. Never encountered twice.**

*[This entry's file was found sealed — wax over string, unbroken, the
only sealed item among the four thousand. On the wrapper, in the early
brown ink: "Not to be read before Guatavita is understood." Her final
index places it here, immediately after El Dorado. I broke the seal on
11 November 2024, and I record that my hands were not entirely steady,
and that the file contained no horror at all: only the gentlest
material in the papers. The reader may judge whether that is a relief.
— E.M.]*

The caravan literature of the overland silk trade is a genre of
practicalities: stages, wells, fodder, tolls, which headmen must be
gifted and which merely greeted. It does not deal in wonders; a
route-book that lied got its copyists killed at one remove. And it is
in this hardest-headed of genres — in route-books compiled centuries
apart, in three languages, by merchants with no access to one another —
that Voss found the town of the scales.

The earliest witness is a Persian safarnāma of the twelfth century,
whose anonymous author records, between two stages otherwise devoted to
the badness of the water, the following: that four days beyond
Sang-i-Safid the road rises through a defile to a town not listed by
the customs-masters, where the caravan is met, courteously, and every
load is weighed. Nothing is taxed. Nothing is opened. The weighing is
conducted on a beam balance of great size and greater age, each pan
"broad enough for a kneeling camel," and the results are entered in a
ledger by clerks described as neither young nor old. Asked the purpose
of a toll that takes nothing, the clerks answer — the author gives the
answer in the tone of a man transcribing what he does not claim to
understand — *the toll is the weighing.*

An Armenian merchant's manual of the seventeenth century — the genre's
masterpiece, elsewhere accurate to the last abbas of every real toll
from Julfa to Lhasa — carries the town under the name Mīzān, "the
Scales," with the flat annotation: "Here the goods are weighed and no
duty taken. Enter what the clerks tell you in your own book, for their
figure and yours will not agree, and theirs is correct." And a Jesuit
lay brother, crossing toward the Chinese missions in 1689, writes home
the version that has kept the file alive in three centuries of
marginalia: that his small caravan was weighed at a high cold town of
no visible livelihood; that the clerks weighed the men as well as the
bales, "with neither insolence nor explanation"; and that his servant,
a boy taken on at the last river town, was weighed both on entering
and on leaving — the only member of the party so honoured — and that
the clerks compared the two figures, and recorded something, and the
senior of them said to the boy, in passable Portuguese, words the
brother swears to: *the difference is entered against the house.*

    IV: Three witnesses; three centuries; three languages; one
    institution, described identically: the courtesy, the great
    balance, the ledger, the refusal of payment. And note what is
    absent, in a genre that records everything saleable: no one
    ever buys anything in Mīzān, sells anything, sleeps there, or
    is asked to. It has no economy. It has only an *instrument*.
    The route-books, which grade every town by what can be got
    there, could not grade it, and so entered it the way a ledger
    enters an auditor: without a price, and without affection, and
    without the least doubt of its authority.

No expedition ever found it. That statement means less on the roof of
the world than at sea — whole real towns have hidden in those folds
for centuries — but the pattern in the file is not the failure; it is
its shape. Mīzān is reported only by parties bound elsewhere, moving
laden, in the ordinary anxiety of the trade. Every deliberate attempt
— and Voss documents four, from a Qing revenue survey to a Royal
Geographical Society traveller of the 1890s who made three seasons of
it — approached empty, so to speak: unburdened, purposeful, seeking
the town for the town's sake. None so much as found the defile. The
reader has met this etiquette before, at the Auroras: *you do not find
them by looking. You find them on the way to something worse* — or,
Voss amends here, on the way to something heavier.

    IV: Sit with the mechanism of Part I a moment, and see how this
    town inverts it. Our audits kill phantoms: the sounding, the
    walk-through, the eclipse — we name an hour, the place fails the
    appointment, the chart is cleansed. Mīzān is the traffic running
    the other way. It audits *us*. It names no hour; it waits on the
    road it knows we must use; it takes no toll but the figure; and
    the caravan passes on, lighter by nothing, heavier by an entry.
    For fifty years I have called the third place a bank on the
    evidence of its deposits. I was slow. A bank is not made of
    vaults. It is made of *records*, and somewhere the records are
    weighed, and the weighing has a town.[^29]

The sealed file's last item is a single sheet in the late shaking
hand, and it concerns the sign — the small circle divided by a line,
which the reader has now met cut into a cairn on Grimsholm's shore and
drawn in the margin of the La Palma inquiry. I had assumed, as I
believe Voss long assumed, that it was an emblem: the disc of Brasil,
the divided island, the shape of the category. The sheet corrects us
both, in eleven words:

    IV: Fifty years I took it for a shape. It is an instrument. A
    beam, and two pans, seen end-on. The sign is not the picture of
    an island. It is the picture of a balance, and it is drawn at
    the places where the weighing is done. — And below this, sole
    occupant of the bottom half of the page: *They have weighed me
    twice now. I do not know what the difference was. I know it was
    entered against the house.*[^30]

[^29]: The route-books exist. The safarnāma is catalogued in a Tehran
collection; the Armenian manual survives in multiple copies; the
Jesuit letter is in the Society's Roman archive. I verified one — the
Armenian manual, in the Bodleian's copy, in January 2025. The Mīzān
annotation stands on folio 214, where Voss cites it. I report, because
this edition has made a discipline of reporting, that the Bodleian's
own 1888 catalogue collates that copy as *lacking folio 214*, and has
never been corrected, and the folio is there, and it does not have the
look of a replacement. I no longer say of such findings that the
archive is wrong. I say that the account is current.

[^30]: Weighed *twice now*. The sheet is undated. If the sign marks
the places where the weighing is done, then the reader possesses, as
I do, a short list of the places Iris Voss may have stood to be
weighed, and one of them is an island that does not exist off the
coast of Iceland, and the other is a strait of fog in the Caribbean
where she waited nine nights for the door to open. I have begun — I
record this as fact, not decision — to keep my own figures. On the
11th of November 2024 I weighed sixty-one kilograms and some grams.
The scale is a good one. I bought it new. I keep the ticket.
