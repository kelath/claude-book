# 15. El Dorado

**A man (c. 1535); a city (c. 1541); a lake (c. 1595). Final charted
position: Lake Parime, 3°N, 60°W. Actual position: see final paragraph.**

Every phantom in this Atlas has cost something — ships' time, treaty
lines, a man on the ice. El Dorado is the entry where the cost column
becomes a casualty list. Across three centuries it consumed expeditions
the way its imagined mines consumed labour: thousands of Europeans and
uncounted, because unrecorded, thousands of the people they conscripted,
starved beside, and did not bury. It is the deadliest error in the
history of maps, and Voss's file on it is the angriest in the papers —
and the entry she built from that file turns, in its last movement, into
something none of the others attempt: an accusation of the audit itself.

Begin, as she insists we begin, with the fact almost everyone has
forgotten: El Dorado was not a place. The name is a job description.
*El hombre dorado*, the gilded man — Spanish shorthand, picked up in the
1530s around the new settlements of Colombia, for a ceremony reported
from the high lagoons of the Muisca: at the accession of a new lord, his
body gummed with resin and powdered head to foot with gold dust, rafted
to the centre of a mountain lake at dawn with braziers burning, where he
washed the gold from his skin into the water while his people threw in
offerings of worked gold and emeralds from the shores. The reports were
true. The ceremony was real; the lake was real — Guatavita, a drowned
crater two days' ride from modern Bogotá; the gold was real, and it went
where the ceremony sent it: into the water.

    IV: Hold the image, because the whole catastrophe is already inside
    it. The first Spaniards to hear the story understood it perfectly
    and could not afford to. A civilisation that gilds a man in order
    to *wash the gold off* — that mines treasure in order to sink it —
    is a civilisation whose ledger runs in the opposite direction, and
    the report of it arrived among men who had crossed an ocean on
    credit. They did the only thing their arithmetic permitted: they
    reversed the sign. Deposit became vault. Offering became hoard.
    The gilded man, who was an instrument for putting gold *into* the
    dark, was rebuilt in the European imagination as a king who could
    be made to give it back.

What follows, once the sign is reversed, follows like weather. A
deposit implies a depositor; a depositor implies a capital; and the
gilded man acquired, in the telling, first a city — Manoa, golden-
roofed — and then, because a city must stand somewhere, a lake to
stand beside: Parime, a vast inland sea in the Guiana highlands, which
swelled on the charts through the seventeenth century to the size of
the Caspian. The phantom had executed the manoeuvre Voss's Part I
reader knows as migration, but in a new dimension: not across the map
but *up the chain of abstraction* — man to city to lake, each stage
retreating one audit further from the original, checkable, true thing.

    IV: This is the Fifth Law, and the interiors alone could have
    taught it: a phantom under pursuit does not merely move its
    position; it moves its *category*. Audit the man and you will be
    referred to his city. Audit the city and you will be referred to
    its lake. Every verification is answered with a forwarding
    address one ontological floor up, where the light is worse.

The pursuits themselves Voss recounts with a coroner's economy, and I
compress her compression. Gonzalo Pizarro left Quito in 1541 with two
hundred and twenty Spaniards and four thousand conscripted highlanders
to find the golden country east of the Andes; the expedition ate its
horses, then its dogs, then its dead, and its lieutenant Orellana,
sent ahead for food, was taken by the current and carried involuntarily
down the entire length of the Amazon — the greatest river on earth
discovered as a *side effect*, by men looking for something that was
not there. Philipp von Hutten led the Welser company's hunt out of
Venezuela and came back to be beheaded with a blunt machete by a rival
claimant of the very governance the gold would have justified. Antonio
de Berrío, who spent three fortunes and thirty years narrowing the
search to the Guiana highlands, had the misfortune of being captured
and debriefed by Walter Raleigh — who took Berrío's lifetime of field
notes, added English marketing, and produced *The Discoverie of Guiana*,
the most consequential real-estate prospectus ever written: Manoa the
golden city, Parime its lake, an empire awaiting only investors. Raleigh
went back in 1617 to make the book true. His son was killed storming a
Spanish fort; the gold was not there because it had never been there;
and Raleigh returned to London to be executed under the suspended
sentence his prospectus had been written to lift. The phantom, Voss
notes, had by then developed the distinguishing habit of the interior
class: it did not merely fail to be found; it *specifically killed the
people who wrote it down*.

Parime outlived them all on the maps — a sea the size of a nation,
plotted from testimony extracted at the end of chains of translation
and torture — until Humboldt walked and paddled the region around 1800
and dissolved it into what it had always been: the seasonal flooding of
the Rupununi savanna, a country that becomes a mirror of water for some
months and dries to grass. The greatest of the interior phantoms died
exactly as the interlude predicted: not by sounding but by a walk, the
walker armed with instruments and patience, and the lake went off the
charts leaving the hole that interior phantoms leave. Travellers kept
reporting its glimmer for another generation.

But Guatavita — the small, real, original lake, the scene of the true
ceremony — Guatavita could be audited. That is the terminal movement of
Voss's entry, and she structures it as the trial it was. The audits
began within one generation of the conquest: in the 1580s the merchant
Antonio de Sepúlveda put Indian labour to cutting a drainage notch in
the crater rim — the gash is visible today — lowered the lake some
metres, recovered gold enough to whet every appetite that followed, and
died poor. Attempts continued at intervals of roughly a lifetime for
three hundred years, culminating in 1912, when a London syndicate
called Contractors Ltd. — the name wants no ornament — piped the lake
empty with steam pumps. They stood at last on the floor of the oldest
bank in the Americas. The mud was four feet of ooze; it yielded a
scatter of votive gold; and then the Andean sun dried the exposed
sediment to the hardness of poured concrete, sealing the deposit
beyond their machinery, and the lake quietly refilled, and the company
auctioned its findings at Sotheby's for a sum that did not cover the
pumps, and went into liquidation.[^27]

    IV: Read the 1912 record as the ceremony's answer, four centuries
    delayed. The lake was asked, formally, with engines, the question
    the gilded man had been settling every accession morning: *whose
    is the gold in the dark water?* And the account was — I use the
    banker's word deliberately — *frozen*. Not emptied; frozen. The
    deposit is still there, under mud gone to stone, under water gone
    back to stillness, under law: the Colombian state has since closed
    the lake to salvage entirely, so that the ceremony's intention is
    now enforced by statute of the successor polity. Sunk wealth
    stays sunk. Every party to the transaction has kept its side
    except the auditors, and the auditors are all dead or in
    liquidation. — The reader of my Part I asked, at Emerald, what
    the bank is. I do not yet say what the bank is. But Guatavita is
    a teller's window, and it is shut, and the shutting of it is the
    one event in this entire Atlas that every witness — Muisca,
    Spanish, English, geological — agrees actually happened.[^28]

[^27]: The Contractors Ltd. episode is documented to the shareholder
level and checks throughout; the British Museum holds items from the
Sotheby's sale. I note for the reader keeping the cost column that the
company's liquidation file lists among its creditors a Bogotá firm of
carters, unpaid, for "the return haulage of pumping plant" — the
machinery of the audit itself could not afford to leave.

[^28]: Voss visited Guatavita once, in 1977, and the notebook page is
mostly a sketch: the crater, the colonial notch, the waterline. Beneath
it she has written a single sentence I withheld from the entry proper
because it belongs to the reader who has come this far: "The only
phantom place in the Atlas is the vault they believed in; the deposit
was real; we have the sign reversed on every chart but this one."
