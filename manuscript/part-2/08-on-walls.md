# 20. On Walls

*[Entry 20 differs from everything before it. It is the only paper in
the Atlas that was delivered aloud: to the symposium of a learned
society concerned with the history of cartography, in Vienna, in April
1988. The society's printed proceedings list the paper as withdrawn.
It was not withdrawn; it was given, to a full room, and the society
elected afterwards not to print it, and the minute-book records the
session as having ended early "by agreement." What survives is Voss's
typescript, and a cassette recording made — against the society's
rules — by a doctoral student in the third row, whose estate returned
it to Voss's papers in 2017 with a note reading only "she was right
about the acoustics." I print the paper from the typescript, cut for
length, and the end of the question period from the tape, verbatim.
— E.M.]*

—

…I have shown you, then, three walls. A mountain range four thousand
kilometres long that detained the geography of a continent for ninety
years. A wall of rapids that turned back the largest fleet France ever
sent west, guarding a kingdom composed expressly to be guarded by it.
And a wall of fog that stands off a certain latitude of the Caribbean
— I see some of you smiling; the smile is in the minutes of every
institution that ever filed one of these reports, and the reports
outlast the institutions — a wall of fog that opens, according to
three centuries of depositions, from one side only.

My colleagues, we have all been trained on the same catechism. The
blank spaces on the map are ignorance; the walls we draw at their
edges are confessions; cartography advances by demolition, and the
history of our science is the history of walls coming down. I have
spent thirty-five years in the demolition records — I believe I may
say without immodesty that I have read more of them than anyone in
this room — and I am here to report what is actually in them.

What is in them is maintenance.

The walls of the phantom literature are not ruins and they do not
behave like confessions. They are pointed. Kong's hachures thicken
precisely where the trade routes press; the fog of San Borondón is
punctual to the hour of the offices; the category "not for stations"
— I have found it now in two languages, in two centuries, in the
files of two institutions that shared no members, no correspondence,
and no god — is applied, both times, to islands that had *offered
themselves*, and the grammar of both refusals is not doubt. It is
policy. Gentlemen, ladies: doubt erodes. Policy is renewed. Show me
a wall that is repainted, and I will show you a groundskeeper.

You will want the word for what I am proposing, so that you can
reject it precisely. Here it is. I propose that the blank spaces of
the historical map were never empty, because emptiness at that scale
is not a natural condition; it is expensive, and something paid for
it. I propose that a proportion of what this profession catalogues
as error is better described as *enclosure* — that certain places
were not failed discoveries but successful exclusions, and that the
instruments of exclusion were precisely the ones we mock: the
doubtful sounding, the disproven range, the island marked E.D., the
file sealed by a clerk whose name we do not keep. The map has two
functions and we teach only one. It shows the way. It also, and
first, keeps the ground. We are not the discoverers' heirs. We are
the groundskeepers', and we have lost the keys, and we call the
losing of them the growth of knowledge…

—

*[From the tape. Final four minutes of the question period.]*

CHAIR: — time for perhaps two more. Professor B—.

B—: A question of method, Frau Professor. When you say "policy" —
you are speaking, surely, metaphorically. Institutions repeat
formulae; clerks copy clerks. You would not have us believe you mean
it — administratively.

VOSS: I am speaking cartographically, Herr Professor, which is a
stricter language than either of ours. A metaphor does not recur at
fixed depths. A copied formula does not correct its own drift. I
mean that the pattern in the record is the pattern of upkeep, and I
decline to apologise for the record.

B—: Then I will say what several of us are thinking. Thirty years
ago a paper crossed my desk for review which mistook — I said so at
the time — the sociology of error for a property of the places
themselves. I see the mistake has matured.

VOSS: It has, Herr Professor. So has the evidence. I recognised
your phrase then, too.

[Laughter; some of it uneasy. Sound of the chair's water glass.]

CHAIR: A last question. Yes — at the back.

UNIDENTIFIED: A question of staffing, Professor. Your
groundskeepers. When a keeper retires — who advertises the post?

[Pause: four seconds on the tape.]

VOSS: The post is never advertised. The post is *offered*.

UNIDENTIFIED: And has it been?

[Pause: eleven seconds. No audience sound whatever.]

VOSS: This session was to end at five, I believe.

CHAIR: — yes. Yes. I believe we are — I believe we can bring
matters —

*[The tape ends here, forty seconds before the hour. The minute-book,
as noted, records the session ending early "by agreement." — E.M.]*

—

    IV [added to the typescript in the late hand, undated]: I have
    reread this paper every few years since Vienna, each time
    intending to feel embarrassment, and each time finding I said
    less than I knew. One correction, for the file. I told the room
    the post is offered. This is true but incomplete. The post is
    offered TWICE — the first time so that it can be declined,
    which teaches the candidate what declining feels like; there is
    no other way to teach it. The second offer does not feel like
    an offer. It feels like an errand.[^37]

[^37]: The voice of the last questioner does not appear elsewhere on
the tape, including the coffee interval the student recorded over in
part. German-accented English, age indeterminate — the student's
accompanying note offers "neither young nor old," a phrase the reader
has met, and which I assume, because the alternative is not usable,
is coincidence. Professor B— I have identified with certainty from
the society's attendance book; he died in 2003; his review files,
which his university holds, contain the 1971 report quoted at Kong,
signed with the same initial as the attendance book. That thread, at
least, closes cleanly: the hostile referee and the man in the second
row were the same person, and he was only ever what he appeared to
be — a colleague who disagreed. I set this down with something like
affection. In this archive, a man who is exactly what he appears to
be is a rest for the eyes.[^38]

[^38]: Voss's addendum — the post offered twice, the second time as
an errand — was written, by the ink, in the last two years of her
life. She boarded the boat at Dutch Harbor on 14 September 2019 to
verify, she told the skipper, a depth sounding: an errand. Part III
of this Atlas concerns the waters and the poles — the entries she
called, in her index, "the kept appointments." I have decided to
follow her order. I no longer believe it is an order of subject
matter. I believe it is a syllabus.
