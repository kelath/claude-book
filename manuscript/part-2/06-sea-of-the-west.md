# 18. The Sea of the West

**La Mer de l'Ouest: an inland sea the size of the Mediterranean,
occupying, on the French maps of the mid-eighteenth century, most of
what is now Washington, Oregon, Idaho, and British Columbia. Held
privately 1700–1750; published 1750–c. 1795. Died of soundings.**

Every phantom so far entered the record by accident, appetite, or
fraud. The Sea of the West is the only one that was *quarantined* — by
its own author, who understood exactly what he had made, kept it locked
away for half a century, and was betrayed in the end by the one
instrument no cartographer has ever mastered: his heirs.

The author was Guillaume Delisle, and the name is the point. Delisle
was the finest map-maker of the age of Louis XIV — the man who purged
the Island of California from French cartography, who shrank the
Mediterranean to its true length after fifteen centuries of inherited
exaggeration, whose maps were the first anywhere to deserve the word
*scientific*. Around 1700, compiling everything Europe knew and
suspected about the far west of North America — Indian reports of great
waters, the unverifiable strait of the Greek pilot Juan de Fuca, the
ordinary hunger of an empire for a route to the Pacific — Delisle
sketched a hypothesis: a vast sea, opening from the western coast,
reaching deep into the continent. He drew it several times. He
considered it seriously enough to write a memoir on it.

And he never published it. Not once, in a working life of forty years
at the summit of his profession, did the Sea of the West appear on a
map with Delisle's name on it. The sketches stayed in the workshop;
the memoir stayed in manuscript; the public Delisle showed the world
a west coast honestly vacant. He had invented the most seductive
geography of his century and he kept it in a drawer, because he could
not verify it, and he was the first man in this Atlas's five hundred
years who believed that was a reason.

    IV: Honour him. In the entire literature I know of no other case:
    a phantom conceived, fully drawn, *and successfully contained* —
    for fifty years the sea existed in one drawer in Paris and
    nowhere else on earth. Delisle had discovered, ahead of every
    academy, the only protocol that actually works: an error cannot
    be killed once published, therefore it must not be published.
    The drawer is the quarantine of the third place. Remember the
    drawer. This Atlas is one, and its author knew it, and the reader
    is advised to notice whose hands the drawer is in now.

He died in 1726. The drawer held for another generation, and then it
was opened by the two men with the best claim and the worst motives:
his half-brother Joseph-Nicolas, home from thirty years in Russia
with a reputation to rebuild, and his son-in-law Philippe Buache,
inheritor of the workshop and a devotee of what the period politely
called cabinet geography — the doctrine that a sufficiently elegant
theory of the earth need not wait upon its verification. In 1750,
before the Académie, they produced the family treasure: the Sea of
the West, drawn now not as a question but as a discovery, buttressed
by the testimony the reader has been waiting for — the voyage of
Admiral Bartholomew de Fonte, who in 1640 had allegedly sailed from
the Pacific into a web of straits and lakes across the continent's
top and met a Boston merchantman coming the other way.

The de Fonte narrative was fiction. Not error — fiction, composed for
a London magazine in 1708, in the golden age of the invented voyage,
by an author still disputed; no Spanish admiral of the name exists;
the account is a tissue of impossible watercourses. It had lain
harmless in the back-files for forty years, a story. Buache and
Delisle the younger promoted it to *evidence*, because their sea
needed a witness and the dead admiral was available. Fiction
corroborated hypothesis; hypothesis dignified fiction; and the
composite — argued in memoirs, engraved beautifully, stamped with
the most authoritative surname in French science — spread across
the ateliers of Paris and outward to the world's atlases. For forty
years the schoolchildren of Europe could study a sea the size of
the Mediterranean, furnished with capes and islands, in the space
where Idaho is.

    IV: The mechanism deserves its own line in the doctrine. Kong
    was secreted by a theory, California by a market. The Sea of
    the West was assembled — deliberately, in committee, from
    matched parts: one dead genius's suppressed hypothesis, one
    anonymous fiction, one unverifiable pilot. Nothing in it was
    new; everything in it was *inherited*; the assemblers
    contributed only the decision that the parts should be allowed
    to corroborate one another. Errors do not only grow, like Kong,
    or invade, like the island California. They can be *curated*.
    And curation, unlike mirage, leaves minutes of its meetings.

It died as the interlude says interiors die — of a walk, though this
walk was made by sea. Cook's third voyage in 1778 ran the actual
coast where the sea's mouth should have gaped, and found coastline;
Vancouver's survey in the 1790s closed every remaining gap with the
patient, freezing thoroughness that killed phantoms from Brasil to
Sandy Island: soundings, transects, boats sent up every opening
until it ended in river or rock. The Sea of the West went off the
maps within the decade, unmourned, its lobby dead, its function —
France's hope of a private route to the Pacific — extinguished at
Quebec in any case decades before.

But the entry does not end in defeat, because this phantom, alone in
the Atlas, left an *estate*. Among its foundation documents was the
deposition of Juan de Fuca, the Greek pilot sailing for Spain who
claimed in 1592 to have found, at 47 or 48 degrees north, a broad
strait leading inland toward a shining sea. His account has every
mark of the tavern: no Spanish record of the voyage exists; scholars
have doubted the man himself. He was, at best, unverifiable — the
Sea of the West's third matched part. In 1787 the fur trader Charles
Barkley, working the coast Cook had charted, found at 48°30′ north a
broad strait leading inland, within a degree of where the old Greek
had put it, and named it — the name it bears on every chart on earth
today — the Strait of Juan de Fuca.

    IV: The First Law, at the end of its tether. An error is a
    promise the map makes before the world is ready to keep it — and
    here the world kept it to the very door: the strait is there, at
    his latitude, of his breadth, leading inland to waters that
    shine. It does not open into a Mediterranean; it opens into
    Puget Sound, which is a Mediterranean sold by the yard. I have
    stood at Cape Flattery where the promise was redeemed and I
    will testify that the pilot's description is *better than
    accurate: it is recognisable*. The question this Atlas exists
    to keep open stands nowhere more nakedly than here. Either a
    liar in a Venetian tavern guessed a coastline to within one
    degree, or the coast was, in some sense I have spent my life
    trying to state respectably, *listening*.[^33]

One administrative note closes the file. The Delisle drawer —
the physical archive: the sketches, the suppressed memoir — passed
with the workshop's papers into the French naval hydrographic
collection, where it survives and may be consulted. Voss consulted
it in 1968; her transcriptions check.[^34] Clipped to her copy of
the suppressed memoir is her earliest dated note in the entire
four thousand cards — she was thirty-seven; the Atlas did not yet
have a name — and it reads, in the young, unhesitating brown ink:

    IV: G.D. kept it in the drawer his whole life and died at peace.
    The question is not how errors escape. The question is what the
    drawer was FOR. You do not build a drawer that strong for a
    thing you believe to be false.

[^33]: Puget Sound at its deepest, off Point Jefferson, sounds at
about 280 metres — I know the reader by now, and no, the figure does
not fit the band, and I report it anyway, per the discipline of the
Dougherty control. Not every door opens on the vault.

[^34]: Bibliothèque nationale and the naval archives both; the Sea
of the West drafts are among the most reproduced curiosities in the
history of cartography and their provenance is impeccable. It is
restful, once an entry, to report a drawer with nothing impossible
in it. I have come to prize the entries that stay shut.
